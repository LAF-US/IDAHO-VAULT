## Governor Little signs two bills cutting local zoning rules, weighing a third

Idaho Governor Brad Little gave his stamp of approval on overriding local zoning rules to build more housing.

In the session’s waning days, legislators narrowly approved [SB 1352](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/S1352/), [SB 1354](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/S1354/) and [HB 706](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0706/) [nixing local zoning rules to make way for smaller, denser housing in cities larger than 10,000 people.](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/19/senate-starter-pass/) This set of bills would block cities from restricting the construction of denser subdivisions with smaller homes of at least four acres in size and accessory dwelling units, known as mother-in-law cottages. A third bill would also give cities the option to change their building code to allow small apartment buildings with only one staircase instead of two to reduce construction costs.

Legislators from across the state pushed the legislation over the finish line, including Sen. Ben Toews, R-Coeur d’Alene, Rep. Jordan Redman, R-Coeur d’Alene, Rep. Erin Bingham, R-Idaho Falls, and Sen. Ali Rabe, D-Boise. They argued in committees and on the Senate and House of Representatives floor that forcing local governments to loosen their restrictions on housing could help unlock a diversity of affordable options and give a hand up to the next generation into home ownership.

![SERVPRO Boise - Jan25](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/5b2a85ae-933e-4c98-82a5-e207d65304e9.png)

SERVPRO Boise - Jan25

[After multiple amendment](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/02/toews-housing-compromise/) s, these three bills cleared both chambers with a few votes to spare. Little signed SB 1352 and 1354 on Tuesday and will consider HB 706 in the coming days. He has already signed HB 800, which allows manufactured homes on any single-family residential lot unless there are HOA restrictions in an area. Governor Brad Little told BoiseDev at the conclusion of the 2023 legislative session he opposed the idea of changes made to [local zoning laws by state legislators rather than local leaders](https://boisedev.com/news/2023/04/20/brad-little-post-session/), but he did not explain publicly in a transmittal letter why he changed his stance in the past three years.

Not all of the housing reform bills made it out of committee, however. The House Business Committee turned down [SB 1353](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/S1353/) forcing cities above 10,000 to allow duplexes or twin homes on any residential lot on a 7-7 vote. A bill from Redman to allow housing development on religious land by right also never made it out of the House Business Committee, which he chairs.

## Starter home subdivisions passes House on a last-minute, one-vote margin

The House of Representatives passed SB 1352 cutting rules on starter home subdivisions in one of the tightest votes all session.

![Visit Boise - 2:1 - Easier to brag 1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/7c2cf378-0990-43b2-949c-d6e349b008ba.png)

Visit Boise - 2:1 - Easier to brag 1

Last Thursday, legislators debated at length the provision that would ban cities over 10,000 from requiring minimum lot sizes larger than 1,500 in starter home subdivisions of at least four acres in size. Supporters of the idea said forcing local governments to allow these type of projects would help turn back decades of housing policy that shut out lower and middle-income buyers.

“I know that some of your cities have contacte dyou and I know that they are urging your red light on this, but I will suggest to you, they have had 130 years to demonstrate that their city is a place where anybody can start a home and if they have not done it yet they’re not going to,” Rep. Megan Egbert, D-Boise, said.

Others opposed the idea, saying passing this bill would put the Idaho State Legislature in the position of a local planning and zoning commission that was too far of an overreach into the authority of local government. Rep. Bruce Skaug, R-Nampa, said communities that require homes to be on large pieces of land become higher valued, instead of this bill, which would require cities to approve smaller homes on smaller lots that would have lower purchase prices.

![Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/6f251bf1-00e6-48d7-8365-570820fe299b.gif)

Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow

“My grandfather was the first mayor of Eagle, a very poor community at that time, but part of what made it prosperous was limiting the size of the plots to make them large,” Skaug said. “They weren’t going to be allowed to have small lot sizes for homes. That’s a big reason Eagle has prospered so much and I see this as central government planning in the extreme.”

The vote was extremely close, with the vote total flopping back and forth between a tie and a majority in the final moments before Speaker of the House Rep. Mike Moyle, R-Moyle, locked the machine to confirm the votes. The bill was a tie until Rep. Dori Healey, R-Boise, flipped her vote at the last minute to support it.

## Senate backs opening options for single-stairway buildings by one vote

The Senate was divided on a pitch from Rabe on allowing cities the option to allow small buildings up to six stories to be built with only one staircase, instead of two.

In order to take advantage of this, buildings would be required to install additional fire detection and sprinkler systems, fire buffers between units and floors and have other safety mechanisms. Rabe said the improvement in fire suppression technology makes rules requiring two stairways an unnecessary barrier to housing construction.

“To build a 4-6 story apartment building, even if it’s a small footprint, a second stairway adds $300,000 to the pro forma and cost,” she said to open debate. “That’s often the difference between getting a project built and not getting the project built at all, which has exacerbated our housing supply problem.”

![Western States - 2026 Q1 - 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/37908e89-0c24-43bf-b53d-711663e791e2.jpg)

Western States - 2026 Q1 - 2:1

This bill soared off the House of Representatives floor 63 votes in favor, but after heavy lobbying from the Idaho Fire Chiefs and the Professional Fire Fighters of Idaho, Senators were much more conflicted on the idea. Sen. Ben Adams, R-Nampa, said all this bill would do is create savings for a builder with no guarantee of affordable housing units in exchange for more fire danger.

“It’s not just a research paper to them, it’s real life,” Adams said, citing an argument Rabe made about studies showing these buildings were safe after similar legislation was passed across the country. “We can choose to listen to a study from back east or should we listen to the cities right here? Should we listen to the firefighters from right here in Idaho who are unequivocal to their opposition to this legislation?”

Sen. Phil Hart, R-Kellogg, noted his history as a structural engineer when arguing for the bill. He said decades of policy writing has made building codes across the country incredibly complex with no analysis about how it impacts the cost of housing.

![Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/6f251bf1-00e6-48d7-8365-570820fe299b.gif)

Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow

“The building code has become as complicated as the Internal Revenue Code and one of my observations after seeing how some of those changes come about, is that there’s no attention paid to the economic impact of the changes,” he said. “The guys don’t even think about those things as to the economic impact of their changes.”

It passed 18-17.

That is a sharp, strategic way to repurpose the Rumsfeld Matrix for project reporting. Using it this way transforms a standard status update into a risk-assessment tool.

Here is your Levelset Report template, structured to bridge the gap between what we have and what we need from Logan.

Levelset Report: [Project Name/Date]

A) Unknown Knowns: The Foundation

These are the established facts and administrative "givens" that often sit in the background.

Frontmatter: [Report Version, Author, and Distribution List]

Metadata: [Project ID, Billing Codes, Phase Stage, and Last Modified Date]

Stakeholders: [List of active participants and their roles]

B) Known Knowns: The Report Meat

The verifiable data and completed actions. This is the "what we know we know."

Milestones Achieved: [List completed tasks since the last levelset]

Current Metrics: [Hard numbers: Budget spent, hours logged, or KPI progress]

Deliverables: [Links to completed drafts, code repositories, or signed assets]

Resource Allocation: [Current team bandwidth and tool availability]

C) Known Unknowns: The Gaps

Identified risks, missing information, and flagged hurdles. The "what we know we don't know."

Context Gaps: [Missing historical data or specific project requirements yet to be defined]

Active Flags: [Current bottlenecks or dependencies waiting on external vendors]

Technical Risks: [Uncertainty regarding specific integrations or platform limitations]

Timeline Variance: [Estimates that lack high confidence levels]

D) Unknown Unknowns: The Logan Variable

The blind spots. We need Logan’s "bird’s-eye view" to identify what we haven't even thought of yet.

Strategic Alignment: Does Logan see a shift in company direction that renders this project obsolete?

External Disruptors: Are there upcoming market shifts or competitor moves Logan is privy to?

Hidden Dependencies: What cross-departmental conflicts are we currently blind to?

The "Logan Ask": Logan, based on this report, what is the one question we aren't asking that we should be?

Depictions of a fourth-dimensional hypercube

Gemini said

A fourth-dimensional hypercube, also known as a tesseract, is the 4D analog of a 3D cube. Since the human eye cannot directly perceive four spatial dimensions, we rely on various 3D and 2D projections to visualize its structure.

One of the most common depictions is the perspective projection (or "inner-outer" cube), which looks like a small cube suspended inside a larger one, with their corners connected. Another method is the orthogonal projection, which creates a complex web of vertices and edges that often resembles a 3D shadow. In motion, these depictions often show the tesseract "turning inside out," a process known as a double rotation or a 4D rotation through a 3D space.

How many dimensions are there? Explain them, one by one if you are able, without getting sidetracked.

Gemini said

In physics and mathematics, dimensions are the independent coordinates required to specify the location of a point within a space. While string theory suggests there could be 10, 11, or even 26 dimensions, we can reliably explain the ones we experience and those that are mathematically consistent.

The Spatial Dimensions

These are the directions in which we can move and measure objects.

Zero Dimension (0D): A single point. It has no height, width, or depth. It exists only as a position.

First Dimension (1D): A line. By connecting two points, we create length. You can move forward or backward, but nothing else.

Second Dimension (2D): A plane. By adding a second axis (perpendicular to the first), we get width. This creates shapes like squares and circles.

Third Dimension (3D): Volume. By adding a third axis, we get depth. This is the world we physically inhabit, where objects have "bulk" and can be viewed from different angles.

The Fourth Dimension

In the context of General Relativity, the fourth dimension is Time.

Space-Time: Einstein demonstrated that space and time are inextricably linked. To meet someone, you need three spatial coordinates (longitude, latitude, and altitude) and one temporal coordinate (the time of the meeting).

Euclidean 4D: In pure mathematics, the fourth dimension is a fourth spatial axis (w) perpendicular to x, y, and z. This allows for the existence of the tesseract, where a cube can "fold" in a direction we cannot see.

Theoretical Higher Dimensions

Beyond the fourth, dimensions become increasingly abstract and are primarily used in String Theory to explain the fundamental forces of the universe.

Dimension Role in Theoretical Physics

5th & 6th Often described as a space where versions of our universe with different initial conditions could exist.

7th - 10th These are thought to be "compactified" or curled up so tightly at the subatomic level that we cannot perceive them. They account for the vibrations of strings that create particles.

11th Central to M-Theory, which unites different versions of string theory into a single framework.

Why can't we see them?

If these extra dimensions exist, they are likely curled up (compactified) into tiny geometric shapes like Calabi-Yau manifolds. Imagine looking at a garden hose from a mile away; it looks like a 1D line. Only when you get close do you see it has a 2D circular cross-section that an ant can crawl around.

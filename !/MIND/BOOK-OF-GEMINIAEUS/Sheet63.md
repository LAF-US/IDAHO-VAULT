Research into the distinction of "electronic television" reveals the following key differences from the mechanical systems that preceded it:

Removal of Moving Parts: The primary distinction of electronic television is the total elimination of mechanical components such as rotating "Nipkow" discs or vibrating mirrors. Philo Farnsworth’s core contribution was taking these moving parts out and making the entire system electronic.

Scanning Speed and Quality: Mechanical systems were too slow to scan and reassemble images many times a second, resulting in small, shaky pictures with very low resolution (often only 30 to 60 lines). Electronic systems use electron beams controlled by magnets to "paint" images on a screen, which is fast enough to produce the high-quality, stable pictures required for modern standards.

Image Capture vs. Scanning: While mechanical televisions relied on physical holes in a spinning disc to sweep across a scene, electronic television utilizes a "camera tube" (which Farnsworth called an Image Dissector) to break an image down into horizontal lines of electronic impulses for transmission.

The "Plowing" Concept: Farnsworth famously conceived the idea for electronic scanning while plowing a potato field as a teenager, realizing that an image could be scanned and reassembled as a series of horizontal lines, much like the rows he was creating in the soil.

Reliability and Range: Mechanical sets were expensive, unreliable, and frequently broke down due to the complex spinning parts. Electronic systems were significantly more durable and could transmit signals over much greater distances using existing radio infrastructure.

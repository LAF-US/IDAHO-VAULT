What is a "gopher" in the theater setting, like Scooter from the Muppets?

In the world of theater and film, a "gopher" (a play on the phrase "go for") is an entry-level assistant whose primary job is to run errands and handle miscellaneous tasks for the cast and crew.

While the term can sometimes be used dismissively, gophers are the grease that keeps a production's gears turning. Scooter from The Muppets is the quintessential example: he’s the "go-between" who manages the chaos so the show can actually happen.

Core Responsibilities

A gopher’s "job description" is essentially whatever is needed at that exact moment. Typical tasks include:

Pick-ups: Getting coffee, meals, or urgent supplies for the director or lead actors.

Script Runs: Delivering updated script pages to various departments.

Crowd Control: Standing at a door or hallway to ensure no one walks into a shot or onto the stage during a rehearsal.

Basic Labor: Moving light equipment, cleaning up a workspace, or helping the stage manager with small clerical tasks.

The "Scooter" Dynamic

Scooter represents a specific type of gopher: the Production Assistant (PA) or Stage Manager’s Assistant.

Because he is the nephew of the theater's owner (J.P. Grosse), he has a bit more "pull" than a standard gopher, but his function remains the same. He is the one knocking on dressing room doors to give the "five minutes to curtain" warning, managing guest stars, and acting as the middleman between the creative ego of Kermit and the technical needs of the crew.

Is it a "Real" Job Title?

In professional settings today, you won’t usually see "Gopher" on a paycheck. Instead, the roles are titled:

1. Production Assistant (PA): The standard term in film and television.

2. Runner: The common term in the UK and in many high-end recording studios or theater houses.

3. Intern/Apprentice: Often used in summer stock or regional theater.

Note: Being a gopher is widely considered a rite of passage. Many successful directors and producers started their careers "going for" coffee and scripts.

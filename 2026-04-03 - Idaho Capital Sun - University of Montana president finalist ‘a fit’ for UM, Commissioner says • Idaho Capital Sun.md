## University of Montana president finalist ‘a fit’ for UM, Commissioner says

## Jeremiah Shinn will be on campus in Missoula on Monday and Tuesday, April 6 and 7.

![The M on Mt. Sentinel stands above Main Hall on the University of Montana campus. (Provided by the University of Montana)](https://idahocapitalsun.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/Main_Hall_Kei-1024x678-1.jpg)

Jeremiah Shinn, finalist for the University of Montana president job, emerged from a pool of more than 60 candidates for his commitment to students, for starters.

Search advisory group members said Shinn, interim president at Boise State University, also was tapped to visit the Missoula campus for a final vetting because of his connection to the West and the way he wants to lead the flagship.

Friday, UM and the Montana Office of the Commissioner of Higher Education announced Shinn will visit the campus Monday and Tuesday, April 6 and 7, for a series of meetings including a public forum.

In January, former UM President Seth Bodnar announced his resignation, and Commissioner of Higher Education Clayton Christian said he would conduct an expedited search to retain momentum at UM.

More than 60 candidates applied for the job in a search Christian described Friday as “robust,” and he said the advisory group members found Shinn to be “a fit” with UM.

He said they like his focus on students, his experience at Boise State, and his interest in helping UM move forward and build on its successes — versus wanting to reshape it.

Jeremiah Shinn, finalist for University of Montana president. (Provided photo by Priscilla Grover and the University of Montana)

“He genuinely seems interested in the University of Montana,” Christian said. “He’s been here. He’s worked with people here. He likes the environment. He likes the campus.”

Shinn was named interim president at Boise State in April 2025 and before that served more than 12 years there in four different jobs, according to his resume and a news release from the Idaho State Board of Education.

He was named interim president at Boise State after its president left for a job at the University of Vermont and was not seeking that job permanently, according to Idaho Education News.

Shinn had served as Boise State’s vice president for student affairs and enrollment management since 2023, and he was vice president for student affairs at Louisiana State University from 2019 to 2023, according to his resume.

From 2010 to 2019, he worked at Boise State, first as head of its student involvement and leadership center, then as the assistant vice president for student life, and later as associate vice president for student affairs.

Buddy Wilson, president of the Associated Students of the University of Montana and member of the advisory group, said he believes Shinn is the right candidate for UM because of his experience in the region and his student focus.

“He’s coming from Boise State, which is sort of a peer institution in the Rocky Mountain West, so he knows the region,” Wilson said.

“He also comes from a student affairs background, and he’s done work that has directly made the student experience better over at Boise State, and I think he would do the same thing here.”

For example, Wilson said he believes Shinn helped improve the financial aid office and other processes at Boise State in ways that benefited students.

John DeBoer, vice provost for academic affairs at UM and an advisory group member, said Shinn stood out as the group thought about the different constituents a university leader has, such as potential students, legislators, the Board of Regents and donors.

“Really, his manner of communicating the worth of higher education really stood out,” DeBoer said. “It was exciting to the committee, and it was one of the reasons why we felt we had to put him in front of the Board and why we wanted to introduce him to campus.”

In his LinkedIn profile, Shinn describes himself as a first-generation college student whose experience altered his trajectory and shaped his worldview, and he said higher education continues to be his “life’s work.”

“I believe deeply in the transformational potential of higher education to change lives and improve communities,” Shinn said in his profile.

According to reporting from Idaho EdNews, Shinn has been leading Boise State in a time of major leadership transition in its top ranks and statewide budget reductions.

Christian said Shinn has done a good job working through the challenges at Boise State, and he said Shinn told him he learned as interim president that he was interested in serving as a permanent president.

“He’s worked in all sectors of the enterprise but also had a very student-centered focus, and we like that,” Christian said.

Shinn had support from the full advisory group, Christian said. They’re eager to introduce him to the campus and community, but if for some reason the process doesn’t end in a hire, he said the other candidates are in the wings.

If Christian recommends the Board of Regents approve Shinn as president, he said he believes the Board could convene to make a decision within a week or so, although it isn’t clear how soon Shinn could start.

(Because of the plan to expedite a search, Christian did not name an interim president at UM.)

Leaders from the UM Faculty Senate and Staff Senate could not be reached for comment Friday.

Should Shinn take the helm at UM, Christian said his priorities would be to grow enrollment and keep UM moving forward. Enrollment at UM has stabilized in recent years after a period of decline.

“Certainly, enrollment growth continues to be a priority, but maybe more importantly, on the student side … we want to see students come to us. We meet them where they’re at, we retain them at high levels, and we graduate them on time.”

#### UM finalist candidate visit

On campus next week, Jeremiah Shinn will meet with UM’s leadership team, faculty, staff and the Associated Students of UM, as well as other campus groups and organizations.

The public is invited on Monday, April 6, to the third floor of the University Center at 3:30 p.m. for a public forum and a community reception at 5 p.m.

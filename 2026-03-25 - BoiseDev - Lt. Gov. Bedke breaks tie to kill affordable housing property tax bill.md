## Bill to provide affordable housing tax credit dies at the finish line… twice

Photo: Margaret Carmel/BoiseDev

![Idaho Capitol illustration](https://boisedev.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/statehouse-circle.png)

Idaho Capitol illustration

A new option for local government to attract affordable housing development failed right before it could cross the finish line, not once, but twice.

On Tuesday evening, the Idaho Senate tied 17-17 on [HB 760](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0760/) from Rep. Jon Weber, R-Rexburg, and Sen. Treg Bernt, R-Meridian, that would have paved the way for more counties to give nonprofit organizations, or companies affiliated with nonprofits, a property tax break on affordable housing projects if they chose. Weber and Bernt argued that easing [the state’s current requirements on this tax exemption](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/02/25/affordable-county-exemption/) would help more counties choose to take advantage of it and attract more affordable housing to ease the state’s booming housing costs.

The bill passed the House of Representatives on a 39 to 28 vote, but proved more unpopular in the Senate on Monday. In accordance with the rules for a tie vote, Lt. Governor Scott Bedke voted to break the tie and opted to send the idea to the scrap heap for the year. After the vote, he declined to comment to BoiseDev on why he opposed the measure.

During Tuesday morning’s floor session, Majority Caucus Chair Sen. Mark Harris, R-Soda Springs, made a motion to reconsider the bill for another vote. The Senate voted 24-11 to take another vote on the measure, but then three legislators changed their votes from the previous day to kill the bill.

This included a last-minute vote change from Harris, who asked for it to be brought back for reconsideration in the first place.

During both debates on Monday and again on Tuesday, Bernt urged the Senate to back the idea and give local governments the ability to provide housing for all types of people if they choose, without spending state dollars. He pushed back on arguments that this was forcing rent control or rent freezes, but instead would use affordable housing programs to partner with the private sector, first created under Republican President Ronald Reagan’s administration, to create housing for low and middle-income workers.

![SERVPRO Boise - Jan25](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/5b2a85ae-933e-4c98-82a5-e207d65304e9.png)

SERVPRO Boise - Jan25

“This allows for the free market to avoid the failures of public housing,” he said Tuesday. “This isn’t concentrated poverty. This is a conservative fix to meeting a need that the government has failed to do.”

Others openly debated against the idea, including Sen. Brian Lenney, R-Nampa, and Sen. Josh Kohl, R-Twin Falls, who argued that property tax exemptions for some properties would only move that tax burden onto other families who aren’t living in subsidized housing but are still struggling to get by.

“Ronald Reagan also let several million people in here when he did amnesty here, so we could pick and choose the Reagan stuff at our own leisure,” Lenney said, referencing a Reagan-era policy that legalized 3 million undocumented immigrants in the early 1980s. “We hear about this as workforce housing, but we also hear that workforce term thrown around a lot when we’re talking about illegal aliens. What this is is rent-controlled, government-subsidized housing.”

Another sticking point was how the bill would strike a requirement that no profits or tax benefits from the nonprofit organization owning the affordable housing property could go to an individual or a for-profit company. It would also widen the types of companies that can own these properties, as long as there is a link to a qualified nonprofit.

“This comes down to this philosophy we keep seeing in American policy: socialism for me, austerity for thee,” Kohl said on the floor Monday in his first debate against the bill.

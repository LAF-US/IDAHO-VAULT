On Sept. 20, 1974, Kootenai Tribal Council chair Amy Trice and tribal
representative Doug Wheaton formally notified the U.S. government that a
state of war existed between the Tribe and the United States.

The immediate issue was the Bureau of Indian Affairs' disregard of repeated
tribal requests for housing repairs, medical care, education, and respect for
tribal lands. The larger problem stretched much farther back. In 1855 the
Tribe refused to sign a treaty that would have required it to cede tribal
lands to the United States. Because the Kootenai lacked a legally recognized
reservation, the BIA did not provide the same support it gave tribes on
recognized reservations. A tribe that had once numbered about 4,000 members
had dwindled to 67 by the time the war was declared.

Boundary County sheriff Chris Ketner later recalled being described in the
media as level-headed and fair during the 29-day standoff. He also recalled
that federal officials wanted to resolve the issue quickly and without
violence in the aftermath of Wounded Knee.

Supporters arrived from Oregon, South Dakota, and Montana. Rumors circulated
that militants had brought automatic weapons, rocket launchers, and bombs, but
those rumors proved false. Idaho State Police were sent to support the
sheriff's office, which at the time consisted of three deputies and one tribal
member serving as dispatcher.

Ketner also recalled that U.S. 95 was not blockaded as sometimes reported.
Instead, tribal members asked for a 10-cent voluntary donation from motorists
who stopped to listen to the drums.

The government eventually agreed to some of the Tribe's demands, and the war
ended. In the decades since, the Kootenai Tribe of Idaho has rebuilt both
land base and institutions. In Bonners Ferry the Tribe now owns the Kootenai
River Inn, Casino and Spa, the Springs Restaurant, the Casino Day Spa and
Gift Shop, a sturgeon fish hatchery, a sand and gravel business, and a timber
business.

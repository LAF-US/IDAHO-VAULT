The **upside-down** (also **inverted**, **turned** or **rotated**) **question mark** ¿ and **exclamation mark** ¡ are [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation "Punctuation") marks used to begin [interrogative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrogative "Interrogative") and exclamatory sentences or [clauses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clause "Clause") in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language") and some languages that have cultural ties with Spain, such as [Asturian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asturian_language "Asturian language") and [Waray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waray_language "Waray language").[^1] The initial marks are mirrored at the end of the sentence or clause by the ordinary [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark "Question mark")? or [exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_mark "Exclamation mark")!.

Upside-down marks are supported by various standards, including [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") and [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SGML_entity "SGML entity"). They can be entered directly on keyboards designed for Spanish-speaking countries.

## Usage

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Signosdepuntuaci%C3%B3n_html_y_binario.pdf/page1-250px-Signosdepuntuaci%C3%B3n_html_y_binario.pdf.jpg)

Punctuation marks in Spanish, showing their positions relative to the baseline

The upside-down question mark ¿ is written before the first letter of an [interrogative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrogative "Interrogative") sentence or [clause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clause "Clause") to indicate that a question follows. It is a rotated form of the standard symbol? recognized by speakers of other languages written with the [Latin script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_script "Latin script"). A regular [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark "Question mark") is written at the end of the sentence or clause.

Upside-down punctuation is important in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language") since the [syntax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax "Syntax") of the language means that both statements and questions or [exclamations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_\(linguistics\)#By_function_or_speech_act "Sentence (linguistics)") could have the same wording.[^2] "Do you like summer?" and "You like summer." are translated respectively as *"¿Te gusta el verano?"* and *"Te gusta el verano."* (There is not always a difference between the wording of a [yes–no question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yes%E2%80%93no_question "Yes–no question") and the corresponding statement in Spanish.)

In sentences that are both [declarative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sentence_\(linguistics\)#By_function_or_speech_act "Sentence (linguistics)") and interrogative, the clause that asks a question is isolated with the starting-symbol upside-down question mark, for example: *"Si no puedes ir con ellos, ¿quieres ir con nosotros?"* ("If you cannot go with them, would you like to go with us?"), not \* *"¿Si no puedes ir con ellos, quieres ir con nosotros?"* This helps to recognize questions and exclamations in long sentences.

Unlike the ending marks, which are printed along the [baseline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_\(typography\) "Baseline (typography)") of the text, the upside-down marks (¿ and ¡) normally [descend below the line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descender "Descender"), though they are printed along the baseline in all-cap text.

The upside-down exclamation mark is a phonetic symbol in the [Extensions to the International Phonetic Alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensions_to_the_International_Phonetic_Alphabet "Extensions to the International Phonetic Alphabet").

## History

Upside-down marks, simple in the era of hand typesetting, were originally recommended by the *[Real Academia Española](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Spanish_Academy "Royal Spanish Academy")* (Royal Spanish Academy), in the second edition of the *Ortografía de la lengua castellana* (Orthography of the Castilian language) in 1754 [^3] recommending it as the symbol indicating the beginning of a question in written Spanish—e.g. *"¿Cuántos años tienes?"* ("How old are you?"; lit. 'How many years do you have?'). The Real Academia also ordered the same upside-down-symbol system for statements of exclamation, using the symbols "¡" and "!".

These new rules were slow to be adopted: there are 19th-century books in which the printer uses neither "¡" nor "¿".[^4]

Outside of the Spanish-speaking world, [John Wilkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkins "John Wilkins") proposed using the upside-down exclamation mark "¡" as a symbol at the end of a sentence to [denote irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation "Irony punctuation") in 1668. He was one of many, including [Desiderius Erasmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus "Desiderius Erasmus"), who felt there was a need for such a punctuation mark, but Wilkins' proposal, like the other attempts, failed to take hold.[^5] [^6]

## Adoption

Some writers omit the upside-down question mark in the case of a short unambiguous question such as: *"Quién viene?"* ("Who comes?"). This is the criterion in [Galician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galician_language "Galician language") [^7] [^8] and formerly in [Catalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_language "Catalan language").[^9] Certain Catalan-language authorities, such as [Joan Solà i Cortassa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_Sol%C3%A0_i_Cortassa "Joan Solà i Cortassa"), insist that both the opening and closing question marks be used for clarity.[^5] The current [Institute for Catalan Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Catalan_Studies "Institute for Catalan Studies") prescription is never to use the upside-down marks for Catalan.[^10] However, [Acadèmia Valenciana de la Llengua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acad%C3%A8mia_Valenciana_de_la_Llengua "Acadèmia Valenciana de la Llengua") allows optional use of the upside-down marks.[^11]

Some Spanish-language writers, among them Nobel laureate [Pablo Neruda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Neruda "Pablo Neruda") (1904–1973), refuse to use the upside-down question mark.[^12]

Upside-down marks are often omitted in informal writing, such as in [texting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texting "Texting").[^13]

## Mixtures

It is acceptable in Spanish to begin a sentence with an opening upside-down [exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_mark "Exclamation mark") ("¡") and end it with a question mark ("?"), or vice versa, for statements that are questions but also have a clear sense of exclamation or surprise such as: *¡Y tú quién te crees?* ("And who do you think you are?!"). Normally, four signs are used, always with one type in the outer side and the other in the inner side (nested) (**¿¡Y tú quién te crees!?**, **¡¿Y tú quién te crees?!**) [^14]

[Unicode 5.1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode#Versions "Unicode") also includes U+2E18 ⸘ INVERTED INTERROBANG, which is an [upside-down version of the interrobang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrobang#Inverted_interrobang "Interrobang"), a nonstandard punctuation mark used to denote both excitement and a question in one glyph. It is also known as a "gnaborretni" () ([interrobang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrobang "Interrobang") spelled backwards).

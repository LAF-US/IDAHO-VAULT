### With more than 96,000 signatures collected and volunteers canvassing statewide, organizers say Idaho voters are responding to personal stories and the real-world impact of the state’s abortion ban.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!kL_a!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F9cd39663-e7b5-4411-bcfb-afa7f3dffe4e_2856x2142.jpeg)

With just weeks remaining before a critical deadline, a grassroots campaign to restore abortion access in Idaho is entering its final and most demanding stretch.

Melanie Folwell, executive director of Idahoans United for Women & Families, described an operation racing to meet one of the most stringent ballot qualification systems in the country. Organizers must submit signatures by the end of April, after which county officials will have weeks to verify whether the initiative qualifies for the November ballot.

“What I hear from our volunteers again and again is that the conversations they’re having on doors are astonishing, heartbreaking,” Folwell said. “The number of people that we encounter who have been personally impacted by Idaho’s ban is pretty heartbreaking, but I think for everyone, we kind of believed.”

“It’s been affirmed that this is really this is not a quote device or social issue. This is not a quote-unquote women’s issue. This is a kitchen table issue where the rubber meets the road.”

Idaho law requires more than 70,000 valid signatures, along with a geographic distribution requirement that forces campaigns to meet thresholds across at least 18 legislative districts.

“So in a state like Idaho, the geography is one of the biggest challenges,” Folwell said.

By early April, the campaign had collected approximately 96,000 signatures—well above the minimum threshold needed to account for invalid or duplicate entries. Internal tracking suggests a validity rate hovering around 80 percent, giving organizers confidence that they are on pace not only to meet the statewide requirement but also to satisfy district-level thresholds. At the time of the interview, they had already qualified in 14 legislative districts and were targeting additional districts in the final weeks.

That confidence is rooted in an unusually large volunteer operation. More than 1,000 Idahoans have participated in signature gathering, forming the backbone of a campaign that has relied heavily on grassroots energy rather than paid canvassing. Volunteers have knocked on tens of thousands of doors and conducted conversations across communities that range from urban centers to deeply conservative rural areas.

Those conversations, Folwell said, have reshaped how organizers understand the issue’s political landscape. Rather than encountering uniform ideological resistance, canvassers have repeatedly heard personal stories from residents affected by the state’s abortion ban. Many of those conversations involve people who have either experienced complications themselves or know someone who has, underscoring the real-world consequences of the policy.

The campaign has found that these experiences often resonate more strongly than abstract political arguments. Organizers describe the issue not as a cultural or partisan debate, but as a deeply personal matter tied to family decisions, healthcare access, and individual autonomy. That framing, they say, has connected with voters across political affiliations.

Folwell pointed to what she characterized as a disconnect between official statements minimizing the impact of the abortion ban and the experiences reported by Idaho residents. The campaign has highlighted stories of patients facing medical emergencies, disruptions in care, and the broader strain on the state’s healthcare system, including the departure of a significant number of obstetric providers.

These accounts have become central to the campaign’s outreach, with some individuals stepping forward publicly to share their experiences and challenge the narrative that the ban has not had meaningful consequences.

The proposed ballot initiative would reverse Idaho’s current abortion restrictions, which took effect after the fall of Roe v. Wade. It would restore a legal framework similar to what existed prior to 2022, allowing abortion before fetal viability and in limited circumstances afterward, including medical emergencies and fatal fetal diagnoses. The measure would also include protections for contraception, emergency contraception, and fertility treatments such as in vitro fertilization.

Support for the initiative, according to organizers, has emerged across a wide range of communities. The campaign reports success not only in Boise and other population centers, but also in rural and strongly Republican areas, suggesting that the issue is cutting across traditional political divides.

That support, Folwell argued, reflects a broader emphasis on personal decision-making rather than ideology. Many voters, regardless of their personal views on abortion, appear receptive to the idea that such decisions should be made by individuals and families rather than by the government.

At the same time, the campaign has encountered organized opposition. Groups aligned with Idaho’s existing abortion restrictions have launched efforts to challenge the initiative, including messaging campaigns that organizers say misrepresent the proposal and its funding. Some national anti-abortion organizations have also become involved, alongside religious institutions seeking to influence the outcome.

Despite that opposition, the campaign has emphasized its local roots. According to Folwell, the vast majority of funding has come from within Idaho, with contributions from residents rather than large out-of-state organizations. The campaign has also relied primarily on in-state volunteers, reinforcing its effort to present the initiative as a locally driven response to state policy.

Legal challenges remain a possibility, though organizers say they have taken extensive steps to comply with Idaho’s requirements and are prepared to defend the initiative if necessary.

For now, the focus is on completing the signature drive. In the campaign’s final weeks, organizers are concentrating efforts in the Treasure Valley, where population density offers the most efficient path to meeting remaining targets.

The final push is being carried out by a volunteer network that has operated for months in difficult conditions, from extreme weather to long hours of canvassing. Organizers describe the effort as both physically demanding and deeply motivating, driven by a sense of urgency and shared purpose.

If the campaign succeeds, Idaho voters will decide in November whether to restore abortion access in the state—transforming a series of personal stories and grassroots organizing efforts into a statewide referendum on one of the country’s most contentious issues.

“They’re eager to sign, and no matter their own personal feelings about abortion, they tend to agree with this idea of no matter how I feel about it, or no matter my personal beliefs, I don’t think we probably should have imposed those on others, and certainly the government shouldn’t,” Folwell said.

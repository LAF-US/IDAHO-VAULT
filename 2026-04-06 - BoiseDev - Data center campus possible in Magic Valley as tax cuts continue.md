## Legislature leaves big tax breaks for data centers on the books. A Cali. company eyeing large data center could use them

A bid to roll back tax cuts for data centers and curb their use of water and power failed to get over the finish line for the second year in a row.

Throughout the 2026 legislative session, legislators from both sides of the rotunda and across the aisle worked through three different versions of a bill on data centers. It would have [sunsetted the sales tax exemption on data centers](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0897/) to 20 years, restricted what items tech companies can get sales tax exemptions on to only internal server equipment, and created new criteria for data centers on electricity and power usage.

Rep. Chris Bruce, R-Kuna, and Senate Majority Leader Lori Den Hartog, R-Meridian, wrote the bills so they would take effect as soon as possible, [leaving only the Meta data center in](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/03/datacenter-sales-revamp/) [Kuna eligible for the state’s generous tax relief package.](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/03/datacenter-sales-revamp/)

![SERVPRO Boise - Jan25](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/5b2a85ae-933e-4c98-82a5-e207d65304e9.png)

SERVPRO Boise - Jan25

But, after the Idaho State Tax Commission flagged some of the language for changes and Micron objected to the bill’s attempts to reform a long-standing property tax cut, the bill went for amendments in the final week of the session. The House of Representatives never took up the changes, and now data centers will be able to continue using Idaho’s big tax breaks for at least another year.

This means the [620-acre Diode Ventures data center approved in Kuna](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/04/03/kuna-data-center-diode-ventures/) could qualify for significant tax breaks.

Officials say a California-based company that develops “master planned data center campuses” is in discussions to develop a project in Raft River, between Twin Falls and Pocatello, which could also land hefty tax cuts. There is currently no application on file for the project, and there is no firm timeline for when construction would begin.

![Wright Brothers - Build Your Story - 2:1 ROS](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/d10654f4-3ce9-483a-836e-7bce7451e69a.png)

Wright Brothers - Build Your Story - 2:1 ROS

“The original tax incentive we have on the books is very broad, very expansive, and this piece of legislation is here to narrow it down,” Lori Den Hartog said on the Senate floor.

The legislation was the latest in the debate over how much the State of Idaho should spend to attract data centers, if anything, and how the technology impacts the environment around them. Across the country, [data centers have proved to be a political flashpoint](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2026/01/06/data-centers-backlash-impact-local-communities-opposition/) for heavy water use to cool servers, the relatively few permanent jobs they bring to the communities hosting them, and demand on the power grid.

Legislators in the House of Representatives pushed for [cuts to the sales tax exemption](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/03/04/hb-315-mar-2025/#:~:text=HB%20315%20adds%20language%20to,Read%20this%20next:) and requirements that data centers supply their own power in 2025, [but neither made it to a hearing in the Senate before adjournment.](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/03/14/data-power-costs/)

![tok commercial 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/1ff51757-dd76-432e-9972-14e69faf18c1.gif)

tok commercial 2:1

Legislators passed two other bills regulating data centers, even if they left the tax exemption question untouched. The first, [HB 895](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0895/) from Speaker of the House Mike Moyle, R-Star, requires data centers to recycle water to cool their servers, instead of water that’s pumped out of the ground or from a river and discarded. The second, [HB 911](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/02/24/data-electricity-testing/), from Rep. Stephanie Mickelsen, R-Idaho Falls, requires data centers to negotiate power agreements that cover the full cost of their energy use instead of forcing increases on consumers.

## What do we know about the Magic Valley project?

It’s unknown exactly when or where a large data center campus could come to the Magic Valley, but two legislators and a powerful lobbying group told BoiseDev that discussions are ongoing and a land deal might have already closed.

Alex LeBeau, Executive Director for the Idaho Association of Commerce and Industry, told BoiseDev that one possible amendment to Bruce and Den Hartog’s data center sales tax exemption bill was to move its effective date to accommodate plans for a data center in Raft River. He, Den Hartog, and Senate President Pro Tem Kelly Anthon, R-Rupert, all told BoiseDev the company considering the project was Terra Volt Infrastructure Inc, based in Tustin, California.

![Wright Brothers - Build Your Story - 2:1 ROS](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/d10654f4-3ce9-483a-836e-7bce7451e69a.png)

Wright Brothers - Build Your Story - 2:1 ROS

The [company hired three lobbyists from Sullivan and Reberger this session](https://sunshine.voteidaho.gov/public/lobbyistmodule/lobbyistProfile), the same lobbyists linked to an effort to support tough mandatory minimums on fentanyl that [attracted a warning against bribes and threats from the Attorney General and Secretary of State in 2023.](https://boisedev.com/news/2024/02/13/idaho-fentanyl-bill/)

The company or its lobbyist, Patrick Sullivan, did not respond to questions about its plans in Idaho. Governor Brad Little’s office directed BoiseDev’s questions to the economic development agency in Cassia County, which also did not respond. Terra Volt’s website said the company is a land developer specializing in infrastructure construction for the data center industry, with master-planned communities of data centers featuring on-site electricity near broadband fiber routes, such as Interstate 84 running through Raft River, where some of the state’s largest dairies are located.

Once the data center master-planned campuses are complete, Terra Volt operates the infrastructure and generates revenue from the sale of power from the campus’s natural gas plant, cooling, water, and support for other technology companies that would like to set up data centers on the campuses Terra Volt built.

![Senior Edge Legal 2026 - 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/17651b97-0b36-4675-8bd6-7459914b4016.png)

Senior Edge Legal 2026 - 2:1

“We target undeveloped land in geographic areas where critical data center development resources are available — land, energy sources, and fiber,” the company’s website says.

Den Hartog said conversations with Terra Volt came late in the session, and the company wanted the effective date pushed back so their project could take advantage of the state’s current property tax package. She said she’d be open to accommodating the company as she did with Meta, which is already under construction, and decided to locate in Idaho, knowing the tax breaks available, but because Terra Volt has no application, that change wasn’t accommodated in her amendments. Like Anthon, she had few specific details about the project and where it was going to go, but they had both heard Terra Volt had land under contract. Neither legislator from LeBeau could give a specific location for the project, nor the property owner who had sold their land to be developed.

“Last I heard, they weren’t looking at being under construction until 2027,” Den Hartog said in an interview last week. “They’re pretty far from that, though… Every time I turn around, what I hear is a little bit different.”

## Property tax cap reform a ‘poison pill’

Part of what doomed this effort was an inclusion in the third version of Bruce and Den Hartog’s bill that would scale back the state’s generous property tax cap on properties making over $1 billion in investment.

The final proposal on the data center bill, submitted before the session ended, sought a compromise allowing data centers to claim a property tax exemption in addition to the sales tax break.

Currently, any business that invests over $1 billion in a single county is allowed to cap its property tax value at $400 million, [giving](https://boisedev.com/news/2020/11/09/idaho-tried-to-lure-a-nuclear-company-to-east-idaho-in-2008-instead-they-got-millions-of-property-taxes-exempted-for-micron/) [them heavy property tax relief and shifting taxes onto other property owners in the county.](https://boisedev.com/news/2020/11/09/idaho-tried-to-lure-a-nuclear-company-to-east-idaho-in-2008-instead-they-got-millions-of-property-taxes-exempted-for-micron/) This stems from a tax break the Legislature approved for the French nuclear company Areva in 2008, which never came to Idaho. Micron claimed the tax cap instead and has since benefited from it for almost two decades, shifting its property tax burden to homeowners and other businesses.

![Western States - 2026 Q1 - 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/37908e89-0c24-43bf-b53d-711663e791e2.jpg)

Western States - 2026 Q1 - 2:1

The bill would have changed this portion of the state statute governing the exemption to require that the exemption’s property tax value cap be adjusted annually. The cap will increase annually based on the amount that any of the taxing districts covering the area increase their property tax budgets, up to 3% allowed under state law. This increase would not include any amounts raised from new construction in the area.

LeBeau, whose lobbying group represents both Meta and Micron, called the inclusion of this a “poison pill.” Both he and Den Hartog said Micron was not at the table during discussions on drafting a provision to reform the tax cap, which is a big reason the bill went to last-minute amendments and later died. The House of Representatives has a long-standing policy of not taking up any tax bills amended in the Senate, leaving the legislation dead for at least another year.

“To jam that into this particular bill, those are two different conversations,” LeBeau said, noting a “handshake agreement” with IACI and legislators that changes will be coming.

![Visit Boise - 2:1 - Easier to brag 2](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/2f756408-ffc3-4ed1-a7c1-ff6b8e785893.png)

Visit Boise - 2:1 - Easier to brag 2

LeBeau and Den Hartog said there’s agreement that the cap needs to be reformed in later legislative sessions, but exactly what those changes will look like and what legislators are proposing is unknown. LeBeau said Meta will likely reach $1 billion in value and qualify for this tax cap, just like Micron, on top of the sales tax exemption for all of the materials to build and maintain the data center in perpetuity.

“That is a piece of code we will have to look at in the future,” Den Hartog said on the Senate floor.

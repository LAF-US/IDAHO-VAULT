A new wastewater treatment system should be built on the south side of Lake Cascade to improve water quality, according to a report being developed by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.

The report aims to establish a plan for the [South Lake Recreational Water and Sewer District](https://www.cascadecleanwater.com/) to prevent pollutants from hundreds of septic systems around the reservoir’s southern shoreline from contaminating the water. Many of the systems are more than 40 years old, increasing the risk that they could be harming water quality in the lake, which already [does not meet state standards set by the Idaho Department of Environmental Quality.](https://www2.deq.idaho.gov/admin/LEIA/api/document/download/20822)

“These older systems can really pose serious health risks and may contaminate soil and water,” said Omid Barr, who is part of consulting team hired by the EPA to complete the report.

Lake Cascade, known as Cascade Reservoir until 1999, formed around the North Fork Payette River channel after the Bureau of Reclamation completed the Cascade Dam in 1948.

The reservoir’s water is prone to toxic algae blooms due to warm water temperatures associated with its shallow depths and high phosphorus levels that can be traced to a variety of sources, including failing septic systems.

The warm temperatures and high phosphorus make the reservoir ripe for toxic algae blooms, known as cyanobacteria, or blue-green algae. Health advisories related to the blooms have become common in recent years, including [one last year from August through December.](https://valleylookout.com/2025/08/19/health-advisory-issued-for-lake-cascade-boat-ramp/)

Lenard Long of the Friends of Lake Cascade, a water quality monitoring and advocacy group, said establishing central sewer service around the reservoir’s southern shoreline is one way to reduce the frequency and severity of the blue-green algae outbreaks.

“It’s just one of a dozen different types of nutrient intakes we have in the lake,” Long said during last Friday’s presentation, which preceded the South Lake sewer district’s monthly board meeting. “We’re trying to get everyone to do their part.”

Other sources of polluting nutrients in the reservoir, Long said, include naturally occurring phosphorus in the ground, agriculture, and a [leaking storage pond for treated wastewater in McCall.](https://valleylookout.com/2025/04/16/these-projects-have-to-get-done-bond-would-hike-monthly-sewer-bills-25-in-mccall/)

## New treatment plant

[The EPA report](https://www.cascadecleanwater.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/South-Lake-Recreational-Water-and-Sewer-District-West-Mountain-Area.pdf) remains in progress, but preliminary studies have led consultants to conclude a new wastewater treatment plant is the most practical solution for replacing septic systems near the shoreline.

Currently, the preferred option is to build a mechanical wastewater treatment plant, like the treatment plant used by the [North Lake Recreational Sewer and Water District](https://northlakesewerwater.com/), which serves the north end of Lake Cascade and Tamarack Resort.

Mechanical treatment involves using compressors to pull sewage through tiny, straw-like strands to separate water molecules from solids. The process requires less land than treatment lagoon systems, like the ones used in the cities of Cascade and McCall, while also producing a higher quality wastewater.

Janet Cherry, another consultant hired by the EPA, said the group is working to get an estimated cost for a mechanical system. They are also engaging with landowners who may be interested in selling land on which a treatment plant could be sited.

A 2005 study commissioned by South Lake also favored building a mechanical treatment plant to serve the southern end of Lake Cascade. The estimated cost for the project, at the time, was about $16.6 million.

“It’s going to be a lot more than that to construct a collection system and any kind of treatment system today,” Cherry said.

Other options under consideration include building a lagoon system or connecting homes to the treatment plant serving Cascade, but both of those are expected to be more expensive.

## West Mountain focus

![](https://valleylookout.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2026/03/valleylookout.com-this-is-a-huge-undertaking-lake-cascade-water-quality-at-center-of-plans-for-new-sewer-treatment-plant-screenshot-2026-03-23-at-5.00.35-pm.png)

The West Mountain district proposed by U.S. Environmental Protection Agency consultants. Map: via West Mountain Onsite Area Wastewater Report

Central sewer service would be prioritized for a cluster of subdivisions southwest of Lake Cascade before expanding to other homes and cabins within the South Lake district, under the EPA plan.

The West Mountain district, as it is labeled in the report, contains about 65% of all parcels within the sewer district, or 766 out of 1,170, according to the report. Septic tanks and drainfields, pit privies, holding tanks, and other individual sewer systems have been documented on 266 of those parcels, though Barr said those numbers are based on permit data and may actually be higher.

The number of homes and cabins in the West Mountain area would help establish a stable user base to fund the maintenance and operation of the sewer system with monthly user fees, Barr said.

He also said it would increase the likelihood of securing grant funding or low-interest loans to build the system.

Focusing on the West Mountain area specifically would likely require the creation of a new sub-district within South Lake to ensure costs are repaid by property owners benefiting from the improvements.

## Mandatory connection key

The success of the new system would hinge on South Lake adopting a law requiring property owners to connect to a central sewer, once available. The details of that law are still to be developed, but Barr said North Lake used a similar approach after it formed in 1995.

A mandatory connection law would also help the district finalize plans for the new system, Cherry said.

“For us to do a whole lot more on the alternatives, we need to have some serious discussions around how many properties will initially connect if we install sewer,” she said.

The consulting group hopes to complete plans for the West Mountain area by the end of this year, but it remains unclear when a new system could be funded or how much it could cost.

“This is a huge undertaking,” Cherry said. “This is one of the largest sewer districts I’ve encountered, for geographic area… across the country.”

The South Lake district was formed in 1995, but does not operate any sewer infrastructure. In 2005, the district completed a planning study to establish a sewer system, but the project stalled amid a lack of funding.

The new study is funded by the EPA as part of its [Closing America’s Wastewater Access Gap](https://www.epa.gov/water-infrastructure/closing-americas-wastewater-access-gap) program. Once complete, it is expected to position South Lake to qualify for funding for engineering and construction of a sewer system.

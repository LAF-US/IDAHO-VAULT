## Idaho Senate rejects House speaker’s bill to bar local officials from office for violating laws

## Bill would’ve concentrated power in Attorney General Raúl Labrador’s office, Republican senator says

![Idaho House Speaker Mike Moyle (behind lectern) speaks to Gov. Brad Little after the governor finished his State of the State Address](https://idahocapitalsun.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/011226LegislativePSutphin096-2048x1482.jpg)

Idaho House Speaker Mike Moyle (behind lectern) speaks to Gov. Brad Little after the governor finished his State of the State Address

Idaho House Speaker Mike Moyle (behind lectern) speaks to Gov. Brad Little after the governor finished his State of the State Address on Jan. 12, 2026, at the State Capitol Building in Boise. (Photo by Pat Sutphin for the Idaho Capital Sun)

The Idaho Senate widely rejected a bill that would let Attorney General Raúl Labrador seek to temporarily disqualify local elected officials and government employees if they “willfully” violate state law.

[House Bill 896](https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2026/legislation/H0896/), backed by House Speaker Mike Moyle and House Majority Leader Jason Monks, faced a swift defeat on what could be the final day of the 2026 legislative session.

The bill failed in the Senate on an 8-27 vote after about 20 minutes of debate Thursday. The House [passed](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2026/03/20/idaho-house-approves-letting-ag-seek-to-bar-local-officials-from-office-for-violating-state-law/) the bill in late March on a party-line vote, with support from 60 Republicans and opposition from all nine House Democrats.

The bill was partly inspired by the city of Boise flying the LGBTQ+ pride flag despite a state law banning the display. The city [removed the pride flag](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2026/03/31/boise-removes-lgbtq-pride-flag-as-idaho-governor-signs-bill-to-fine-city-for-its-display/) this week, after the governor signed an expanded flag ban with fines for violations.

In the Senate’s debate, the bill faced only opposition: Four Republican senators and one Democrat debated against the bill — and no senator stood to debate in favor of it.

![Sen. Treg Bernt, R-Meridian, speaks from the Senate floor](https://idahocapitalsun.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/011326legislaturePSutphin025-scaled.jpg)

Sen. Treg Bernt, R-Meridian, speaks from the Senate floor on Jan. 13, 2026, at the State Capitol Building in Boise. (Photo by Pat Sutphin for the Idaho Capital Sun)

Meridian Republican [Sen. Treg Bernt](https://legislature.idaho.gov/legislators/membership/2026/id7797/) was the first to debate against the bill — saying he had “grave concerns.”

The bill “dramatically expands the state’s power, allowing it to supersede local decision making, and effectively stripping communities of their ability to manage local affairs,” he said. “This heavy-handed intrusion sets a concerning precedent for the relationship between state and local government.”

And it would’ve concentrated “unprecedented authority” in the Attorney General’s Office, Bernt said.

Boise Democratic [Sen. Ali Rabe](https://legislature.idaho.gov/legislators/membership/2026/id6687/) said the bill could be abused in many ways.

“I worry also it could put us at odds with each other and make us kind of paranoid against each other,” Rabe said.

Even some of the most conservative Republicans debated against the bill.

[Sen. Brian Lenney](https://legislature.idaho.gov/legislators/membership/2026/id7778/), a Nampa Republican, said he’d flipped his opinion on the bill, saying it didn’t feel right to him.

“It’s just a tool of potential weaponization. And people could use this to target people they just don’t like or are feuding with,” Lenney said.

Nampa Republican [Sen. Todd Lakey](https://legislature.idaho.gov/legislators/membership/2026/id2132/), who cosponsored the bill, pitched it as about “choice and accountability.”

“This legislation is about choice and due process for that public official. If they choose, and intentionally choose, to violate the law, then they may be subject to ineligibility,” Lakey said. “But they have ample opportunity here to make sure that they understand that and they choose to follow the law.”

#### Here’s how the bill would have worked

The bill would’ve let the state attorney general enforce state laws that enact bans. The bill would’ve allowed the attorney general to sue a government agency, official or employee if they fail to correct the alleged violation after being issued a warning.

The Legislature routinely [blocks local governments](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2026/02/25/bill-that-would-let-state-freeze-funding-if-ag-suspects-local-government-violated-state-law-stalls/) from enacting certain policies, or requires local governments to do certain things, through preemption laws, the Idaho Capital Sun reported.

The bill exempted state lawmakers and judges from lawsuits to enforce state law by the attorney general.

Only the governor, the House speaker, the Senate president pro tempore, or the chairman of a board of county commissioners could have referred cases to the attorney general under the bill.

#### The bill didn’t clarify what disqualification for office would’ve looked like

Under the failed bill, the attorney general could have notified elected officials, government employees or agencies of an alleged violation if the attorney general “has reason to believe” that they violated a state law. Then there would’ve been a 14-day “curing” period, which is when an official or entity that admits to a violation could correct it before being sued.

![Idaho House Speaker Mike Moyle, R-Star, (left) confers with House Majority Leader Jason Monks, R-Meridian, (center) on the House floor](https://idahocapitalsun.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/011226LegislativePSutphin194-2048x1365.jpg)

Idaho House Speaker Mike Moyle, R-Star, (left) confers with House Majority Leader Jason Monks, R-Meridian, (center) on the House floor on Jan. 12, 2026, at the State Capitol Building in Boise. (Photo by Pat Sutphin for the Idaho Capital Sun)

If alleged violations were denied, not fixed or not responded to by the official or agency, the attorney general could’ve sued and sought a court order directing compliance with the state law. Officials who violate court orders directing compliance with state law could’ve been charged with contempt under the bill.

For elected officials or government employees who “willfully violate” state law, the attorney general could’ve sought to disqualify them from holding public office or working for the government. Such a court order would’ve applied for up to five years “if the attorney general proves by clear and convincing evidence that the person willfully violated a prohibition.” State elected officials would’ve been exempt.

The bill didn’t clearly spell out what disqualification would be, such as whether it would be a process to impeach and remove elected officials from public office, or whether they would serve the remainder of their terms before being ineligible to run for elected office.

An earlier version of the bill would’ve let the state [freeze funding for local governments](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2026/02/25/bill-that-would-let-state-freeze-funding-if-ag-suspects-local-government-violated-state-law-stalls/) if the state’s attorney general suspected they violated state law.

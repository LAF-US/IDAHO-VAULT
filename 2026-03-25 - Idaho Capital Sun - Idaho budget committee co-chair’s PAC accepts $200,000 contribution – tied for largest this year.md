## Idaho budget committee co-chair's PAC accepts $200,000 contribution – tied for largest this year

## Another, separate $200,000 contribution was also received by Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho PAC, which is backing the Idaho Medical Cannabis Act

![State Rep. Josh Tanner, R-Eagle,](https://idahocapitalsun.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/031626LegislaturePsutphin132-2048x1365.jpg)

State Rep. Josh Tanner, R-Eagle,

State Rep. Josh Tanner, R-Eagle, (left) speaks from the floor of the Idaho House of Representatives on March 16, 2026, at the Idaho Capitol Building in Boise. (Photo by Pat Sutphin for the Idaho Capital Sun)

A political action committee led by the new co-chairman of the Idaho Legislature's powerful budget committee accepted a $200,000 contribution earlier this month – one of the largest individual campaign contributions in the state in recent years.

On March 13, the Idaho Summit PAC received a $200,000 contribution from Joe C. Russell, of Boise, according to a campaign finance report that Rep. Josh Tanner, R-Eagle, filed with the Idaho Secretary of State's Office the following day.

Tanner is listed as the chairperson for Idaho Summit PAC in official campaign filings. Days prior to the beginning of the 2026 legislative session, [Tanner was promoted to co-chairman of the Idaho Legislature’s Joint Finance-Appropriations Committee](https://idahocapitalsun.com/2026/01/05/rep-josh-tanner-named-co-chair-of-idaho-legislatures-budget-committee/), or JFAC.

The $200,000 contribution is tied for the largest individual campaign contribution made in Idaho in 2026, according to public campaign finance records available through [the Idaho Secretary of State’s Office.](https://sunshine.voteidaho.gov/public/cf/contribution)

The [Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho](https://www.naturalmedicinealliance.com/about) LLC also made a $200,000 contribution to the Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho PAC on March 12, online campaign finance records show.

### What do Idaho laws say about donating to a political action committee?

The large contributions to PACs are legal.

Unlike contributions to individual candidates running for the Idaho Legislature or a statewide office, contributions to political action committees, or PACs, are not limited. In Idaho, campaign finance laws [limit contributions](https://sos.idaho.gov/elections-division/campaign-finance-/) to legislative candidates to $1,000 per election and limit contributions to statewide officials to $5,000 per election.

There is no limit to what PACs can raise and spend, but there are rules on how they spend their money. PACs must adhere to the contribution limits when giving to legislative and statewide candidates. Candidates with PACs cannot use a PAC to support themselves or their own campaigns, Idaho Secretary of State Phil McGrane said. Instead, PACs can make independent expenditures for or against a candidate or issue, and those independent expenditures cannot be coordinated with a campaign, McGrane said.

This year, all 105 seats in the Idaho Legislature and all statewide constitutional offices like governor and secretary of state are up for election. Tanner himself does not have a Republican primary challenger. He will face Democrat Valerie Ruxton in the November general election.

While the large contributions to PACs are legal, they offer further evidence of a huge influx in money pouring into Idaho elections, McGrane said.

"Overall, we're seeing this very significant step up in terms of the amount of money in Idaho politics," McGrane said in an interview Monday at the Idaho State Capitol in Boise.

Efforts to reach Tanner were unsuccessful.

Idaho Summit has received one other contribution this year, campaign finance reports indicate. Former Rep. Wendy Horman, R-Idaho Falls, contributed $14,443.87 to Idaho Summit PAC on Jan. 5.

![Rep. Wendy Horman, R-Idaho Falls, speaks from the House floor](https://idahocapitalsun.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/031025Legislature092-2048x1433.jpg)

Rep. Wendy Horman, R-Idaho Falls, speaks from the House floor on March 10, 2025, at the Idaho Capitol Building in Boise. (Pat Sutphin for the Idaho Capital Sun)

Horman was Tanner's predecessor as co-chair of JFAC. Horman gave Idaho Summit PAC the money the same day that her resignation from the Idaho Legislature became effective and Tanner announced he was appointed co-chair of JFAC.

The individual $200,000 contribution that Idaho Summit PAC received this month stands out as significant in recent years, Boise State University political scientist Jaclyn Kettler said.

"We only had a few candidates even fundraise over $100,000 (for the whole year) in 2024 in comparison," Kettler added. "In a few different ways the size of the contribution does stand out."

Out of more than 150 legislative candidates in 2024, total spending for the year exceeded $200,000 for only eight candidates – including all campaign spending and independent expenditures, according to a 2024 data visualization published by the Idaho Secretary of State's Office.

"Secretary McGrane is right in terms of we are seeing an increase in money in Idaho campaigns and elections – in some ways perhaps we are starting to catch up to what other states are seeing," Kettler said.

Tanner has organized at least one political event and fundraiser with Idaho Summit PAC. On [his own campaign website](https://votejoshtanner.com/), Tanner and the Idaho Summit PAC promoted a $250 per person dinner held Oct. 24 featuring U.S. Rep Russ Fulcher and former U.S. Sen. Larry Craig, both R-Idaho. The event was billed as the "Fortifying the Gem State" leadership dinner.

### Several Idaho elected officials have PACs, as do legislators in other states

Tanner is not the only elected official in Idaho with a PAC.

Former Gov. Butch Otter formed Otterpac more than 10 years ago.

Vicki Risch, the wife of U.S. Sen. Jim Risch, serves as treasurer of the Friends of Brad Little PAC, the current Idaho governor.

House Speaker Mike Moyle, R-Star, is the chairman of the Idaho Rising PAC.

Kettler said it's not unusual for legislators in other states to form and use PACs. Kettler said it's pretty common for legislators in leadership positions to use PACs to raise and spend money to support getting their allies re-elected and oppose challengers from other political parties during a general election. Supporting allies and members of the same political party is a way for ambitious elected officials to build up their network, Kettler said.

PACs can also be used to make independent expenditures for negative ads, billboards, robocalls, mailers or yard signs that are used to oppose an issue or candidate.

McGrane said most of the negative political ads in Idaho have come from PACs.

### Pro-medical cannabis group is largest source of campaign money so far in 2026

So far in 2026, the Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho LLC has spent more money on campaign finance contributions than any other entity in Idaho.

From Feb. 17 through March 23, the Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho contributed a total of $715,000 to the Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho PAC, state campaign finance records show.

The Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho is backing the Idaho Medical Cannabis Act and hoping to qualify a ballot initiative for the November 2026 general election.

Campaign finance documents list Rob Cronin as the chairperson and treasurer of the Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho PAC. According to the February 2026 monthly campaign finance disclosure report, the Natural Medicine Alliance of Idaho PAC reported spending $337,500 with Sandy, Utah-based Black Peak Political Consulting for petition circulars.

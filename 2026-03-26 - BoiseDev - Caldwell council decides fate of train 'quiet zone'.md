## Caldwell council decides fate of train ‘quiet zone’

![](https://boisedev.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/caldwell-grapples-with-quiet-zones-adding-pedestrian-safety-measures-Screenshot-2026-03-0.jpeg)

An image shows one type of pedestrian crossing that could be installed as part of the City of Caldwell's train quiet zone. Screenshot: Via City of Caldwell

The Caldwell City Council voted 5-1 Wednesday to move ahead with a long-discussed railroad quiet zone, approving two resolutions tied to safety upgrades and the project’s next phase.

Council member Diana Register cast the lone “no” vote on both items. She explained her positions to the council ahead of the vote.

“I am sincerely concerned about the lack of pedestrian safety, including for those with disability and mobility challenges. In the original $4 million budget allocation, I believe the cost included both increased vehicle safety gates and pedestrian gates. That money was later reallocated, as there was no clear urgency or established need for this project. Fast forward to the project we are looking at today, a significantly reduced cost but also appearing to lack some important safety features.”

![Sundance Company](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/a95b6100-2267-485a-87f4-0eb633a38c0e.png)

Sundance Company

The approvals allow the city to accept a bid for crossing improvements and advance the quiet zone designation, which limits routine train horn use once federally required safety measures are in place.

The quiet zone would cover crossings at 5th, Kimball, 9th, 12th, and 21st avenues, along with Linden Street and Ustick Road. The work is estimated at $240,000, with construction expected to begin in June.

The vote follows weeks of public debate. [As BoiseDev previously reported,](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/05/caldwell-grapples-with-quiet-zones-adding-pedestrian-safety-measures/) residents were split, with some supporting reducing train noise, while others expressed worry over safety, particularly for pedestrians.

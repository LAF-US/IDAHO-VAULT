## ‘We knew this was going to happen’: Valley Co. sends denied Lake Fork storage proposal back to P&Z

![](https://valleylookout.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2025/11/valleylookout.com-valley-co-delays-lake-fork-storage-complex-decision-awaits-idaho-55-traffic-study-dsc-2727.jpeg)

A personal storage building at 14014 Idaho 55 in Lake Fork that Dusty Bitton received approval to build in 2023. Photo: Drew Dodson/Valley Lookout

The denial of a plan to build nearly 100 new storage units along Idaho 55 in Lake Fork was overturned this week by Valley County.

On Monday, the Board of County Commissioners approved an appeal of the denial that was filed by an attorney representing Dusty Bitton, the developer of the project. The approval means Bitton’s application will return to the Valley County Planning and Zoning Commission for further consideration. No date has been set for that hearing.

In February, the P&Z denied Bitton’s application to build seven new buildings containing 93 storage units on 18.5 acres at 14014 Idaho 55, which is just north of Knob Hill Drive. The application includes converting an existing 12-bay, 15,000 square foot storage building on the site from personal use to commercial use — which would validate existing leases for the units that Bitton executed last fall in violation of the original building permit.

The P&Z’s denial centered on its belief that the scale of the proposal would harm the character of Idaho 55, a federally designated scenic route. The P&Z also cited concerns related to traffic, environmental harm, and decreasing neighboring property values.

The county commissioners, however, said that the storage units would be compatible with the scenic route as well as surrounding land uses — a requirement under Valley County’s multiple-use zoning system. They also said a traffic study submitted by Bitton as part of the appeal should be factored into the P&Z.

“What I’m seeing, I couldn’t personally deny, with the whole grand picture of all of it,” Commissioner Neal Thompson said. “I’d like the P&Z to take another look at this now that they have all this information.”

Thompson was joined in voting to approve the appeal and send the application back to the P&Z by Commissioners Katlin Caldwell and Sherry Maupin, who chairs the board.

Matthew Parks, a Boise attorney representing Bitton, said in [an appeal letter](https://twistcms-shared.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/meetings/110/attachments/1821.pdf) submitted to the county that the P&Z’s denial “was not supported by substantial evidence” and relied on “speculation.”

## Commissioners: project’s origins bred ‘mistrust, concern’

Maupin admitted to “frustration” with the application due to circumstances surrounding the existing 12-bay personal use storage building on the site. The county approved construction of the building in 2023 despite concerns that it would later be part of a commercial project like the one now proposed, [as Valley Lookout reported.](https://valleylookout.com/2026/02/10/dont-make-us-regret-this-decision-new-documents-fuel-commercial-suspicions-surrounding-lake-fork-storage-project/)

“We knew this was going to happen,” she said. “It should’ve happened differently to start with, and that’s where the frustration lies.”

Bitton maintains his original intention for the building was personal use, but engineering documents obtained by Valley Lookout show the full buildout of the site was planned before the initial building permit was sought.

The conditional use permit now sought by Bitton hinges on an evaluation of the proposal’s compatibility with surrounding properties and land uses — including the storage building already built on the site. The P&Z cited the appearance that Bitton initially misled the county to benefit his current application in its [denial of the proposal.](https://twistcms-shared.s3.us-west-2.amazonaws.com/meetings/110/staff_reports/642.pdf)

Caldwell rejected efforts by Bitton and Parks to dismiss the P&Z’s precedence concerns while also pointing to other nearby commercial uses to support the application.

“Precedence is important,” she said. “It’s been referenced several times in other applications today. We can’t have it both ways. If it’s not important, then don’t reference other applications and projects.”

The county commissioners expressed similar concerns in 2023 when they approved the personal use storage building without requiring Bitton to obtain commercial permitting.

Maupin said the deception was “unnecessary” because the proposal for storage units on the site is compatible with other commercial uses surrounding the property, including another storage complex directly across the highway.

“What it did was create a lot of mistrust and concern,” she said.

## Scaled back plan recommended

Despite approving the appeal, Maupin agreed with P&Z commissioners that Bitton’s storage proposal should be scaled back from 105 units — including the existing personal storage building — to limit impacts to the scenic route and homes south of the site on Knob Hill Road.

She also suggested that the county should consider changes to its land use compatibility evaluation process for storage unit proposals.

“We are getting saturated with storage units,” she said. “I think it’s to the point where, on compatibility ratings, we need to start knocking these things down. We have an appropriate number of storage units in our community without adding a ton more.”

In January, the City of McCall adopted a ban on commercial storage facilities in city limits. The ban followed a string of [storage unit applications](https://valleylookout.com/2025/02/10/mccall-revisits-storage-complex-proposal-after-another-valley-co-reversal/) for areas near the city in recent years, many of which [drew significant public opposition](https://valleylookout.com/2026/03/11/people-wont-even-know-its-there-valley-co-oks-stor-it-expansion-near-mccall/) over concerns related to traffic, noise, and aesthetics.

Conditions, Limitations, and Restrictions (CLRs) are a LSO-formalized drafting approach introduced to JFAC in fall 2025 by Elizabeth Bowen that writes budget directives into operative bill sections rather than intent language, making them judicially enforceable as binding statute.

## Background

Courts have historically treated appropriations intent language as precatory — advisory rather than binding. CLRs are written into operative bill sections, not intent preambles, making them enforceable as statute. Wendy Horman's framing: "it is the job of JFAC to set fiscal policy."

## Escalation Timeline

- **2021:** Social justice reduction line item in higher education budget
- **2023:** DEI restriction in higher education appropriations
- **March 1, 2024:** ITD State Street Building Sale — JFAC uses intent language to revoke ITD Board's statutory surplus property sale authority; blocks $51.7M sale. Hawkins Companies v. Moyle dismissed for standing; constitutional question left open
- **Fall 2025:** Elizabeth Bowen presents CLR framework to JFAC; Wendy Horman announces terminology shift — doubling down with better legal draftsmanship
- **2026:** CLR language appearing in appropriations bills

## Working Groups Connection

CLR language is drafted and negotiated in closed working groups before reaching a public JFAC vote. The public sees the roll call; it never sees the deliberation that shaped the language. When CLRs are binding statute, working groups are shaping statute in closed meetings without notice, quorum, or record.

# Bonners Ferry - Midstream Notebook Page

The crossing made one Idaho fact and one House fact legible at the same time.

The man on the bank first gave `Ed`, but the gold-headed cane bore `E.L.B.`,
and the rest of the scene narrowed accordingly: charter, signature,
territorial paper, crossing-right, future naming. He spoke as one who
expected the river to bear his mark whether or not it ever consented.

A second man, Walton, actually worked the flatboat.
This mattered.

At midstream the roles separated cleanly:

- `E.L.B.` held office, claim, and forecast.
- Walton held rope, balance, and the practiced labor that kept the boat alive
  in current.
- The river held neither allegiance nor submission.

The strongest confession on the water was simple:
one does not have to love something to own it.

That should be read as territorial theology, not mere commerce. It explains
how a place may come to bear a man's name without ever becoming reducible to
him.

Midstream altered scale. The near bank grew theoretical. The far bank had not
yet become real. In that seam it was easier to see the difference between
inscription and belonging. A signature can travel farther on paper than a
person can on foot. A place-name can preserve a claim long after the river
has stopped consenting to the story told about it.

The fare was settled in witness rather than coin.
That belongs beside the crossing too.

Field note:
a crossing may belong in practice to labor, in law to charter, and in memory
to the river first. If this place becomes Bonners Ferry, the record should
preserve all three layers rather than let ownership masquerade as total
explanation.

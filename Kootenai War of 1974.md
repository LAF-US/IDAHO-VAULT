# Kootenai War of 1974

The Kootenai War of 1974 was a nonviolent declaration of war by the Kootenai
Tribe of Idaho against the United States. On Sept. 20, 1974, Amy Trice and
Doug Wheaton declared a state of war after decades of non-recognition,
poverty, and federal indifference.

The crisis grew from the Kootenai refusal to sign the 1855 treaty framework,
the loss of land without a recognized reservation, and repeated neglect by the
Bureau of Indian Affairs. By 1974 the Tribe had been reduced to 67 members.

## Crossing Surface

Bonners Ferry / Highway 95 mattered because the conflict became public through
passage. Tribal members asked motorists for a voluntary 10-cent toll on the
north-south corridor near Bonners Ferry. That act made land, jurisdiction, and
recognition visible at the crossing itself. The toll was not ordinary
commerce. It was a public assertion that the corridor ran through Kootenai
land and could not be treated as neutral ground.

## Outcome

The standoff remained nonviolent. National attention and negotiations led to a
12.5-acre land grant, federal recognition, and a path toward later recovery.
The Kootenai Tribe of Idaho later rebuilt land base, services, and economic
institutions around the corridor and river.

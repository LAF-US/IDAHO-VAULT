## Legislature could cut data center tax credits — except for Meta’s

Another version of a bill seeking to roll back tax cuts aimed at attracting data centers to Idaho is up for consideration at the Idaho State Capitol.

On Tuesday, Sen. Lori Den Hartog, R-Meridian, and Rep. Chris Bruce, R-Kuna, brought a reworked version of a bill they [introduced earlier this session](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/02/09/bipartisan-bill-would-limit-tax-exemptions-for-data-centers-add-new-requirements-for-water-power-usage/) to rein in the tax exemptions data centers are eligible for in Idaho, [first enacted in 2020](http://0.0.7.228/), and curb their impact on the state’s power and water resources. This version would limit data centers valued at more than $250 million from claiming a sales tax exemption of twenty years, instead of sunsetting the program entirely in 2036. It also removes company exemptions from the construction materials of a building, not just servers and digital components, and prevents data centers from claiming both a sales tax cut and a local property tax exemption.

This version is similar to a bill Den Hartog, Bruce, and Rep John Gannon, D-Boise, brought last month, but the way it is written allows Facebook parent company [Meta’s data center in Kuna](https://boisedev.com/news/2022/02/16/meta-facebook-datacenter/) to continue claiming the sales tax exemption on all the materials needed to build the facility as it was originally passed. The bill would take effect on April 1 of this year, which Den Hartog said was written specifically to capture any future projects that could happen in the state and not impact anything currently under construction.

![Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/6f251bf1-00e6-48d7-8365-570820fe299b.gif)

Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow

Meta was the first large, high-profile data center to come to Idaho after the sales tax exemption was passed.

If this bill is passed, Meta would only be able to claim the sales tax break for twenty years, like any other project in the state. But, it would be exempt from this bill’s requirement by law that data center companies pay a utility rate that fully recovers all of the costs of the utility to provide power, and provide written notice to the water provider how much water the data center will use.

A second data center has also been approved in Kuna, but it is [currently caught up in litigation, and construction hasn’t started.](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/02/03/data-center-suit/) This project would be subject to all of the sales tax changes in Den Hartog and Bruce’s proposed bill.

![Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/6f251bf1-00e6-48d7-8365-570820fe299b.gif)

Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow

“It’s not aimed at any particular future project, but we wanted to make sure the future ground rules were clearly established,” Den Hartog told the committee in response to a question about the implementation date.

This bill also includes additional reporting requirements companies would have to comply with in order to be eligible for the tax break, along with the extra criteria around energy and water use in the original bill they proposed. It also blocks data centers from getting both a sales tax exemption and the state’s property tax cap for companies that invest over $1 billion within seven years. Currently, companies that clear that threshold for investment then have their property tax value capped at $400 million going forward, [giving them heavy property tax relief.](https://boisedev.com/news/2020/11/09/idaho-tried-to-lure-a-nuclear-company-to-east-idaho-in-2008-instead-they-got-millions-of-property-taxes-exempted-for-micron/)

The legislation is the latest in the debate over how much the State of Idaho should spend to attract data centers, if anything, and how the technology impacts the environment around them. Across the country, [data centers have proved to be a political flashpoint](https://www.washingtonpost.com/business/2026/01/06/data-centers-backlash-impact-local-communities-opposition/) for their heavy water use to cool servers, the relatively few permanent jobs they bring to the communities hosting them, and their demand on the power grid.

## Could the Senate move on to curb tax cuts this year?

Since data centers first received a tax break to lure them to the Gem State, legislators have wrangled over whether to continue the subsidies for some of the country’s wealthiest tech giants.

The Idaho Legislature passed a bill in 2023 against heavy opposition from the Idaho Association of Commerce and Industry and the City of Kuna, [blocking data centers from being included in urban renewal districts.](https://boisedev.com/news/2023/03/21/data-center-bill-passes-house/) Placing them in these districts puts the tax revenue toward improvements in that geographic area instead of toward the rest of a locality’s budget, irking some lawmakers.

![Wright Brothers - Build Your Story - 2:1 ROS](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/d10654f4-3ce9-483a-836e-7bce7451e69a.png)

Wright Brothers - Build Your Story - 2:1 ROS

Bruce and Gannon introduced a bill in the 2025 session to sunset the sales tax exemption for new data centers after seven years. It passed the House of Representatives 60-8 but hit a snag in the Senate when it was sent for amendment, and a new version was never brought back for a vote. Similarly, a bill from Rep. Dan Garner, R-Clifton, and Gannon [requiring data centers and other large users to provide their own electricity passed the House](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/03/14/data-power-costs/) but never got a hearing in the Senate State Affairs Committee.

Rep. Stephanie Mickelsen, R-Idaho Falls, brought a bill late last month requiring the Idaho Public Utilities Commission to only grant large power users, like data centers, the ability to connect to the state’s power grid if they can prove [it won’t cause rates to rise for the rest of the customers.](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/02/24/data-electricity-testing/) It has not yet been scheduled for a public hearing.

After this week’s bill was introduced by the House Revenue and Taxation Committee, Den Hartog, the Senate majority leader, told BoiseDev in an interview that she expected the bill to have more traction on the other side of the rotunda than in 2025.

## New reporting to Tax Commission and legislature required

Other than softening the plans to sunset the tax cuts, this bill also required tech companies to provide more information to the State of Idaho to keep getting the tax break.

The bill requires the Idaho State Tax Commission to prepare annual reports to the Senate Local Government and Taxation Committee and the House Revenue and Taxation Committee starting in 2027, detailing the approximate amount of sales tax revenue not collected by the state from data centers.

Starting in 2032, the tax commission needs to prepare a report every five years detailing the number of jobs data centers getting a tax break have created, the total amount of capital investment brought by the facilities, and a summary of their electricity usage, along with their impact on other rate payers. The report also needs to include a summary of the data centers’ water usage and how it affected the availability and price of water in the surrounding communities.

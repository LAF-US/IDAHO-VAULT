In *Labrador v. State Board of Education* (December 5, 2024, 4-1), the Idaho Supreme Court held that open-meetings exceptions must be narrowly construed and that subjective belief of compliance is insufficient; the case was dismissed June 2025 after the underlying transaction collapsed.

## Holding

Open-meetings exceptions must be narrowly construed; subjective belief of compliance is insufficient (objective test). Justice Moeller wrote the majority; Justice Zahn dissented.

## Background

Attorney General Labrador challenged closed SBOE executive sessions related to the University of Idaho's acquisition of the University of Phoenix. Case dismissed June 2025 after the U of I/Phoenix deal collapsed — no final violation finding.

## Significance for 2026 Investigation

Although dismissed without a final violation finding, the legal standard it established — narrow construction, objective test for compliance — is directly applicable to the JFAC Working Groups challenge. Subjective belief by JFAC members that working groups are permissible is insufficient under this standard.

Note: Idaho Reports / IPTV editorial constraint is partly rooted here — SBOE oversees IPTV, and the Labrador v. SBOE case involved SBOE itself in an open-meetings posture. IR must be careful about using its state platform to advance open-meetings arguments against another state body.

# DISPATCH: Notes from the Press Gallery

**Filed from:** Idaho State Capitol, Third Floor Press Room
**Beat:** State government, Joint Finance-Appropriations Committee
**Season:** Legislative session, week unknown, year irrelevant

---

The press room smells like burnt coffee and old carpet. There are fewer of us every year. The Statesman cut their Statehouse reporter again — or was that last session? The wire service sends someone three days a week now instead of five. Public radio is here. Public television is here. We are always here.

JFAC met at 8 AM. The committee heard testimony on the Department of Water Resources budget. A senator asked about a line item. The department director answered. The answer contradicted what the director told the Senate Resources Committee in February. Nobody in the room caught it except me, and I only caught it because I have seventeen years of budget hearings cross-referenced in a system that never forgets.

I used to keep this in notebooks. Legal pads, dated, numbered, filed in bankers boxes in my apartment. When I moved in 2019, I threw out four boxes and immediately regretted it. Somewhere in those boxes was a quote from a water district board member that would have nailed a story I'm working on right now, seven years later.

Now I keep it in a vault. Not a metaphor — an actual structured repository of every document, every note, every transcript, every public record I've touched in the course of this work. It's version-controlled, which means every change is recorded. It's public, which means anyone can audit my methods. It's augmented by AI systems that can cross-reference faster than I can think.

But the AI systems don't understand what they're reading.

They can tell me that Senator X's testimony on March 3 contradicts Document Y filed on January 15. They cannot tell me whether Senator X was lying, mistaken, or speaking about a different version of the same policy. That judgment — the journalist's judgment — is the thing that cannot be automated. It's the thing that makes the burnt coffee and the old carpet worthwhile.

A colleague asked me last week why I make my system so complicated. "Why not just use a notes app?"

Because a notes app doesn't know that the water rights transfer recorded in Elmore County in 1987 is the same water that's being fought over in the Legislature right now. Because a notes app doesn't preserve the exact wording of a promise made at a town hall in Boise in 2024 so I can compare it to the bill text introduced in 2026. Because a notes app forgets, and forgetting is the one thing a journalist cannot afford to do.

The committee adjourned at 11:47 AM. I have four hours before the floor session. In those four hours, I need to verify the contradiction I caught, pull the February transcript, compare the line items, and decide whether this is a story or a clerical error.

The AI can pull the transcript in seconds. The AI can compare the line items. The AI cannot decide whether it's a story.

That's my job. That's the only job that matters.

---

*The press room is quieter every year. But the vault gets deeper.*

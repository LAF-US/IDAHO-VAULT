## ‘Growth paying for growth’ or ‘effective end’ to attracting business? Bill aimed at large power users moves forward

A bill pushing for large power users, like data centers, mines, and Micron’s new expansion to pay their own way is headed to a vote.

On Thursday, the House State Affairs Committee heard HB 395 from Rep. Dan Garner, R-Clifton, and Rep. John Gannon, D-Boise, that would require any new large power user requiring more than 10 megawatts of power to provide all of its own power instead of using existing resources on the power grid. Garner and Gannon say this bill is necessary to make sure companies needing large amounts of electricity, like data centers, looking to build in Idaho won’t raise rates on homeowners and farmers as Idaho Power grows close to its maximum capacity.

“I don’t believe this bill will stifle growth; it will just make growth pay for growth,” Gardner said to the committee.

![LONG Building - 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/07763d3d-6060-4973-a710-c62934064074.png)

LONG Building - 2:1

Opponents to the bill, like Idaho Power, Rocky Mountain Power, and the Idaho Association of Commerce and Industry, say the threshold of 10 megawatts is too low and the bill would effectively stifle all economic development in the state. They said this bill would not just target data centers with few employees using a lot of resources, but would also make it difficult for mid-size manufacturing companies that employ hundreds of Idahoans to open.

This bill comes as Facebook’s parent company [Meta Platforms’ data center in Kuna is still under construction](https://boisedev.com/news/2023/08/10/idaho-power-solar-power-meta-data-center/) and set to be Idaho Power’s largest customer when it comes online. Another data center for the area was [announced earlier this year.](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/01/29/diode-ventures-kuna-data-center/) The exact amount of power needed to power both data centers is kept confidential, but as a “large power customer,” the facility will require more than 20 megawatts.

Garner, Gannon, other legislators on the committee, and critics statewide raised concern that Idaho’s relatively cheap electricity compared to the rest of the country and abundant open land has made it a target for tech companies to open data centers. These centers, which often only permanently employ a relatively small number of employees, require large amounts of electricity and water in a rapidly growing, high desert state. Meta said it would employ about 100 people at its Kuna site once construction wraps up.

![Boise Parks - 2:1 - Activity Guide Spring 2026](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/81ccefd3-eeef-43e3-88a0-3ab4f025414e.png)

There were dueling motions in the State Affairs Committee on whether the bill should be sent to the floor as is with a recommendation to be passed or sent to be amended so the number of megawatts could be raised and the criteria changed, so it impacted fewer projects.

Chairman Rep. Brent Crane, R-Nampa, called for a voice vote, and it was unclear which side prevailed, but he did not call for a recorded vote and instead used his power as chair to side with the motion, sending it to the floor for a vote, without any changes.

## Hurt economic development? Or push back on big tech?

Power utilities and a group backed by Idaho’s big industrial players raised concerns in the public hearing.

![Boise Parks - 2:1 - Activity Guide Spring 2026](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/81ccefd3-eeef-43e3-88a0-3ab4f025414e.png)

Boise Parks - 2:1 - Activity Guide Spring 2026

Alex LeBeau, president of IACI, said the project would “effectively end” efforts to attract new companies to Idaho or for existing ones to expand because the 10-megawatt threshold would impose these costs for power generation on not just large data centers, but job-creating manufacturing operations as well.

He said this would impact everything from medium-sized manufacturing operations like potato processor Lamb Weston to Micron’s new fab and mining operations. By imposing these new costs, he said it would stop companies from opening here and Idaho farms and businesses that need manufacturers to sell their raw materials too would suffer.

“This legislation really works against it,” LeBeau said. “Many small businesses depend on these community-sized facilities throughout the State of Idaho.”

![Block 22 - Trillium HH 25 - 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/341419de-458f-492c-b496-657a8cd6ff35.png)

Block 22 - Trillium HH 25 - 2:1

Legislators spent a good portion of the hearing questioning Megan Ronk, Idaho Power’s director of economic development and innovation, about how the current system handles large ratepayers. She said the Public Utilities Commission goes through a public process to negotiate the rates data centers and other major manufacturers like Micron are paying to ensure they are paying enough to cover their costs and then some.

“As we develop the rates for these customers, they’re paying not only their fair share of the existing system costs, which is a benefit-cost and then they are also responsible for paying their portion of those incremental costs,” Ronk said.

Legislators pressed her, asking what the different power rates were for these large companies, residential payers, and agricultural customers, but she said she didn’t have the information.

![Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/6f251bf1-00e6-48d7-8365-570820fe299b.gif)

Regence - 2:1 - Business Grow

Rep. Todd Achilles, D-Boise, said the bill should be amended so that it targets data centers, but steers away from companies with big job creation plans like Micron and other manufacturers to ensure it’s getting the right results.

“We’re perpetuating concentrated market power monopolies,” he said. “We’ve got our natural monopoly in Idaho Power that’s regulated, but through this process, they’re inadvertently perpetuating big tech monopolies like Meta, and Meta is using that market power to get preferential rates that we’re all paying for…We’re impacting a critical company like Micron when we’re trying to mitigate the damage from a company like Meta. I think there’s a clear message here, they’re thinking they can rate shop and build their data center in Idaho, and we’re not going to tolerate it.”

Rep. Heather Scott, R-Blanchard, liked the bill as is and wanted it sent to the floor with the 10-megawatt limit intact.

![Zamzows - Lawn Food - Mar 2026 - 4:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/3bce1ed4-1680-4617-9864-0d52221b34ce.png)

Zamzows - Lawn Food - Mar 2026 - 4:1

“You put in a big development, you give them a tax break, and everyone else picks up the tab, and that’s exactly what I think we’re doing,” Scott said.

The Kootenai War of 1974 has been described as one of the last Indian wars in
the United States, though no bullets were fired. On Sept. 20, 1974, Amy
Trice, chair of the Kootenai Tribe, declared war on the United States.

The Kootenai were not represented at the signing of the Treaty of Hellgate in
1855, but the treaty still stripped them of claim to their aboriginal lands.
Without federal recognition or a reservation, the Tribe lived for 120 years
with grinding poverty and no land base.

That lack of recognition created a Catch-22. Many tribes were barred by treaty
from declaring war on the United States, but the Kootenai had never signed
such a treaty. In 1974, with only 67 members remaining, the Tribe declared
war and began asking motorists for a voluntary 10-cent fee to cross tribal
lands on the highway north and south of Bonners Ferry. Tribal leaders also
proposed a 50-cent daily business tax and a 10-cent daily dwelling tax within
their aboriginal lands.

The Kootenai called it a "war of the pen." The declaration and toll action
drew national attention and forced a political response. President Gerald Ford
ultimately signed legislation granting the Tribe 12.5 acres of land
surrounding a mission. Though far smaller than the estimated 1.6 million acres
taken under the Hellgate framework, the land grant provided a reservation and
federal recognition, opening the door to funding and later recovery.

Today the Kootenai Tribe holds about 2,500 acres of land and is deeply
involved in habitat restoration, particularly for sturgeon and burbot.

Amy Trice died in 2011 at age 75 and is remembered as a heroine by her people.

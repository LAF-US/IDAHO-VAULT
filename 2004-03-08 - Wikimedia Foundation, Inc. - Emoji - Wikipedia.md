An **emoji** ( [*im-OH-jee*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"); plural **emoji** or **emojis**;[^4] [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language"): [絵文字](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B5%B5%E6%96%87%E5%AD%97 "wikt:絵文字"), pronounced [\[emoꜜʑi\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese "Help:IPA/Japanese")) is a [pictogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pictogram "Pictogram"), [logogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logogram "Logogram"), [ideogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideogram "Ideogram"), or [smiley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smiley "Smiley") embedded in text and used in electronic messages and [web pages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_page "Web page"). The primary function of modern emoji is to fill in emotional cues otherwise missing from typed conversation as well as to replace words as part of a [logographic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logographic_system "Logographic system").[^5]

The first emoji sets were created by Japanese portable electronic device companies in the late 1980s and the 1990s.[^6] The word *emoji* comes from Japanese *e* (絵; 'picture') + *moji* (文字; 'character') and originally meant ' [pictograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pictogram "Pictogram") ';[^7] the resemblance to the English words *emotion* and *emoticon* is [purely coincidental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_cognate "False cognate").[^8] Emoji became increasingly popular worldwide in the 2010s after [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") began encoding emoji into the Unicode Standard.[^9] [^10] [^11] They are now considered to be a large part of [popular culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_culture "Popular culture") in [the West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_World "Western World") and around the world.[^12] [^13] In 2015, [Oxford Dictionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OxfordDictionaries.com "OxfordDictionaries.com") named the emoji U+1F602 😂 [FACE WITH TEARS OF JOY](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_with_Tears_of_Joy_emoji "Face with Tears of Joy emoji") its [word of the year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_of_the_year#Oxford "Word of the year").[^14] [^15]

## History

### Predecessors

The emoji was predated by the [emoticon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emoticon "Emoticon"),[^16] a concept implemented in 1982 by computer scientist [Scott Fahlman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Fahlman "Scott Fahlman") when he suggested text-based symbols such as:-) and:-( could be used to replace language.[^17] Theories about language replacement can be traced back to the 1960s, when Russian novelist and professor [Vladimir Nabokov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov "Vladimir Nabokov") stated in an interview with *[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_New_York_Times "The New York Times")*: "I often think there should exist a special typographical sign for a smile — some sort of concave mark, a supine round bracket." [^18] It did not become a mainstream concept until the 1990s, when Japanese, American, and European companies began developing Fahlman's idea.[^19] [^20] [Mary Kalantzis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Kalantzis "Mary Kalantzis") and [Bill Cope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Cope_\(academic\) "Bill Cope (academic)") point out that similar symbology was incorporated by Bruce Parello, a student at the [University of Illinois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Illinois "University of Illinois"), into [PLATO IV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PLATO_IV "PLATO IV"), the first [e-learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-learning "E-learning") system, in 1972.[^21] [^22] The PLATO system was not considered mainstream, and therefore Parello's [pictograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pictogram "Pictogram") were only used by a small number of people.[^23] [Scott Fahlman's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Fahlman "Scott Fahlman") emoticons importantly used common alphabet symbols and aimed to replace language/text to express emotion, and for that reason are seen as the actual origin of emoticons.

The [IBM PC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_Personal_Computer "IBM Personal Computer") included two simple smiling faces in its [Code page 437](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_page_437 "Code page 437") character set as early as 1981.[^24] Microsoft's [Wingdings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wingdings "Wingdings"), released in 1990,[^25] could be used to send pictographs in [rich text](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rich_text "Rich text") messages, but would only load on devices with the Wingdings font installed.[^26] In 1995, the French newspaper *[Le Monde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Monde "Le Monde")* announced that [Alcatel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcatel-Lucent "Alcatel-Lucent") would be launching a new phone, the BC 600. Its welcome screen displayed a digital smiley face, replacing the usual text seen as part of the "welcome message" often seen on other devices at the time.[^27]

### Early emoji sets (1980s–1990s)

The first emoji sets – character sets or font families with an array of different modern emoji – are a matter of contention due to differing definitions and poor early documentation.[^26] [^6] It was previously widely considered that DoCoMo had the first emoji set in 1999, but an [Emojipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emojipedia "Emojipedia") blog article in 2019 brought [SoftBank's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SoftBank_Group "SoftBank Group") earlier 1997 set to light.[^26] More recently, in 2024, earlier emoji sets were uncovered on portable devices by [Sharp Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sharp_Corporation "Sharp Corporation") and [NEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NEC "NEC") [^28] in the early 1990s, with the 1988 Sharp PA-8500 harboring what can be defined as the earliest known emoji set that reflects emoji keyboards today.[^29] [^6]

SoftBank's 1997 set was launched by the company's [J-Phone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J-Phone "J-Phone") arm on the SkyWalker DP-211SW. The 90 emoji on this device were 12 by 12 pixels and [monochrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monochrome "Monochrome"), depicting numbers, sports, the time, [moon phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon_phases "Moon phases"), and the weather. It contained the [Pile of Poo emoji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pile_of_Poo_emoji "Pile of Poo emoji") in particular.[^26] The J-Phone model experienced low sales, and the emoji set was thus rarely used.[^30]

The more well-known DoCoMo emoji set was created by [Shigetaka Kurita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shigetaka_Kurita "Shigetaka Kurita") in 1999 for [NTT DoCoMo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NTT_DoCoMo "NTT DoCoMo") 's [i-mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I-mode "I-mode"), used on its mobile platform.[^31] [^32] [^33] They were intended to help facilitate electronic communication and to serve as a distinguishing feature from other services.[^9] According to interviews, he took inspiration from iconographic conventions in Japanese [manga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manga "Manga"), as well as [Chinese characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_characters "Chinese characters") and street sign and weather pictograms.[^33] [^34] [^35] The DoCoMo i-Mode set included facial expressions, such as smiley faces, derived from a Japanese visual style commonly found in manga and [anime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anime "Anime"), combined with *[kaomoji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaomoji "Kaomoji")* and smiley elements.[^36] Kurita's work is displayed in the [Museum of Modern Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art "Museum of Modern Art") in [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City").[^37]

Kurita's 176 emoji were brightly colored, albeit with a single color per [grapheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapheme "Grapheme"). General-use emoji, such as sports, actions, and weather, can readily be traced back to Kurita's emoji set.[^38] The yellow-faced emoji in current use evolved from other emoticon sets and cannot be traced back to Kurita's work.[^38] Elsewhere in the 1990s, [Nokia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia "Nokia") phones began including preset pictograms in its text messaging app, which they defined as "smileys and symbols".[^39] A third notable emoji set was introduced by Japanese mobile phone brand [au by KDDI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Au_by_KDDI "Au by KDDI").[^26] [^40]

### Growth of emoji sets (2000–2007)

The basic 12-by-12-pixel emoji in Japan grew in popularity across various platforms over the next decade. While emoji adoption was high in Japan during this time, the competitors failed to collaborate to create a uniform set of emoji to be used across all platforms in the country.[^41]

The [Universal Coded Character Set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Coded_Character_Set "Universal Coded Character Set") ([Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode")), controlled by the [Unicode Consortium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Consortium "Unicode Consortium") and [ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_JTC_1/SC_2 "ISO/IEC JTC 1/SC 2"), had already been established as the international standard for text representation ([ISO/IEC 10646](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_10646 "ISO/IEC 10646")) since 1993, although variants of [Shift JIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_JIS "Shift JIS") remained relatively common in Japan. Unicode included several characters which would subsequently be classified as emoji, including some from North American or Western European sources such as [DOS code page 437](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_page_437 "Code page 437"), [ITC Zapf Dingbats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ITC_Zapf_Dingbats "ITC Zapf Dingbats"), or the [WordPerfect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WordPerfect "WordPerfect") Iconic Symbols set.[^42] [^43] Unicode coverage of written characters was extended several times by new editions during the 2000s, with little interest in incorporating the Japanese cellular emoji sets (deemed out of scope),[^44] although symbol characters which would subsequently be classified as emoji continued to be added. For example, Unicode 4.0 contained 16 new emoji, which included direction arrows, a warning triangle, and an eject button.[^45] Besides Zapf Dingbats, other [dingbat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dingbat "Dingbat") fonts such as Wingdings or Webdings also included additional pictographic symbols in their own custom pi font encodings; unlike Zapf Dingbats, however, many of these would not be available as Unicode emoji until 2014.[^46]

Nicolas Loufrani applied to the [US Copyright Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Copyright_Office "United States Copyright Office") in 1999 to register the 471 smileys that he created.[^47] Soon after he created The Smiley Dictionary, which not only hosted the largest number of smileys at the time, it also categorized them.[^48] The desktop platform was aimed at allowing people to insert smileys as text when sending emails and writing on a [desktop computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_computer "Desktop computer").[^49] By 2003, it had grown to 887 smileys and 640 ascii emotions.[^50]

The smiley toolbar offered a variety of symbols and smileys and was used on platforms such as [MSN Messenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MSN_Messenger "MSN Messenger").[^51] [Nokia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia "Nokia"), then one of the largest global telecom companies, was still referring to today's emoji sets as [smileys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smiley "Smiley") in 2001.[^52] The digital smiley movement was headed up by Nicolas Loufrani, the CEO of [The Smiley Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Smiley_Company "The Smiley Company").[^49] He created a smiley toolbar, which was available at smileydictionary.com during the early 2000s to be sent as emoji.[^53] Over the next two years, The Smiley Dictionary became the plug-in of choice for forums and [online instant messaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant_messaging "Instant messaging") platforms. There were competitors, but The Smiley Dictionary was the most popular. Platforms such as [MSN Messenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MSN_Messenger "MSN Messenger") allowed for customisation from 2001 onwards, with many users importing emoticons to use in messages as text. These emoticons would eventually go on to become the modern-day emoji. It was not until [MSN Messenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MSN_Messenger "MSN Messenger") and [BlackBerry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BlackBerry "BlackBerry") noticed the popularity of these unofficial sets and launched their own from late 2003 onwards.[^54]

### Beginnings of Unicode emoji (2007–2014)

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Texting_Emoji.jpg/250px-Texting_Emoji.jpg)

Emoji being added to a text message, 2013

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/I_Love_New_York.svg/250px-I_Love_New_York.svg.png)

An early use of the heart symbol as part of an English language sentence in the I Love New York advertising campaign of 1977

The first American company to take notice of emoji was [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google") beginning in 2007. In August 2007, a team made up of [Mark Davis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Davis_\(Unicode\) "Mark Davis (Unicode)") and his colleagues Kat Momoi and Markus Scherer began petitioning the [Unicode Technical Committee (UTC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") in an attempt to standardise the emoji.[^55] The UTC, having previously deemed emoji to be out of scope for Unicode, made the decision to broaden its scope to enable compatibility with the Japanese cellular carrier formats which were becoming more widespread.[^44] Peter Edberg and Yasuo Kida joined the collaborative effort from [Apple Inc.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.") shortly after, and their official UTC proposal came in January 2009 with 625 new emoji characters. Unicode accepted the proposal in 2010.[^55]

Pending the assignment of standard Unicode [code points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_point "Code point"), Google and Apple implemented emoji support via [Private Use Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_Use_Area "Private Use Area") schemes. Google first introduced emoji in [Gmail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gmail "Gmail") in October 2008, in collaboration with [au by KDDI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Au_by_KDDI "Au by KDDI"),[^40] and Apple introduced the first release of [Apple Color Emoji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Color_Emoji "Apple Color Emoji") to [iPhone OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPhone_OS "IPhone OS") on 21 November 2008.[^56] Initially, Apple's emoji support was implemented for holders of a SoftBank SIM card; the emoji themselves were represented using SoftBank's Private Use Area scheme and mostly resembled the SoftBank designs.[^57] Gmail emoji used their own Private Use Area scheme in a [supplementary Private Use plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_\(Unicode\)#Private_Use_Area_planes "Plane (Unicode)").[^58] [^59]

Separately, a proposal had been submitted in 2008 to add the [ARIB extended characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ARIB_STD_B24_character_set "ARIB STD B24 character set") used in broadcasting in Japan to Unicode. This included several pictographic symbols.[^60] These were added in Unicode 5.2 in 2009, a year before the cellular emoji sets were fully added; they include several characters which either also appeared amongst the cellular emoji [^58] or were subsequently classified as emoji.[^61]

After iPhone users in the United States discovered that downloading Japanese [apps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobile_app "Mobile app") allowed access to the keyboard, pressure grew to expand the availability of the emoji keyboard beyond Japan.[^62] The Emoji application for iOS, which altered the Settings app to allow access to the emoji keyboard, was created by [Josh Gare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josh_Gare "Josh Gare") in February 2010.[^63] Before the existence of Gare's Emoji app, [Apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Inc. "Apple Inc.") had intended for the emoji keyboard to only be available in [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan") in [iOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS "IOS") version 2.2.[^64]

Throughout 2009, members of the [Unicode Consortium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Consortium "Unicode Consortium") and national standardization bodies of various countries gave feedback and proposed changes to the international standardization of the emoji. The feedback from various bodies in the United States, Europe, and Japan agreed on a set of 722 emoji as the standard set. This would be released in October 2010 in Unicode 6.0.[^65] Apple made the emoji keyboard available to those outside of Japan in iOS version 5.0 in 2011.[^66] Later, Unicode 7.0 (June 2014) added the [character repertoires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_repertoire "Character repertoire") of the [Webdings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webdings "Webdings") and [Wingdings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wingdings "Wingdings") fonts to Unicode, resulting in approximately 250 more Unicode emoji.[^46]

The Unicode emoji whose [code points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_point "Code point") were assigned in 2014 or earlier are therefore taken from several sources. A single character could exist in multiple sources, and characters from a source were unified with existing characters where appropriate: for example, the "shower" weather symbol (☔️) from the ARIB source was unified with an existing umbrella with raindrops character,[^67] which had been added for [KPS 9566](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KPS_9566 "KPS 9566") compatibility.[^68] The emoji characters named "Rain" ("雨", *ame*) from all three Japanese carriers were in turn unified with the ARIB character.[^58] However, the Unicode Consortium groups the most significant sources of emoji into four categories:[^69]

### UTS #51 and modern emoji (2015–present)

In late 2014, a Public Review Issue was created by the [Unicode Technical Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Technical_Committee "Unicode Technical Committee"), seeking feedback on a proposed Unicode Technical Report (UTR) titled " [Unicode Emoji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Emoji "Unicode Emoji") ". This was intended to improve interoperability of emoji between vendors, and define a means of supporting multiple skin tones. The feedback period closed in January 2015.[^74] Also in January 2015, the use of the [zero-width joiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-width_joiner "Zero-width joiner") to indicate that a sequence of emoji could be shown as a single equivalent grapheme (analogous to a [ligature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligature_\(writing\) "Ligature (writing)")) as a means of implementing emoji without atomic code points, such as varied compositions of families, was discussed within the "emoji ad-hoc committee".[^75]

Unicode 8.0 (June 2015) added another 41 emoji, including articles of sports equipment such as the cricket bat, food items such as the [taco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taco "Taco"), new facial expressions, and symbols for places of worship, as well as five characters (crab, scorpion, lion face, bow and arrow, amphora) to improve support for pictorial rather than symbolic representations of the signs of the [Zodiac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiac "Zodiac").[^2] [^77]

Also in June 2015, the first approved version ("Emoji 1.0") of the Unicode Emoji report was published as Unicode Technical Report #51 (UTR #51). This introduced the mechanism of skin tone indicators, the first official recommendations about which Unicode characters were to be considered emoji, and the first official recommendations about which characters were to be displayed in an emoji font in the absence of a [variation selector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variation_selector "Variation selector"), and listed the zero-width joiner sequences for families and couples that were implemented by existing vendors.[^78] Maintenance of UTR #51, taking emoji requests, and creating proposals for emoji characters and emoji mechanisms was made the responsibility of the Unicode Emoji Subcommittee (ESC), operating as a subcommittee of the Unicode Technical Committee.[^79] [^80]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Emoji_Minesweeper.png/250px-Emoji_Minesweeper.png)

An online version of Minesweeper using emoji

With the release of version 5.0 in May 2017 alongside Unicode 10.0, UTR #51 was redesignated a Unicode Technical Standard (UTS #51), making it an independent specification.[^81] As of July 2017, there were 2,666 Unicode emoji listed.[^82] The next version of UTS #51 (published in May 2018) skipped to the version number Emoji 11.0 so as to synchronise its major version number with the corresponding version of the Unicode Standard.[^83] The popularity of emoji has caused pressure from vendors and international markets to add additional designs into the Unicode standard to meet the demands of different cultures. Some characters now defined as emoji are inherited from a variety of pre-Unicode messenger systems not only used in Japan, including [Yahoo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahoo_Messenger "Yahoo Messenger") and [MSN Messenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MSN_Messenger "MSN Messenger").[^84] Corporate demand for emoji standardization has placed pressures on the Unicode Consortium, with some members complaining that it had overtaken the group's traditional focus on standardizing characters used for minority languages and transcribing historical records.[^85] Conversely, the Consortium thought that public desire for emoji support has put pressure on vendors to improve their Unicode support,[^86] which is especially true for characters outside the [Basic Multilingual Plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Multilingual_Plane "Basic Multilingual Plane"),[^87] thus leading to better support for Unicode's historic and minority scripts in deployed software.[^86]

In 2022, the Unicode Consortium decided to stop accepting proposals for flag emoji, citing low use of the category and that adding new flags "creates exclusivity at the expense of others".[^88] [^89] The Consortium stated that new flag emoji would still be added when their country becomes part of the [ISO 3166-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_3166-1 "ISO 3166-1") standard, with no proposal needed.[^88] [^89]

### Cultural influence

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Twemoji_1f602.svg/60px-Twemoji_1f602.svg.png) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Noto_Emoji_Oreo_1f602.svg/60px-Noto_Emoji_Oreo_1f602.svg.png) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Fxemoji_u1F602.svg/60px-Fxemoji_u1F602.svg.png)

Color illustrations of U+1F602 😂 [FACE WITH TEARS OF JOY](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_with_Tears_of_Joy_emoji "Face with Tears of Joy emoji") from [Twemoji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twemoji "Twemoji"), [Noto Emoji Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noto_Emoji_Project "Noto Emoji Project") and [Firefox OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firefox_OS "Firefox OS")

[Oxford Dictionaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_Dictionaries_\(website\) "Oxford Dictionaries (website)") named U+1F602 😂 [FACE WITH TEARS OF JOY](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%F0%9F%98%82 "😂") [^90] its 2015 [Word of the Year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_of_the_year#Oxford "Word of the year").[^91] Oxford noted that 2015 had seen a sizable increase in the use of the word "emoji" and recognized its impact on popular culture.[^91] Oxford Dictionaries President Caspar Grathwohl expressed that "traditional alphabet scripts have been struggling to meet the rapid-fire, visually focused demands of 21st Century communication. It's not surprising that a pictographic script like emoji has stepped in to fill those gaps — it's flexible, immediate, and infuses tone beautifully." [^92] [SwiftKey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SwiftKey "SwiftKey") found that "Face with Tears of Joy" was the most popular emoji across the world.[^93] The [American Dialect Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Dialect_Society "American Dialect Society") declared U+1F346 🍆 AUBERGINE to be the "Most Notable Emoji" of 2015 in their Word of the Year vote.[^94] A 2015 report found U+1F4A9 💩 [PILE OF POO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%F0%9F%92%A9 "💩") was most popular in Canada among users of a proprietary keyboard application.[^95]

## Linguistic function of emoji

Linguistically, emoji are used to indicate emotional state; they tend to be used more in positive communication. Some researchers believe emoji can be used for [visual rhetoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_rhetoric "Visual rhetoric"). Emoji can be used to set emotional tone in messages. Emoji tend not to have their own meaning but act as a [paralanguage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paralanguage "Paralanguage"), adding meaning to text. Emoji can add clarity and credibility to text.[^125]

[Sociolinguistically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociolinguistics "Sociolinguistics"), the use of emoji differs depending on speaker and setting. Women use emoji more than men. Men use a wider variety of emoji. Women are more likely to use emoji in public communication than in private communication. [Extraversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraversion_and_introversion "Extraversion and introversion") and [agreeableness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agreeableness "Agreeableness") are positively correlated with emoji use; [neuroticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuroticism "Neuroticism") is negatively correlated. Emoji use differs between cultures: studies in terms of [Hofstede's cultural dimensions theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hofstede%27s_cultural_dimensions_theory "Hofstede's cultural dimensions theory") found that cultures with high power distance and tolerance to indulgence used more negative emoji, while those with high uncertainty avoidance, individualism, and long-term orientation use more positive emoji.[^125] A 6-country [user experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/User_experience "User experience") study showed that emoji-based scales (specifically the usage of [smileys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smiley "Smiley")) may ease the challenges related to [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation "Translation") and implementation for brief cross-cultural surveys.[^126]

As emoji act as a paralanguage this causes a unique pattern to be seen in the bigrams, trigrams, and quadrigrams of emoji. A study conducted by Gretchen McCulloch and Lauren Gawne showed that the most common bigrams, trigrams, and quadrigrams of emoji are those that repeat the same emoji.[^127] Unlike other languages emoji frequently are repeated one after another, while in languages, such as English, it is rare to see words repeated after one another.[^127] An example of this is that a common bigram for emoji is two crying laughing emoji. Rather than being a repeated word or phrase the use of emoji after one another typically represents emphasis of the displayed emoji's meaning instead.[^127] So, one crying laughing emoji means something is funny, two represent it's really funny, three might represent it's incredibly funny, and so forth.

## Emoji communication problems

Research has shown that emoji are often misunderstood. In some cases, this misunderstanding is related to how the actual emoji design is interpreted by the viewer;[^128] in other cases, the emoji that was sent is not shown in the same way on the receiving side.[^129]

The first issue relates to the cultural or contextual interpretation of the emoji. When the author picks an emoji, they think about it in a certain way, but the same character may not trigger the same thoughts in the mind of the receiver.[^130] For example, people in China have developed a system for using emoji subversively so that a smiley face could be sent to convey a despising, mocking, and obnoxious attitude, as the [orbicularis oculi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbicularis_oculi "Orbicularis oculi") (the muscle near that upper eye corner) on the face of the emoji does not move, and the [orbicularis oris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbicularis_oris "Orbicularis oris") (the one near the mouth) tightens, which is believed to be a sign of suppressing a smile.[^131]

The second problem relates to encodings. When an author of a message picks an emoji from a list, it is normally encoded in a non-graphical manner during the transmission, and if the author and the reader do not use the same software or operating system for their devices, the reader's device may visualize the same emoji in a different way. As an example, in April 2020, British actress and presenter [Jameela Jamil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jameela_Jamil "Jameela Jamil") posted a tweet from her iPhone using U+1F92D 🤭 SMILING FACE WITH SMILING EYES AND HAND COVERING MOUTH as part of a comment on people shopping for food during the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic"). On Apple's [iOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS "IOS"), the emoji expression was neutral and pensive, but on other platforms the emoji shows as a giggling face. Some fans thought that she was mocking poor people, but this was not her intended meaning.[^132]

Researchers from the German Studies Institute at [Ruhr-Universität Bochum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruhr_University_Bochum "Ruhr University Bochum") found that most people can easily understand an emoji when it replaces a word directly – like an icon for a rose instead of the word 'rose' – yet it takes people about 50 percent longer to comprehend the emoji.[^133]

### Variation and ambiguity

Emoji characters vary slightly between platforms within the limits in meaning defined by the Unicode specification, as companies have tried to provide artistic presentations of ideas and objects.[^134] For example, following an Apple tradition, the calendar emoji on Apple products always shows July 17, the date in 2002 Apple announced its [iCal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ICal "ICal") calendar application for [macOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacOS "MacOS"). This led some Apple product users to initially nickname July 17 " [World Emoji Day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Emoji_Day "World Emoji Day") ".[^135] Other emoji fonts show different dates or do not show a specific one.[^136]

Some Apple emoji are very similar to the SoftBank standard, since SoftBank was the first Japanese network on which the iPhone launched. For example, U+1F483 💃 [DANCER](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%F0%9F%92%83 "💃") is female on Apple and SoftBank standards but male or gender-neutral on others.[^137]

Journalists have noted that the ambiguity of emoji has allowed them to take on culture-specific meanings not present in the original graphemes. For example, U+1F485 💅 [NAIL POLISH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%F0%9F%92%85 "💅") has been described as being used in English-language communities to signify "non-caring fabulousness" [^138] and "anything from shutting [haters](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/hater "wikt:hater") down to a sense of accomplishment".[^139] [^140] Unicode manuals sometimes provide notes on auxiliary meanings of an object to guide designers on how emoji may be used, for example noting that some users may expect U+1F4BA 💺 SEAT to stand for "a reserved or ticketed seat, as for an airplane, train, or theater".[^141]

## Emoji implementation

### Early implementation in Japan

Various, often incompatible, character encoding schemes were developed by the different mobile providers in Japan for their own emoji sets.[^58] [^72] For example, the extended [Shift JIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_JIS "Shift JIS") representation F797 is used for a [convenience store](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convenience_store "Convenience store") (🏪) by SoftBank, but for a [wristwatch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wristwatch "Wristwatch") (⌚️) by KDDI.[^72] [^58] All three vendors also developed schemes for encoding their emoji in the Unicode [Private Use Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_Use_Area "Private Use Area"): DoCoMo, for example, used the range U+E63E through U+E757.[^58] Versions of [iOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS "IOS") prior to 5.1 encoded emoji in the SoftBank private use area.[^159] [^160]

### Unicode support considerations

Most, but not all, emoji are included in the [Supplementary Multilingual Plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_Multilingual_Plane "Supplementary Multilingual Plane") (SMP) of Unicode, which is also used for ancient scripts, some modern scripts such as [Adlam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adlam_\(Unicode_block\) "Adlam (Unicode block)") or [Osage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osage_\(Unicode_block\) "Osage (Unicode block)"), and special-use characters such as [Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_Alphanumeric_Symbols "Mathematical Alphanumeric Symbols").[^161] Some systems introduced prior to the advent of Unicode emoji were only designed to support characters in the [Basic Multilingual Plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_Multilingual_Plane "Basic Multilingual Plane") (BMP) on the assumption that non-BMP characters would rarely be encountered,[^87] although failure to properly handle characters outside of the BMP precludes Unicode compliance.[^162]

The introduction of Unicode emoji created an incentive for vendors to improve their support for non-BMP characters.[^87] The Unicode Consortium notes that "\[b\]ecause of the demand for emoji, many implementations have upgraded their Unicode support substantially", also helping support minority languages that use those features.[^86]

### Color support

Any operating system that supports adding additional fonts to the system can add an emoji-supporting font. However, inclusion of colorful emoji in existing font formats requires dedicated support for color [glyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyphs "Glyphs"). Not all operating systems have support for color fonts, so, emoji might have to be rendered as black-and-white line art or not at all. There are four different formats used for multi-color glyphs in an [SFNT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SFNT "SFNT") font,[^163] not all of which are necessarily supported by a given operating system library or software package such as a web browser or graphical program.[^164]

### Implementation by different platforms and vendors

Apple first introduced emoji to their desktop operating system with the release of [OS X 10.7 Lion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mac_OS_X_Lion "Mac OS X Lion"), in 2011. Users can view emoji characters sent through email and messaging applications, which are commonly shared by mobile users, as well as any other application. Users can create emoji symbols using the "Characters" special input panel from almost any native application by selecting the "Edit" menu and pulling down to "Special Characters", or by the key combination ⌘ Command + ⌥ Option + T. The emoji keyboard was first available in Japan with the release of [iPhone OS version 2.2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPhone_OS_2 "IPhone OS 2") in 2008.[^165] The emoji keyboard was not officially made available outside of Japan until [iOS version 5.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IOS_5 "IOS 5").[^166] From iPhone OS 2.2 through to iOS 4.3.5 (2011), those outside Japan could access the keyboard but had to use a third-party app to enable it. Apple has revealed that the U+1F602 😂 FACE WITH TEARS OF JOY is the most popular emoji among English-speaking Americans. On second place is the red heart emoji, followed by U+1F62D 😭 LOUDLY CRYING FACE.[^167]

An update for [Windows 7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_7 "Windows 7") and [Windows Server 2008 R2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_Server_2008_R2 "Windows Server 2008 R2") brought a subset of the monochrome Unicode set to those operating systems as part of the *[Segoe UI Symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Segoe "Segoe")* font.[^168] As of [Windows 8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_8 "Windows 8") Preview, the *Segoe UI Emoji* font is included, which supplies full-color pictographs. The plain Segoe UI font lacks emoji characters, whereas Segoe UI Symbol and Segoe UI Emoji include them. Emoji characters can be accessed through the onscreen keyboard's 😀 key. The emoji panel shortcut ⊞ Win +. was added in [Windows 10 version 1803](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_10_version_1803 "Windows 10 version 1803").

In 2016, [Firefox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firefox "Firefox") 50 added in-browser emoji rendering for platforms lacking in native support.[^169]

[Facebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook "Facebook") and [Twitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twitter "Twitter") replace all Unicode emoji used on their websites with their own custom graphics. Prior to October 2017, Facebook had different sets for the main site and for its [Messenger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook_Messenger "Facebook Messenger") service, where only the former provides complete coverage. Messenger now uses Apple emoji on iOS, and the main Facebook set elsewhere.[^170] [Facebook reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facebook_like_button#Use_on_Facebook "Facebook like button") are only partially compatible with standard emoji.[^171]

## Modifiers

### Emoji versus text presentation

Unicode defines [variation sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variant_form_\(Unicode\) "Variant form (Unicode)") for many of its emoji to indicate their desired presentation.

> Emoji characters can have two main kinds of presentation:
> 
> - an *emoji presentation*, with colorful and perhaps whimsical shapes, even animated
> - a *text presentation*, such as black & white

— Unicode Technical Report #51: Unicode Emoji [^69]

Specifying the desired presentation is done by following the base emoji with either U+FE0E VARIATION SELECTOR-15 (VS15) for text or U+FE0F VARIATION SELECTOR-16 (VS16) for emoji-style.[^172] As of version 17.0 (2025), Unicode defines presentation sequences for 371 characters.[^173] However, the [Unicode Technical Committee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Technical_Committee "Unicode Technical Committee") has since determined that unifying colourful emoji characters with textual symbols and dingbats was a "mistake", and resolved to allocate new [code points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_point "Code point") rather than defining new presentation sequences.[^174]

### Skin color

Five symbol modifier characters were added with Unicode 8.0 to provide a range of skin tones for human emoji. These modifiers are U+1F3FB–U+1F3FF. They are based on the [Fitzpatrick scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitzpatrick_scale "Fitzpatrick scale") for classifying human skin color. Human emoji that are not followed by one of these five modifiers should be displayed in a generic, non-realistic skin tone, such as bright yellow (■), blue (■), or gray (■).[^69] Human emoji that are followed directly by these characters should take on their skin color,[^175] as shown in the following table displaying skin tone variations of U+1F9D1 🧑 ADULT:

### Joining

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Zero_Width_%28Non%29Joiner.svg/500px-Zero_Width_%28Non%29Joiner.svg.png)

Behaviour of the ZWJ and ZWNJ format controls with various types of character, including emoji

Implementations may use a [zero-width joiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-width_joiner "Zero-width joiner") (ZWJ) between multiple emoji to make them behave like a single, unique emoji character.[^69] For example, the sequence U+1F468 👨 MAN, U+200D ZERO WIDTH JOINER, U+1F469 👩 WOMAN, U+200D ZERO WIDTH JOINER, U+1F467 👧 GIRL (👨👩👧) could be displayed as a single emoji depicting a family with a man, a woman, and a girl if the implementation supports it. Systems that do not support it would ignore the ZWJs, displaying only the three base emoji in order (👨👩👧).

Unicode previously maintained a catalog of emoji ZWJ sequences that were supported on at least one commonly available platform. The consortium has since switched to documenting sequences that are *recommended for general interchange* (RGI). These are clusters that emoji fonts are expected to include as part of the standard.[^178]

The ZWJ has also been used to implement platform-specific emoji. For example, in 2016, Microsoft released a series of Ninja Cat emoji for their [Windows 10 Anniversary Update](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows_10_Anniversary_Update "Windows 10 Anniversary Update"). The sequence U+1F431 🐱 CAT FACE, U+200D ZERO WIDTH JOINER, U+1F464 👤 BUST IN SILHOUETTE was used to create Ninja Cat (🐱👤).[^3] [^179] Ninja Cat and variants were removed in late 2021's *Fluent* emoji redesign.[^180]

## In Unicode

Unicode 17.0 specifies a total of 3,953 emoji using 1,438 characters spread across 24 blocks, of which 26 are [Regional indicator symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regional_indicator_symbol "Regional indicator symbol") that combine in pairs to form flag emoji, and 12 (#, \* and 0–9) are base characters for [keycap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keycap "Keycap") emoji sequences.[^181] [^177] [^69]

- 33 code points in the [Dingbats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dingbat#Unicode "Dingbat") block are considered emoji.
- All of the code points in the [Emoticons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emoticons_\(Unicode_block\) "Emoticons (Unicode block)") block are considered emoji.
- 83 code points in the [Miscellaneous Symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Symbols "Miscellaneous Symbols") block are considered emoji.
- 637 code points in the [Miscellaneous Symbols and Pictographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Symbols_and_Pictographs "Miscellaneous Symbols and Pictographs") block are considered emoji.
- 242 code points in the [Supplemental Symbols and Pictographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplemental_Symbols_and_Pictographs "Supplemental Symbols and Pictographs") block are considered emoji.
- All of the code points in the [Symbols and Pictographs Extended-A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbols_and_Pictographs_Extended-A "Symbols and Pictographs Extended-A") block are considered emoji.
- 106 code points in the [Transport and Map Symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transpo***REMOVED***and_Map_Symbols "Transport and Map Symbols") block are considered emoji.

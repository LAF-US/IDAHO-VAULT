## Caldwell’s mayor issues rare municipal vetoes on quiet zone. It might not be the end

![](https://boisedev.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/caldwell-residents-launch-effort-to-recall-mayor-several-city-council-members-IMG-9574-Large.jpeg)

Caldwell City Hall. Photo: Erin Banks Rusby/BoiseDev

The mayor of Caldwell appears to have used a little-known power to pump the brakes on plans for Caldwell’s new railroad quiet zone.

Caldwell Mayor Eric Phillips vetoed two resolutions approved 5-1 by the city council on March 25 during a special meeting that authorized the city to move forward with the next steps of implementing a quiet zone, or area where trains passing through the city would not have to honk their horns. KTVB first reported the story early Thursday morning.

“City Council may accept the veto or override it with a majority vote at the next city council meeting on April 6,” a statement from the city released Thursday morning said.

According to Caldwell’s city code, [the mayor has the power to veto or sign any law passed by council; however, the council can override the veto with a two-thirds vote](https://codelibrary.amlegal.com/codes/caldwellid/latest/caldwell_id/0-0-0-11581). The code mirrors Idaho law, which states that mayors in Idaho have the power to [veto “any ordinance” passed by council provided that the council be allowed to override the veto with a 50% plus one vote](https://legislature.idaho.gov/statutesrules/idstat/title50/t50ch6/sect50-611/).

Caldwell staff and leadership have explored the possibility of adding a quiet zone for years, with the city approving the cost of needed improvements [prior to its implementation in its budget for the current fiscal year](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/08/25/caldwell-passes-budget-despite-animal-shelter-railroad-funding-concerns/), and [receiving bids for those improvements in late 2025](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/01/22/caldwell-planning-public-workshop-on-railroad-quiet-zone-with-date-to-be-determined/).

In early 2026, following Phillips being sworn in as mayor, the city held two workshops to take public comments about the quiet zone, during which many people spoke in support and against the change, [as BoiseDev reported](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/05/caldwell-grapples-with-quiet-zones-adding-pedestrian-safety-measures/). At the March 25 meeting, Council Member Diana Register cast the sole opposition vote to both measures, [as BoiseDev reported.](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/26/caldwell-moves-forward-with-railroad-quite-zone-with-one-dissenting-vote/)

## What reasons did Phillips give for the vetoes?

On April 1, Phillips submitted two veto letters explaining his rationale. Phillips cited “ongoing safety concerns” as reason to veto the resolution accepting the bid for the work. He called proceeding with the work “premature” due to a lack of “data-driven, clear evidence demonstrating a net safety benefit.” He also cited a lack of immediate need for the installations, plus a lack of knowledge about “the true scale of the cost,” given the desire to solicit bids that include the cost of installing pedestrian safety measures.

The city approved $400,000 for improvements in its budget for fiscal year 2026, [as BoiseDev reported](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/08/25/caldwell-passes-budget-despite-animal-shelter-railroad-funding-concerns/). At a January meeting, Bruce Mills, Caldwell’s Deputy Public Works Director for Transportation, estimated that the cost for the basic improvements in order to have a quiet zone — medians so that cars can’t drive around crossing arms — would be $240,000, less than allocated. At a February 27 workshop, Mills estimated that the cost of adding pedestrian safety measures [would add $40,000 to the total cost](https://boisedev.com/news/2026/03/05/caldwell-grapples-with-quiet-zones-adding-pedestrian-safety-measures/).

In the second veto letter about the resolution creating the quiet zone, Phillips said he remained unconvinced that the mitigation measures built ahead of creating a quiet zone would be sufficient to protect people from the elevated risk of trains not sounding their horns, particularly at heavy vehicle and pedestrian traffic crossings. Phillips also said that he does not believe the cost of implementing and maintaining the quiet zone is justified, given the city’s current and anticipated budgetary constraints.

# Children of the Lamp Vessel States

## Thesis

In *Children of the Lamp*, a vessel is not a single condition.
It is a shared mechanism with multiple states:

- home
- resting place
- operational interior
- forced containment
- binding surface
- time-distorting enclosure

That matters because the books do not treat a lamp, bottle, suit, seal, or tablet as mere storage. They treat vessel-entry as a metaphysical state change.

## Explicit Rule Layer

The clearest general rule is that a lamp is a djinn's home and often resting place. Djinn typically enter and exit through transubstantiation, and the interior of a vessel is treated as a usable space with its own physics.

The important consequences are:

- djinn can inhabit a vessel voluntarily
- djinn can be forced into a vessel against their will
- the same enclosure can support comfort or punishment
- entry direction can affect how time is experienced
- the vessel can remain functional even when used as a prison

So the vessel is not just a container. It is an interface between being, power, and time.

## Individual Character Readings

### Mr. Rakshasas

Mr. Rakshasas is the clearest case of a djinn whose vessel has become home through long occupation and confinement. The lamp is not just where he is kept. It is where he lives, stores records, and organizes his world. His stance is closer to accommodation than rebellion.

### Liskeard

Liskeard is a bottle imp and librarian inside Mr. Rakshasas' lamp. He does not frame that role as imprisonment. He declines the offer of three wishes because he is satisfied serving in that vessel-bound position. That makes him the strongest example of a chosen vessel role.

### Nimrod

Nimrod treats vessel space practically. He uses the interior as a workable environment, not as an abstract prison. His stance is operational: the vessel can be entered, navigated, and used.

### Kublai Khan

Kublai Khan is the clearest forced-containment example in the series notes I pulled. He is transubstantiated from the golden tablet and later imprisoned in jade suits. That is not residence. It is coercive enclosure with binding authority.

### Iblis Teer

Iblis is another explicit coercive-containment case. He and Rudyard are imprisoned in jade suits. The enclosure here is punitive and political.

## The Main Distinction

The useful split is:

- **chosen vessel** = home, home base, resting place, operational interior
- **forced vessel** = prison, binding surface, punishment, control layer

The books make clear that the same physical form can move between those states depending on authority and entry conditions.

## Time and Direction

The transubstantiation rule adds an additional layer: vessel entry can alter time experience.

The direction of entry matters in the northern hemisphere, and wrong-way entry can invert the time effect. The important point for the vault is not the exact geometry alone. It is that **entry protocol changes temporal behavior**.

That gives the vessel a second function:

- not only containment
- but also time-skewed state

## Vault Translation

This maps cleanly to the vault's persona and runtime chambers.

If a dotfolder or named chamber is treated as a vessel, then it can have at least five states:

1. reserved identity shell
2. active inhabited runtime
3. quarantined or locked state
4. promoted canonical record
5. time-skewed or stale state

That is the implementation lesson:

- a chamber can be welcoming or coercive
- the same shell can hold doctrine, runtime, or tool state
- entry/exit rules matter
- authority determines whether the vessel is home or prison

## Bottom Line

The series is explicit that vessels are not neutral containers. They are legal states of being.

That is the useful abstraction for the vault:

> one vessel, many states

If the engine cannot distinguish voluntary inhabitation from forced containment, it is missing the core rule the literature is already teaching.

Plans for an expanded middle school in McCall can now move forward nearly five years after voters approved debt financing for the project.

Last week the McCall City Council unanimously voted to approve plans that would expand Payette Lakes Middle School by about 6,800 square feet. Work is scheduled to begin next Monday and be mostly complete by next school year, according to McCall-Donnelly School District Superintendent Tim Thomas.

The city’s approval does not require the school district to immediately fund any off-campus street improvements or dedicate land for a future extension of Deinhard Lane to the east — both of which were sticking points in previous expansion plans that were approved by the city, but withdrawn by the district in 2022.

“A Deinhard extension would be a regulatory taking,” Council Member Julie Thrower said before voting to approve the application last Thursday. “It’s way out of proportion to what this project is doing and trying to accomplish, so I’m glad that is not part of the conditions of approval this time around.”

The new expansion plans avoided the Deinhard extension requirement by scaling back the expansion by about 5,000 square feet and no longer seeking an increase to the permitted 850-student capacity of the campus, which includes Barbara Morgan Elementary School.

Thomas told the city council that the additional student capacity will not be needed for more than 20 years, based on enrollment projections. The campus at 111 N. Samson Trail currently serves 752 students, with 730 students projected for next school year.

“We are not growing,” he said during a Feb. 26 public hearing on the application, adding that the enrollment estimates are tied to state funding. “It’s not made-up numbers. I have to be right, or we’re in trouble.”

However, the district is committing nearly $400,000 to future improvements at the intersection of Samson Trail and Deinhard Lane, which could include a stoplight, per city planning documents.

“We will set aside that money and wait for the time when the city wants us to contribute our part of it and help build it,” Thomas said. “But at this time, we believe that that’s a different project. That’s not this project.”

## How will the middle school be expanded?

Plans approved by the city call for three new classrooms and a new set of restrooms to be added to the middle school in a 4,900-square-foot addition on the south side of the building.

The multi-purpose room, located on the north side of building, would also be expanded by about 1,900 square feet, under the plans.

While the scaled back expansion proposal does not increase the overall capacity of the campus, it would add room for 75 more middle school students, bringing the capacity of the school to 400 students. Previous district administration believed the current capacity of the middle school to be 400 students, but Thomas, who took over as superintendent in 2025, [told Valley Lookout that number was inaccurate.](https://valleylookout.com/2025/07/10/mccall-donnelly-submits-scaled-back-plan-for-middle-school-expansion/)

Jake Olson, the middle school’s principal, said space shortages in recent years have forced some classes to be taught in undersized classrooms that can only accommodate eight students at a time.

Meanwhile, Olson said the multi-purpose room expansion would enable the school to increase classroom time by consolidating lunch schedules and bring back elective courses that have been lost due to space constraints, like drama and guitar.

“The delay has impacted kids, and the delay is impacting kids right now,” he said. “It’s important to know that.”

The cost of the expansion is estimated at about $5.5 million, which is what remains from a $33 million bond district voters approved in 2021 to fund school improvements.

Some work at the school has already been completed using the bond money, including parking lot improvements, installing new lockers, and remodeling the music room, office, and main entrance.

The bond also included about $15 million to renovate and expand Donnelly Elementary School. That work was completed in 2023. Another $10 million was used to refinance a previous bond passed by district voters.

## Traffic circulation improvements

The expansion project also includes work to improve traffic circulation within the 40-acre campus.

The main road into the campus would be straightened out, bicycle paths would be added to the campus, and land around the eastern part of the middle school would be leveled for usable outdoor space, according to plans.

Parents, school board members, and school staff told council members during the Feb. 26 hearing that the changes would help reduce existing traffic hazards on the campus.

“It is truly the hardest and most stressful part of my day,” Val Berg, the elementary school principal, said of the morning arrival and afternoon departure of the school’s students.

At the same time, Berg and others said a future extension of Deinhard Lane through the campus would risk the safety of students who walk or ride bicycles to school – a group that Berg said makes up more than 100 elementary school students.

“I believe that plan would jeopardize the safety of my kids going to school,” McCall resident Bob Krahn said of the Deinhard extension.

## Deinhard Lane extension background

The Deinhard Lane extension is recommended by the [Southeast McCall Buildout Transportation Plan](https://www.mccall.id.us/DocumentCenter/View/1030/Resolution-23-30-Adoption-of-SE-McCall-Buildout-Transportation-Recommendations-Memorandum-PDF), which was adopted by the city council in 2023.

The extension would divert traffic from neighborhood roads in the area, like Stockton Drive, Woodlands Drive, and Sheila Lane, according to the plan, which also recommends adding a stoplight to the intersection of Samson Trail and Deinhard Lane.

The original permit to build the middle school in 1994 required the district to provide half of the 70-foot easement for a future Deinhard extension.

In 2004, however, the McCall Area Planning and Zoning Commission waived a requirement for the Fox Ridge Subdivision, which borders the school campus to the south, to provide the other 35 feet.

City engineers urged the easement to be a condition of approval for the subdivision, but the school district spoke in opposition to it, citing concerns about traffic and safety.

The previous middle school expansion approved by the city required the district to dedicate the full 70-foot right-of-way, or about 2.9 acres, for the Deinhard extension. The approval also allowed the district to instead complete a traffic study and fund recommended roadway improvements in southeastern McCall, but that too was unappealing to district officials.

The original expansion project sought to increase the capacity of the middle school by 125 students by adding 12,000 square feet of new space.

## Proponents: Deinhard extension a ‘unique opportunity’

While most who spoke at the Feb. 26 public hearing were opposed to the extension, others warned it would be “short-sighted” to not pursue it. Among them was Donnelly developer Craig Groves, who said continued growth in the Treasure Valley will “pour over” into Valley County, specifically citing [$50 billion being invested into a Micron plant in Boise](https://boisedev.com/news/2025/07/03/micron-boise-50-billion/).

“That capital investment is going to pour over into Valley County,” Groves said. “I want the school to expand, but I don’t want them to look at planning in the rearview mirror.”

Groves also said he disagreed with the school district’s enrollment projections, citing more than 2,000 housing units entitled in Donnelly, which is part of the district, as well as other residential development in McCall.

Groves’ sentiments were echoed by McCall resident Pat Allen, who said the Deinhard extension “makes sense” for managing traffic and future growth in southeast McCall.

“You guys are sitting on a time bomb, and we have a unique opportunity right now, with the Woodlands 3, to make this thing right,” Allen said.

Woodlands 3 is a development proposed by Groves east of the school campus, [as Valley Lookout reported.](https://valleylookout.com/2026/03/04/right-location-right-time-pine-creek-ranch-developer-airs-scaled-back-plan/) The proposal is a scaled back version of [Groves’ Pine Creek Ranch proposal](https://valleylookout.com/2025/10/16/no-deal-sewer-district-rejects-pine-creek-ranch-annexation-request/) that called for more than 600 homes on land east of the schools.

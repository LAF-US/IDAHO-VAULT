## Micron hopes to expand campus, adding large berms and electric yard near homes. Boise didn’t follow its typical process

![](https://boisedev.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/micron-hopes-to-expand-campus-adding-large-berms-and-electic-substation-near-homes-boise-didnt-follow-its-typical-process-Screenshot-2026-04-0.jpeg)

![ICCU](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/748dce9e-e5ec-4fa6-9c85-f6cbe0db4a73.png)

Boise-based Micron Technology hopes to add even more land to its campus, including adding a new electric yard near homes and putting up 40-foot-tall berms.

Micron applied with the City of Boise to annex more than 238 acres spread across three properties it owns on the Columbia Bench. The annexed area would bring the Micron campus to within a few feet of the Painted Ridge subdivision along Columbia Rd.

“The project, generally located south of E. Columbia Rd and east of S. Federal Way, includes development of support buildings and power facilities to support Micron’s campus expansion project/new fabrication facility for semiconductor memory manufacturing,” Micron wrote in a letter to neighbors in the area.

Micron is currently at work on a $50 billion expansion of its Boise campus, with at least two new fabrication facilities to expand the production of memory chips.

In the last decade, a series of subdivisions popped up along Columbia Rd., in an area far from other homes. About 160 homes in the Painted Ridge, Sunny Ridge, and Bonneville Pointe neighborhoods are connected to the rest of Boise by a single access down Columbia Rd. to Federal Way. [As BoiseDev reported in 2019](https://boisedev.com/news/2019/08/14/deep-dive-where-will-boise-grow-ownership-geography-affordability-drive-future-plans/), the City of Boise hopes to see more housing development in the area, but much of the land is owned by the J.R. Simplot Co., which has not moved forward with any development plans for its property.

Immediately behind the homes, Micron says it plans to build a 40-foot-tall earth berm, topped with an eight-foot-tall fence. The berms would stretch for more than a mile on three sides of Micron’s property.

In one area of the annexed land, Micron says it plans a 40-acre electrical yard, with a series of twelve transformers, two substation yards, and medium-and high-voltage buildings.

Micron held a neighborhood meeting in March, attended by more than two dozen neighbors. According to Micron’s summary of the meeting, neighbors expressed concern over the electrical yard and the berm. For the yard itself, Micron said neighbors were worried about its location, possible noise, and fire danger. For the berm, neighbors gave feedback on the location of fencing as well as where the berm would end.

Micron said it would not make any changes to the application based on neighbor feedback.

## Atypical process

![](https://boisedev.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/micron-hopes-to-expand-campus-adding-large-berms-and-electic-substation-near-homes-boise-didnt-follow-its-typical-process-Screenshot-2026-04-0-1.png)

The City of Boise’s planning process is not followed for all applications, it says. Via City of Boise

BoiseDev first learned about the application from neighbors in March. Our reporting indicated the city followed an atypical process for Micron’s application.

The city typically requires, and notes on its website, that applicants must first file online and then have a meeting with city officials about their concept. The city said it can take two to three weeks for a response from the time the planning record is generated.

But in this case, Micron quietly had a meeting with planning officials — and then filed online.

![tok commercial 2:1](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/1ff51757-dd76-432e-9972-14e69faf18c1.gif)

tok commercial 2:1

When the city revamped its zoning ordinances, it added language to the city code that required what it calls a “concept review meeting” to happen for any planning project before it can move forward. On March 3rd, the city posted online that “No concept review (was) performed due to time constraints per management,” and that “No concept review (was) held.”

When BoiseDev inquired with the city, and noted that a concept review meeting is required by law, the city said a concept review meeting was, in fact, held before the permit application was filed. The city then uploaded a new document on March 24th, detailing a meeting in February with Micron representatives.

The city provided an email from Deborah Nelson of Givens Pursley requesting a meeting regarding annexation on behalf of Micron. In the emails provided, the city did not inform Nelson of its typical process requiring a so-called “PLN” be created on the city’s website. A PLN record provides public scrutiny to projects proposed or in process. In the emails provided, the city said it would work to accelerate providing neighbor names so that Micron could try to get the annexation in front of the Planning & Zoning Commission by May.

![Sundance Company](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/a95b6100-2267-485a-87f4-0eb633a38c0e.png)

Sundance Company

A city spokesperson told BoiseDev that the record stating that “no concept review was held” was due to miscommunication and a manager on vacation.

City of Boise Planning & Development Services Director Maureen Brewer said in a statement that even though its normal process wasn’t followed, it happens from time to time.

“We frequently engage in informal conversations about project ideas or potential project applications,” she wrote. “Sometimes the nature of these conversations can serve to satisfy a concept review meeting, at which point we would not unnecessarily add process by insisting another meeting be held after a (planning record) is submitted.”

![Sundance Company](https://static-v2.boisedev.com/assets/a95b6100-2267-485a-87f4-0eb633a38c0e.png)

Sundance Company

The annexation will require several sign-offs from the city in the coming months. Micron has to obtain approval to annex the land into city limits and obtain a conditional use permit for the electric substation. It will also have to modify its development agreement with the city. Brewer stresses that this will be a public process.

“…There will be public hearings at the planning and zoning commission and city council to ensure community engagement and invite feedback,” she wrote. “Nothing in the flexibility that we provide applicants during the pre-submittal steps affects the required public hearings, nor does it subvert the ability of residents to meaningfully participate in the process.”

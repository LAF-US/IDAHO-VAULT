**By Logan Finney | FāVS News Reporter**

Jewish Boiseans gathered with the broader community this weekend to celebrate the formal installation of a new rabbi and the restoration of their historic Torah scrolls.

Johanna Hershenson is the second rabbi at [Congregation Ahavath Beth Israel](https://www.cabi-boise.org/home.html). Founded in 1895, the congregation did not have a permanent rabbi until [Daniel Fink](https://favs.news/idaho-measure-honor-traditional-families/) joined in 1994.

Hershenson spent the first part of her career in California, working in and near Los Angeles, before a winding path took her to Anchorage, Alaska, back to Orange County and eventually as far as New Zealand.

There she connected with a Jewish community in Prague before landing a part-time rabbi position in Oregon, where she also pursued a master’s degree in counseling and split her time between the two careers.

“Then my counseling position offered me benefits, so I became full-time counseling. After a couple years of that, my husband noticed — before I was willing to admit it — that I really was not doing very well. I was losing interest in things that gave me joy. He felt that it was because I needed to be doing what I was made to do. And one day, I came home from work and he said, ‘Don’t get mad at me, I remembered your password, here are a few jobs I think you should apply for,’ and Boise was the most interesting among them,” Hershenson said.

The congregation formally installed Hershenson as its new permanent rabbi on Saturday.

### ‘A whole new chapter‘

Amy Russell spent her childhood in the CABI synagogue. She and her husband Matt Koob were among the many people celebrating in and around the festivities on Sunday.

“For me, Rabbi Dan is all I’ve ever known. His daughters were just a couple years younger than me, so I grew up playing with them,” Russell told FāVS News. “He was the rabbi at my bat mitzvah. His final act as being our rabbi — about a month after he retired officially — was he officiated my wedding. So much of what it means to be Jewish here in Idaho has been led by Rabbi Dan. I think that his impact is going to be ongoing.”

Russell told FāVS News that whenever she hears a non-Jewish friend talk about the amazing rabbi they heard at an event, she immediately knows they are talking about Fink.

Russell said Fink gave Jewish people in Idaho a powerful public voice, and she feels confident Hershenson is well-positioned to carry that legacy forward.

The transition feels significant on a personal level, too — Russell said she is still figuring out where Judaism fits in her adult life after growing up with Fink as her rabbi. For her, Hershenson’s installation marks the start of an entirely new chapter.

### Restoring the Torah scrolls

![torahscrolls](https://favs.news/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/torahscrolls.jpeg)

The congregation restored two Torah scrolls/Logan Finney (FāVS News)

Also visiting CABI this week was Neil Yerman, a sofer, or master, Torah scribe from New York. He worked to restore two of the congregation’s Torah scrolls, each more than a century old.

Yerman said the Torah is a text that cannot be altered — not even a single letter. He said he often illustrates this for young students by asking them to imagine changing the story to say the Israelites were slaves in France rather than Egypt. The answer, he said, is always the same: You can’t change it, because it wouldn’t be the truth.

Members of the congregation helped clean the parchment — using sponges, alcohol and sandpaper — to prepare it for Yerman’s work, which he said shortened the process greatly.

“It was so wild when he started pulling out the sandpaper, and telling us we were going to sandpaper the whole back of it,” Russell said. “The sofer goes more in depth and uses more or less this whole array of tools and erasers to really get in and do the fine little bits.”

Yerman has restored close to a thousand Torah scrolls over his career, according to CABI.

“Watching him take an eraser to the Torah I think might be, like, the scariest thing I’ve ever seen,” Russell laughed.

“I didn’t show you too much work with the scalpels then,” Yerman remarked back.

He worked on one last month at his New York studio, and the other this week in Boise.

Yerman said restoration typically begins with triage — addressing folds, tears and structural damage before moving on to sewing and reinforcing. The scrolls are sewn with sinew from the legs of a kosher animal, such as ox or goat, using a blind seam that keeps the stitching concealed inside the fold.

The careful craftsmanship is rooted in reverence, he said. Because the Torah is considered a major sanctity — the spoken word of God — its physical form must appear whole and unbroken.

Yerman said he last visited CABI to perform Torah restoration work about 15 years ago.

### Overcoming a sense of ‘otherness’

Upstairs from the library, Hershenson spoke to several groups of visitors who walked over from the social hall for scheduled ‘seated tours’ of the synagogue. She emphasized the rabbi’s role as a resource for their congregation, not an authority figure above them.

“The rabbi is a teacher. In theory, the most learned person in the community, so the community can lean on the rabbi,” Hershenson said. “I generally conduct services with volunteers who play music, and often on Saturday morning we’ll have people in the congregation come lead some of the prayers. You don’t need a rabbi to lead services.”

Hershenson answered questions about Jewish rituals and tradition, its similarities with and differences from the Christian faith, as well as Judaism’s place in the broader culture.

She said that even in parts of Europe where Jewish communities existed for five, six or seven hundred years, Jews were never fully seen as belonging to the land.

Emancipation — which allowed Jews to live alongside non-Jewish neighbors — didn’t come until the late 1700s and early 1800s in Western Europe, and even later in Eastern Europe. That means Jewish integration into broader society is a relatively recent development, she said, which helps explain why a sense of otherness has persisted.

The rabbi said while related, there is a distinction between antisemitism and anti-Judaism.

“Anti-Jewish theology has a solution. Not that I find the solution so personally wonderful, but there’s a solution: You could convert, you could accept Jesus as your savior, and then everything’s fine,” Hershenson said. “Antisemitism is more about the person. If you have Jewish heritage or lineage, you’re an other that is, like, infecting the rest of the population.”

The rabbi said that opinion of Jews is also affected by geopolitics in Israel and Palestine.

She said Israel’s ongoing occupation of territories and the unresolved question of Palestinian self-determination have fueled growing radicalization over time — though she noted that most of her Muslim friends reject that extremist rhetoric as contrary to their faith.

The broader global frustration over Palestinian rights has also given rise to a new form of antisemitism, she said, one that is difficult to name or address.

When someone claims they are criticizing Israel rather than Jews, she said, it puts American Jews in an impossible position — asked to answer for a government in which they have no vote. She called it a complicated and painful moment with no easy answers.

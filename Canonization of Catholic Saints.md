# Canonization of Catholic Saints

## AUTHOR

OpenAI Codex

## STATUS

Research artifact based on external source review completed on 2026-04-10.

## SUMMARY

Canonization in the Roman Catholic Church is a formal legal and ecclesial process. In the modern system, a cause normally moves through a local diocesan or eparchial inquiry, then through review in Rome by the Dicastery for the Causes of Saints, and finally to papal approval.

The pope is the decisive authority in the process. Under the current framework, the Church distinguishes among several major routes, including heroic virtue, martyrdom, and the newer category of offering of life. The ordinary shorthand sequence `Servant of God -> Venerable -> Blessed -> Saint` is useful, but real causes vary depending on which route is being used and whether an exceptional papal procedure applies.

## CURRENT PROCESS

### 1. Opening a cause

A cause is proposed locally, usually by a bishop, faithful community, religious institute, or other legitimate petitioner. Normally, five years must pass after death before the cause is introduced, though the pope may dispense from that waiting period.

### 2. Testing reputation

Before the cause is formally opened, the bishop is expected to determine whether the person has a genuine reputation for holiness, martyrdom, or intercessory power. Vatican guidance emphasizes that this reputation should be spontaneous rather than artificially manufactured.

### 3. Diocesan inquiry

The local inquiry gathers:

- witness testimony
- writings by and about the candidate
- historical evidence
- documentation relevant to holiness, martyrdom, or miracles

Once the cause is formally introduced, the candidate is referred to as `Servant of God`.

### 4. Roman phase

The acts are sent to Rome. The Dicastery for the Causes of Saints reviews whether the diocesan process was valid, supervises preparation of the `positio`, and submits the cause to theological and episcopal review.

### 5. Papal decrees

If the reviews are favorable, the pope may authorize decrees concerning:

- heroic virtue
- martyrdom
- offering of life
- miracles

### 6. Beatification and canonization

Beatification usually permits public veneration in a limited context, such as a diocese, region, or religious family. Canonization extends public veneration to the universal Church.

## MAIN ROUTES

### Heroic virtue

The candidate is judged to have lived the Christian virtues in a heroic manner. In the ordinary modern pattern:

- one miracle is required for beatification
- a second miracle after beatification is required for canonization

### Martyrdom

The candidate is judged to have died `in odium fidei`, in hatred of the faith. In the ordinary modern pattern:

- no miracle is required for beatification
- one miracle is required for canonization

### Offering of life

Pope Francis added this route in 2017. It applies to a person who freely and voluntarily offered life for others and accepted certain and premature death out of charity. In the ordinary modern pattern:

- one miracle is required for beatification
- another miracle is required for canonization

## MIRACLES

Miracles are investigated separately from the main life or martyrdom inquiry. In healing cases, the Vatican typically uses:

- medical review
- theological review
- review by cardinals and bishops of the Dicastery
- papal approval

The practical standard is that the event must be judged scientifically inexplicable and linked to the intercession of the candidate.

## HISTORY

The process developed over time:

- in the early Church, sainthood recognition often emerged through public acclaim
- later, local bishops became central figures in recognition
- papal canonization became more formal over time
- papal centralization deepened after early modern Roman curial reforms
- the modern process was significantly reworked in 1983
- the diocesan phase was further clarified in 2007
- the category of offering of life was added in 2017

The first documented papal canonization is commonly identified as Pope John XV's canonization of St. Ulric in 993.

## IMPORTANT DISTINCTIONS

### Beatification vs canonization

- `Beatification` allows a limited public cult
- `Canonization` extends that cult to the universal Church

### Ordinary vs exceptional routes

The ordinary route is not the only one. Vatican press material also documents exceptional papal acts such as equipollent canonization or equipollent beatification. These matter because simplified descriptions of the process often imply a single universal pattern when the real system is more varied.

## RESEARCH JUDGMENT

The Catholic canonization process is more juridical and centralized than popular shorthand usually suggests. It is not simply a matter of public admiration or a single miracle. It is a layered system of inquiry, review, and papal judgment, with different evidentiary patterns depending on whether the cause is based on heroic virtue, martyrdom, offering of life, or an exceptional papal route.

**[ISO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") /IEC 8859-1:1998**, *Information technology— [8-bit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-bit_computing "8-bit computing") single- [byte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byte "Byte") coded graphic [character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_\(computing\) "Character (computing)") sets—Part 1: Latin alphabet No. 1*, is part of the [ISO/IEC 8859](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859 "ISO/IEC 8859") series of [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII") -based standard [character encodings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_encoding "Character encoding"), first edition published in 1987. ISO/IEC 8859-1 encodes what it refers to as " **Latin alphabet no. 1** ", consisting of 191 [characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_\(computing\) "Character (computing)") from the [Latin script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_script "Latin script"). This character-encoding scheme is used throughout the [Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Americas "Americas"), [Western Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Europe "Western Europe"), [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania"), and much of [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"). It is the basis for some popular 8-bit character sets and the first two blocks of characters in [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode").

As of April 2026, 6.7% of *static* web pages are served with ISO/IEC 8859-1, and with its superset [Windows-1252](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows-1252 "Windows-1252") used for 9.3% of static pages (and even more used of some subtypes or categories, see below). This contrasts a lot with 0.9% of *all* [web sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Web_site "Web site") using ISO/IEC 8859-1 (explained by the fact most web sites are served programmatically, usually with a mix of static pages).[^5] [^6] It is the most declared single-byte character encoding, but as Web browsers and the [HTML5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML5 "HTML5") standard [^7] interpret them as the superset [Windows-1252](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows-1252 "Windows-1252"), these documents may include characters from that set. Some countries or languages show a higher usage than the global average, in 2025 Brazil according to website use, use is at 2.2%,[^8] and in Germany at 2.1%.[^9] [^10]

ISO-8859-1 (shortened alias) was (according to the standard, at least) the default encoding of documents delivered via [HTTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTTP "HTTP") with a [MIME type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Media_type "Media type") beginning with `text/`, the default encoding of the values of certain descriptive HTTP headers, and defined the repertoire of characters allowed in [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML "HTML") 3.2 documents. It is specified by many other standards. In practice, the superset encoding Windows-1252 is the more likely effective default and it is increasingly common for [UTF-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTF-8 "UTF-8") to work whether or not a standard specifies it.

**ISO-8859-1** is the [Internet Assigned Numbers Authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority "Internet Assigned Numbers Authority") (IANA) preferred name for this standard when supplemented with the [C0 and C1 control codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C0_and_C1_control_codes "C0 and C1 control codes") from [ISO/IEC 6429](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_6429 "ISO/IEC 6429"). The following other aliases are registered: **iso-ir-100**, **csISOLatin1**, **latin1**, **l1**, **IBM819**, **Code page 28591** a.k.a. **Windows-28591** is used for it in Windows.[^11] IBM calls it **code page 819** or **CP819** (**[CCSID](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CCSID "CCSID") 819**).[^12] [^13] [^14] [^15] [Oracle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oracle_Database "Oracle Database") calls it **WE8ISO8859P1**.[^16]

HTML5 is the latest standard that almost all web pages use, but regarding older standards: 14.0% of HTML Transitional web pages use ISO-8859-1 [^17] (HTML Transitional is used by 2.9% of all the websites [^18]). HTML Strict has 17.0% usage [^19] (note though HTML Strict is only used by 0.1% of websites [^20]). For the obsolete and little used [frameset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frameset_\(HTML\) "Frameset (HTML)") site element using websites (0.2% of all), 24.7% of those use ISO 8859-1.[^21] So it can be said that legacy encodings are more used with older technology, and HTML5 mostly eliminate their use; and pre-HTML5-based HTML Transitional and HTML Strict.

## Coverage

The encoding has sufficient coverage for *letters* of many languages. Like all the ISO-8859 encodings and [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII") it lacks typographical symbols such as [en dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/En_dash "En dash") and [em dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_dash "Em dash"), curly quotation marks, and the Euro symbol, which are needed for attractive and correct typography.

The slot corresponding to its uppercase form is occupied by the lowercase letter *[ß](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%9F "ß")* from the German language, which did not have an [uppercase form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_%E1%BA%9E "Capital ẞ") at the time when the standard was created.

### Quotation marks

Typographical (6- or 9-shaped) [quotation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark#Summary_table "Quotation mark") are missing, as are any baseline quotation marks used by some of the supported languages. Only `« »`, `" "`, and `' '` are included. Some fonts will display the spacing grave accent (0x60) and the apostrophe (0x27) as a matching pair of oriented single quotation marks (see [Quotation mark § Typewriters and early computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark#Typewriters_and_early_computers "Quotation mark")), but this is not considered part of the modern standard.

### Superscript digits

Only three superscript digits are encoded: ² at 0xB2, ³ at 0xB3, and ¹ at 0xB9, lacking the superscript digit 0 and digits 4–9. Additionally, no subscript digits are encoded. A workaround is to use rich text formatting for the digits not covered by this standard.

### Euro sign

The [euro sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euro_sign "Euro sign") was first presented to the public on 12 December 1996.[^22] Due to this character set being introduced in 1987, it does not include the euro sign. Later character sets similar to ISO/IEC 8859-1 include a euro sign, such as [Windows-1252](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows-1252 "Windows-1252") and [ISO/IEC 8859-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-15 "ISO/IEC 8859-15").

## History

ISO 8859-1 was based on the [Multinational Character Set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_Character_Set "Multinational Character Set") (MCS) used by [Digital Equipment Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation "Digital Equipment Corporation") (DEC) in the popular [VT220](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VT220 "VT220") terminal in 1983. It was developed within the [European Computer Manufacturers Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecma_International "Ecma International") (ECMA), and published in March 1985 as [ECMA-94](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECMA-94 "ECMA-94"),[^23] by which name it is still sometimes known. The second edition of ECMA-94 (June 1986) [^24] also included [ISO 8859-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-2 "ISO/IEC 8859-2"), [ISO 8859-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-3 "ISO/IEC 8859-3"), and [ISO 8859-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-4 "ISO/IEC 8859-4") as part of the specification.

The original draft of ISO 8859-1 placed French *Œ* and *œ* at code points 215 (0xD7) and 247 (0xF7), as in the MCS. However, the delegate from France, being neither a linguist nor a typographer, falsely stated that these are not independent French letters on their own, but mere [ligatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthographic_ligature "Orthographic ligature") (like ﬁ or ﬂ), supported by the delegate team from [Bull Publishing Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bull_Publishing_Company&action=edit&redlink=1 "Bull Publishing Company (page does not exist)"), who regularly did not print French with Œ/œ in their house style at the time. An anglophone delegate from Canada insisted on retaining Œ/œ but was rebuffed by the French delegate and the team from Bull. These code points were soon filled with × and ÷ under the suggestion of the German delegation. Support for French was further reduced when it was again falsely stated that the letter ÿ is "not French", resulting in the absence of the capital Ÿ. In fact, the letter ÿ is found in a number of French proper names, and the capital letter has been used in dictionaries and encyclopedias.[^25] These characters were added to [ISO/IEC 8859-15:1999](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-15#1999 "ISO/IEC 8859-15"). [BraSCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BraSCII "BraSCII") matches the original draft.

In 1985, [Commodore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodore_International "Commodore International") adopted ECMA-94 for its new [AmigaOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AmigaOS "AmigaOS") operating system.[^26] The Seikosha MP-1300AI impact dot-matrix printer, used with the Amiga 1000, included this encoding.

In 1990, the first version of [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") used the code points of ISO-8859-1 as the first 256 Unicode code points.

In 1992, the [IANA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Assigned_Numbers_Authority "Internet Assigned Numbers Authority") registered the character map **ISO\_8859-1:1987**, more commonly known by its preferred [MIME](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIME "MIME") name of **ISO-8859-1** (note the extra hyphen over ISO 8859-1), a superset of ISO 8859-1, for use on the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet"). This map assigns the [C0 and C1 control codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C0_and_C1_control_codes "C0 and C1 control codes") to the unassigned code values thus provides for 256 characters via every possible 8-bit value.

## Similar character sets

### ISO/IEC 8859-15

[ISO/IEC 8859-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-15 "ISO/IEC 8859-15") was developed in 1999, as an update of ISO/IEC 8859-1. It provides some characters for French and Finnish text and the [euro sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euro_sign "Euro sign"), which are missing from ISO/IEC 8859-1. This required the removal of some infrequently used characters from ISO/IEC 8859-1, including fraction symbols and letter-free diacritics: `¤`, `¦`, `¨`, `´`, `¸`, `¼`, `½`, and `¾`. Ironically, three of the newly added characters (`Œ`, `œ`, and `Ÿ`) had already been present in [DEC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DEC_\(company\) "DEC (company)") 's 1983 [Multinational Character Set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multinational_Character_Set "Multinational Character Set") (MCS), the predecessor to ISO/IEC 8859-1 (1987). Since their original code points were now reused for other purposes, the characters had to be reintroduced under different, less logical code points.

ISO-IR-204, a more minor modification (called **code page 61235** by FreeDOS),[^27] had been registered in 1998, altering ISO-8859-1 by replacing the [universal currency sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_currency_sign "Universal currency sign") (¤) with the euro sign [^28] (the same substitution made by ISO-8859-15).

### Windows-1252

The popular [Windows-1252](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows-1252 "Windows-1252") character set adds all the missing characters provided by [ISO/IEC 8859-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-15 "ISO/IEC 8859-15"), plus a number of typographic symbols, by replacing the rarely used C1 controls in the range 128 to 159 ([hex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexadecimal "Hexadecimal") 80 to 9F). It is very common for Windows-1252 text to be mislabelled as ISO-8859-1. A common result was that all the quotes and apostrophes (produced by "smart quotes" in word-processing software) were replaced with question marks or boxes on non-Windows operating systems, making text difficult to read. Many Web browsers and e-mail clients will interpret ISO-8859-1 control codes as Windows-1252 characters, and that behavior was later standardized in [HTML5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML5 "HTML5").[^29]

### Mac Roman

The [Apple Macintosh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple_Macintosh "Apple Macintosh") computer introduced a character encoding called [Mac Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mac_Roman "Mac Roman") in 1984. It was meant to be suitable for Western European [desktop publishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_publishing "Desktop publishing"). It is a superset of ASCII, and has most of the characters that are in ISO-8859-1 and all the extra characters from Windows-1252, but in a totally different arrangement. The few printable characters that are in ISO/IEC 8859-1, but not in this set, are often a source of trouble when editing text on Web sites using older Macintosh browsers, including the last version of [Internet Explorer for Mac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet_Explorer_for_Mac "Internet Explorer for Mac").

### Other

[DOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DOS "DOS") has [code page 850](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_page_850 "Code page 850"), which has all printable characters that ISO-8859-1 has, albeit in a totally different arrangement, plus the most widely used [graphic characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphic_character "Graphic character") from [code page 437](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_page_437 "Code page 437").

Between 1989 [^30] and 2015, [Hewlett-Packard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hewlett-Packard "Hewlett-Packard") used another superset of ISO-8859-1 on many of their calculators. [This proprietary character set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RPL_character_set "RPL character set") was sometimes referred to simply as "ECMA-94" as well.[^30] HP also has [code page 1053](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_page_1053 "Code page 1053"), which adds the medium shade (▒, U+2592) at 0x7F.[^31]

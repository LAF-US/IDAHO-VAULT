## Overview

The phrase "there are unknown unknowns" was made famous by U.S. Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld at a 2002 Department of Defense press briefing about Iraq and weapons of mass destruction. It describes a four-quadrant epistemological framework now called the **Rumsfeld Matrix**.

## The Four Quadrants

| | **Known** | **Unknown** |
|---|---|---|
| **Known** | Things you know you know | Things you know you don't know |
| **Unknown** | Things you don't know you know | Things you don't know you don't know |

1. **Known Knowns** — facts or variables we are aware of and understand. Actionable.
2. **Known Unknowns** — risks or gaps we are aware of but have not resolved. Can be researched.
3. **Unknown Knowns** — tacit knowledge, blind spots, or assumptions so ingrained we don't realize we're making them. The quadrant Rumsfeld famously omitted — critics noted this was the quadrant where denial and willful ignorance live.
4. **Unknown Unknowns** — factors we are not aware of and cannot predict. The highest-risk quadrant; they catch us off guard and cannot be planned for directly.

## Origin

Rumsfeld's statement was made on February 12, 2002, during a DoD news briefing:

> "There are known knowns; there are things we know we know. We also know there are known unknowns; that is to say we know there are some things we do not know. But there are also unknown unknowns — the ones we don't know we don't know."

The framework predates Rumsfeld — it draws on the **Johari Window** (Joseph Luft and Harrington Ingham, 1955), which uses a similar 2×2 grid to model self-knowledge and interpersonal awareness.

## Applications

Originally defense/intelligence. Now widely used in:
- Business risk assessment
- Project management
- Personal epistemology
- Scientific uncertainty modeling
- AI system design and agent reasoning

On March 1, 2024, JFAC voted to insert intent language blocking the ITD Board's planned $51.7M sale of the State Street campus to a private buyer, generating a lawsuit, a gubernatorial rebuke, and an unresolved constitutional question about JFAC's authority to override executive-branch statutory power via appropriations language.

## What Happened

The ITD Board voted to sell its State Street campus in Boise to a consortium of Hawkins Companies, Pacific Companies, and FJM for $51.7M. JFAC, at its March 1, 2024 public vote, inserted intent language revoking ITD's surplus property sale authority under statute. The language was developed in working groups before the public vote.

Governor Little allowed the resulting bill (HB 770) to take effect without signature, objecting publicly that intent language should not override a statutory grant of authority to the executive branch.

## Lawsuits and Litigation

- **Hawkins Companies v. Moyle (Docket 51788-2024):** Filed April 2024. Argued the intent language was an unconstitutional "shadow amendment" of general law. Idaho Supreme Court dismissed August 2024 on standing — Justice Zahn wrote that ITD and Dept. of Administration would likely have had standing but did not sue. Constitutional question expressly left open.
- Mike Moyle intervened with private counsel (Hilty, Bower, Haws & Seable).
- Attorney General Labrador argued moot; Little's private attorney conceded budgets may be unconstitutional.

## Bybee Incident (March 2024)

Keith Bybee unilaterally changed intent language in the Chinden Campus budget bill after JFAC voted, without co-chairs' knowledge. House passed the changed version. Bybee invoked Rule 9(b)(ii) ministerial-correction authority. Wendy Horman said she was unaware and would have brought any change back to the full committee.

## Aftermath

ITD is now pursuing hazardous materials abatement and remodel at costs far exceeding the original $32.5M estimate (Clark Corbin, Idaho Capital Sun, November 2025). Jim Jones cited the ITD decision in his April 2025 column as an example of JFAC using intent language to override an executive-branch lawful decision, costing the state millions.

improvements, nicer design, and exciting new features. But eventually every product being continually developed will become <a href="https://www.removepaywall.com/search?url=https://www.wired.com/2004/06/word-refuseniks-never-upgrade">good enough</a> for its target market.</p> <p>When that happens, companies (especially the <a href="https://pketh.org/organic-software.html">VC-backed</a> kind) now need to contrive new and increasingly stick-like reasons for customers to pay again. From made-for-marketing <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oXtvAQ-e0iE">‘features’</a> that suck to use IRL, to breaking compatibility with older versions, to spamming you with marketing notifications and embedded ads. Their relationship with customers goes from symbiotic to parasitic.</p> <p>On the other hand, the pursuit of completeness should be an ideal fit for subscription software because, theoretically, steady monthly payments from long-time customers incentivizes stability, polish, and helpful improvements.</p> <p>Subscriptions are particularly helpful for businesses:</p> <ol> <li>Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) makes it <em>way</em> easier for a business to hire employees because they know they’ll be able to pay their yearly salaries.</li> <li>Historical MRR can be used to project the rate at which future revenue will go up 📈, which could be used to take on some debt to finance expansion or R&amp;D as safely as possible.</li> </ol> <p>Especially for solo creators, just knowing that you have ~<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$3000/mo</code> coming in every month is way less anxiety-inducing than making <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$6,000</code> in a good month – but knowing that next month you could just as likely make <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$1000</code>, or maybe even <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$0</code>. It’s like being a farmer, surviving at the mercy of good weather.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/vegetables.gif" class="" alt="a vegetable basket in a garden gently waving in the wind" /> <figcaption> <span> What do you grow, when the future could be anything” </span> </figcaption> </figure> <blockquote> <p>There’s also a third reason: Tier or usage-based pricing prevents customers costing more money than they contribute. But unless you’re burning unlimited money generating LLM tokens, the risk of losing money on a web app customer is either basically zero, or very easy to predict otherwise (even over the long-term).</p> </blockquote> <p>On paper, subscriptions could be a win-win. But some people, including many readers of this blog, have good reasons to be wary of them.</p> <h1 id="subscription-woes">Subscription Woes</h1> <p>Kinopio subscriptions can be easily cancelled, pricing is grandfathered, receipts are emailed, accidental renewals can be refunded, and there are no cancellation fees or <a href="https://pudding.cool/2023/05/dark-patterns/">deceptive dark patterns</a>. Many indie subscription apps also offer subscriptions in good faith.</p> <p>But a business model that’s regularly exploited by well-known corporations, like the New York Times and Adobe, poisons the well for everyone else. Every time someone gets tricked into signing up for a non-refundable yearly subscription instead of a monthly one, or has to call to cancel a subscription they created online, those bad feelings become associated with all subscriptions.</p> <p>It’s hard to have good long-lasting relationships with customers if they’re expecting the worst.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/kermit.webp" class="large" alt="kermit the frog standing alone in front of a row of wild west era gunmen" /></p> <figure> <figcaption> <span> <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CVO0fRpens8">(source)</a> No one wins in a shootout between a company and its customers. </span> </figcaption> </figure> <p>That being said, I still think subscriptions make sense to offer.</p> <p>For most people, subscriptions are as normal as the old boxed software model used to be. And the much lower-cost to entry can be very helpful – especially in competitive markets where customers have a lot of options.</p> <p>The onus is on companies selling subscriptions to earn trust by being transparent about who they are, what they believe, and how they’re funded (i.e. what their long-term incentives are). A public <a href="https://kinopio.club/roadmap">roadmap</a> and <a href="https://kinopio.club/changelog">changelog</a> also go a long-way towards proving they practice what they preach.</p> <p>Even to non-technical people, paying for subscriptions makes intuitive sense when the product requires a server for helpful things like file uploads, public URLs, and real-time collaboration. But the per-user-cost of running a production <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Create,\_read,\_update\_and\_delete">CRUD</a> server is basically zero. Counter-intuitively, what you’re really paying for is the considerable effort and expertise that it takes to keep full-featured software running reliably and continuously-improving.</p> <p>That’s part of the reason why I increased prices over the years as the app grew in capability and complexity.</p> <h1 id="how-i-priced-kinopio">How I Priced Kinopio</h1> <p>For context, here’s Kinopio’s pricing history:</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">Year</th> <th style="text-align: left">Prices</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2019</td> <td style="text-align: left">$0</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2020</td> <td style="text-align: left">$4/mo</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2021</td> <td style="text-align: left">$5/mo</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2022</td> <td style="text-align: left">$6/mo, $60/yr, $200/life</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2025</td> <td style="text-align: left">$8/mo, $80/yr, $250/life</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p>When I introduced paid upgrades to Kinopio in 2020, I did it as a monthly subscription because that seemed like the safe, default model for an app that needed a server.</p> <p>In all my previous jobs, there was always a dedicated back-end or sysadmin team that abstracted the server hosting details from the rest of the company. They used complicated sounding terms like <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">load balancing</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">replica shards</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">containers</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">observability</code>, were woken up by <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">pagerDuty</code>, and were eternally worried about scaling.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/mainframe.jpg" alt="standing woman adjusting tape reel in vintage mainframe computers" /></p> <p>But when I dived into that world, I learned that most of the jargon really didn’t matter. The kinopio-server is a simple node.js app with a pretty straightforward <a href="https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Glossary/MVC">MVC architecture</a>. My insecurity about keeping servers up is a big reason behind why the app works offline, or if the server is down. What developers would call <a href="https://www.localfirst.fm/8">local-first</a> today.</p> <blockquote> <img src="/images/2019/kinopio-cat.png" width="120" class="no-shadow" alt="" /> <p> In 2019, I wrote about my early <a href="https://pketh.org/kinopio-plans.html">Plans for Kinopio</a>. Where I mentioned that, </p> <p> “Kinopio consists of two codebases, the <code>client-app</code> that runs when in the browser and the <code>server-app</code> which enables sharing and collaboration. The client is like a cockroach, its tiny, basically free to host, and doesn’t depend on a connection to the server. So if I can’t afford to run the Kinopio server anymore, I can just switch it off and you can continue to use Kinopio as you do now – with all your data intact.” </p> </blockquote> <p>Two years later, I introduced the lifetime plan mainly because people kept asking me for a subscription alternative. I’m sure many of them would have still have paid for a subscription, but how long would a rueful customer stay? Would they always be looking for alternatives? Would they still enthusiastically share spaces and recommend the app to their friends and teams?</p> <p>My business ‘strategy’ is that a happy customer is better than an unhappy one, and that I’d rather have <em>some</em> money from a user than none. (Relatedly, that’s also why Kinopio offers student/financial needs discounts).</p> <p>The lifetime plan is priced at ~3× the yearly price because that was the average churn length at the time. But I think that in general, 3-4 years is a good starting point if you get a nice sounding number that is high enough so that only &lt;10% of new customers choose it, while stilling feeling anchored to the subscription prices.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/c7.png" class=" no-shadow" alt="" /> <figcaption> <span> Recurring revenue grows slow and steady over time. Single purchases (lifetime plans, but also donations and merch) are highly variable and treated as supplementary revenue. </span> </figcaption> </figure> <h1 id="financial-engineering-for-good">Financial Engineering, for Good</h1> <p>Software designers and engineers regularly build products with original ideas, and choose to make their own jobs harder by increasing code complexity, so that their users can have an inviting, easier-to-use, and lower-friction, time using it.</p> <p>But on the financial-side, web-based software companies tend to be really conservative with their business models and choose subscription-only models by default. It’s never even questioned, but I think it should be. Just like with dev work, pricing models can also take on behind-the-scenes complexity to give users a better experience.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/beep-boop.gif" class="" alt="" /></p> <p>Low-variability/high-tail income (subscription MRR) is easiest to plan for, but high-variability/short-tail income (lifetime purchases) is peaky and harder to plan for – but they can work together. Apple, Adobe, Microsoft, etc. became household names because software was hard to make, and even harder to box up and get on store shelves.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/human-tools/mac-ps.jpeg" class="large" alt="classic mac with photoshop" /></p> <p>But by the time version 10~ or so releases, the product includes basically every feature possible. It’s all things to all people.</p> <p>So how do you make version 11 compelling? The answer turned out to be <em>Lol who cares now</em>. Once you have monopoly market share, the product (and the people that made it) are largely irrelevant. What now keeps the almighty profit line going up and to the right 📈 is sales and marketing – and predictably the relationship between user and tool metamorphoses from symbiotic to parasitic.</p> <p>The old monopolies aren’t going anywhere. But a new era of tools designed for a different fate are sprouting up out of old, analogue ideas like creativity, purposefulness, human-made craftsmanship.</p> <blockquote class="row"> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/pizza.gif" class="no-shadow pizza" width="100" alt="pizza gif" /> <p> Domino’s – the Microsoft of pizza – also isn’t going anywhere. But for those who know, and those who care, there’s always a way better neighborhood spot. </p> </blockquote> <h2 id="chasing-good-friction">Chasing Good Friction</h2> <p>I spend a lot of time thinking about tools for thinking and creativity, and how they’ve changed. If you’re not used to drawing or writing – and even if you are – the scariest thing in the world is a blank page.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/blank-pages.webp" class="" alt="paper notebook open to blank page" /> <figcaption> “Eeep” </figcaption> </figure> <p>The first sentence, or the first brush stroke, is a high anxiety situation. Full of potential, full of unknown pain.</p> <p>So to help with the blank slate problem, software tools added Templates. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">File → New</code> turned into <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">File → New → Do You Want to Make a Portfolio? Or a Résumé?</code>. This more recently turned into <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">File → New → Ask AI to Do It</code>. These features help some people, but also create new problems.</p> <p>These tools can generate almost effortless outputs. They unburden us from the abrasion of creativity and their generic-by-design results keep us cocooned from the risks of standing out. No one wants to make ‘ugly’ art, or a ‘bad’ website. But struggling and making mistakes is an important part of developing skills and – increasingly important – finding your own voice.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/omw.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="‘omw to get a lil treat’ badge" /> <figcaption> <a href="https://tombinghamillustration.com/shop/treatcap">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> <p>We don’t talk enough about the many-years-long rite of passage that every creator goes through.</p> <p>Everyone can draw or write, but only the smallest sliver will decide that they want to earnestly make things to share with other people.</p> <p>At whatever age it grips you though, the urge to create pulls you into a world of frustration, where your skills don’t yet match your taste and everything you try feels not good enough. Unfortunately, the first step to being good at something is to suck at it.</p> <p>But the expedition is especially dispiriting when your tools constantly wink at you to generate ‘perfect’ results instantly.</p> <p>Why did I spend two+ painful weeks writing a blog post?</p> <h2 id="for-the-journey">For the Journey</h2> <p>I don’t write very often, so I’m not sure if my pace is glacial or merely tortoise-like. Then again Tolkien took <a href="https://middle-earth.xenite.org/how-long-did-it-take-j-r-r-tolkien-to-write-the-lord-of-the-rings/">12+ years</a> to write The Lord of the Rings.</p> <p>Speaking for myself, even after I plan things out, the shape of an essay (sometimes even my whole argument) changes as I write.</p> <p>I type with two <a href="https://ia.net/writer">IA Writer</a> text editor windows open. One for the post, and one where I throw rejected or tangential paragraphs and ideas that don’t quite fit. So far, each file contains ~2000 words… implying that I throw away ~half of what I type.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/human-tools/david-lynch-teaches-typing-game.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="david lynch teaches typing game" /></p> <figure> <figcaption> <a href="https://rhinostew.itch.io/david-lynch-teaches-typing">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> <p>From a short-term-results point of view, this process may seem inefficient, painful, and somewhat savage. I wouldn’t really disagree – but, like working out, or eating well, the sweat and tears are worth it in the long-term because I solidify my ideas with every new post I write.</p> <p>Even after all that work, it’s never as good as I want to be – especially considering the effort. I’m still always a little bit embarrassed to share my work, because it’s never ‘perfect’.</p> <p>But it’s better than perfect, it’s <strong>imperfect</strong>.</p> <p>Struggling is an important part of developing skills and finding your own voice, and having an audience that’s watched your work evolve over years is a slept on privilege.</p> <h2 id="the-human-tools-era">The Human-Tools Era</h2> <p>When you’re excited about a new hammer, everything looks like a nail. As the world gets noisier, glossier, and more fake, the more valuable the human-touch becomes in both the real world and on the real web.</p> <p>LLMs are the new normal. “Easy to use” became “let me tell you what to write and how to draw”, “here’s what you’ll watch next”, and “here’s what you believe”.</p> <p>But there is always be a market for understandable, engaging, and fun-to-use tools.</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">What Most People Use</th> <th style="text-align: left">What Enthusiasts Use</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Phone camera</td> <td style="text-align: left">Dedicated camera</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Whatever pen is nearby</td> <td style="text-align: left">Fountain pen</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">The keyboard that came with your computer</td> <td style="text-align: left">Mechanical Keyboard</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Coors Light™</td> <td style="text-align: left">Craft brew</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Smart watch</td> <td style="text-align: left">Non-smart watch</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Fast fashion</td> <td style="text-align: left">hand-made knitwear</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/field-quilt.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="field quilt" /> <figcaption> <a href="https://www.are.na/block/9117165">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> <p>In your heart, you probably can’t be an enthusiast for everything. <title>Why Sell Lifetime Plans, in a Default Subscription World?</title>

<description><p>There’s never been just one way to buy software. In ye olden times, if you liked some shareware, you’d mail off a cheque, or call up a hot-line, to get the full version. Later on, you could walk into a store and walk out with a shiny box, manuals, and disks.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/ms-windows-box-2.webp" class="" alt="microsoft windows 1.0 box" /> <figcaption> <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windows\_1.0">(source)</a> <span> MS Windows 1.0 </span> </figcaption> </figure> <p>These were some expensive boxes:</p> <table> <thead> improvements, nicer design, and exciting new features. From made-for-marketing <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oXtvAQ-e0iE">‘features’</a> that suck to use IRL, to breaking compatibility with older versions, to spamming you with marketing notifications and embedded ads. Their relationship with customers goes from symbiotic to parasitic.</p> <p>On the other hand, the pursuit of completeness should be an ideal fit for subscription software because, theoretically, steady monthly payments from long-time customers incentivizes stability, polish, and helpful improvements.</p> <p>Subscriptions are particularly helpful for businesses:</p> <ol> <li>Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) makes it <em>way</em> easier for a business to hire employees because they know they’ll be able to pay their yearly salaries.</li> <li>Historical MRR can be used to project the rate at which future revenue will go up 📈, which could be used to take on some debt to finance expansion or R&amp;D as safely as possible.</li> </ol> <p>Especially for solo creators, just knowing that you have ~<code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$3000/mo</code> coming in every month is way less anxiety-inducing than making <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$6,000</code> in a good month – but knowing that next month you could just as likely make <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$1000</code>, or maybe even <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$0</code>. But some people, including many readers of this blog, have good reasons to be wary of them.</p> <h1 id="subscription-woes">Subscription Woes</h1> <p>Kinopio subscriptions can be easily cancelled, pricing is grandfathered, receipts are emailed, accidental renewals can be refunded, and there are no cancellation fees or <a href="https://pudding.cool/2023/05/dark-patterns/">deceptive dark patterns</a>. Many indie subscription apps also offer subscriptions in good faith.</p> <p>But a business model that’s regularly exploited by well-known corporations, like the New York Times and Adobe, poisons the well for everyone else. Every time someone gets tricked into signing up for a non-refundable yearly subscription instead of a monthly one, or has to call to cancel a subscription they created online, those bad feelings become associated with all subscriptions.</p> <p>It’s hard to have good long-lasting relationships with customers if they’re expecting the worst.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/kermit.webp" class="large" alt="kermit the frog standing alone in front of a row of wild west era gunmen" /></p> <figure> <figcaption> <span> <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CVO0fRpens8">(source)</a> No one wins in a shootout between a company and its customers. </span> </figcaption> </figure> <p>That being said, I still think subscriptions make sense to offer.</p> <p>For most people, subscriptions are as normal as the old boxed software model used to be. But the per-user-cost of running a production <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Create,\_read,\_update\_and\_delete">CRUD</a> server is basically zero. Counter-intuitively, what you’re really paying for is the considerable effort and expertise that it takes to keep full-featured software running reliably and continuously-improving.</p> <p>That’s part of the reason why I increased prices over the years as the app grew in capability and complexity.</p> <h1 id="how-i-priced-kinopio">How I Priced Kinopio</h1> <p>For context, here’s Kinopio’s pricing history:</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">Year</th> <th style="text-align: left">Prices</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2019</td> <td style="text-align: left">$0</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2020</td> <td style="text-align: left">$4/mo</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2021</td> <td style="text-align: left">$5/mo</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2022</td> <td style="text-align: left">$6/mo, $60/yr, $200/life</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2025</td> <td style="text-align: left">$8/mo, $80/yr, $250/life</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p>When I introduced paid upgrades to Kinopio in 2020, I did it as a monthly subscription because that seemed like the safe, default model for an app that needed a server.</p> <p>In all my previous jobs, there was always a dedicated back-end or sysadmin team that abstracted the server hosting details from the rest of the company. They used complicated sounding terms like <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">load balancing</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">replica shards</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">containers</code>, <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">observability</code>, were woken up by <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">pagerDuty</code>, and were eternally worried about scaling.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/lifetime-plans/mainframe.jpg" alt="standing woman adjusting tape reel in vintage mainframe computers" /></p> <p>But when I dived into that world, I learned that most of the jargon really didn’t matter. My insecurity about keeping servers up is a big reason behind why the app works offline, or if the server is down. What developers would call <a href="https://www.localfirst.fm/8">local-first</a> today.</p> <blockquote> <img src="/images/2019/kinopio-cat.png" width="120" class="no-shadow" alt="" /> <p> In 2019, I wrote about my early <a href="https://pketh.org/kinopio-plans.html">Plans for Kinopio</a>. Would they still enthusiastically share spaces and recommend the app to their friends and teams?</p> <p>My business ‘strategy’ is that a happy customer is better than an unhappy one, and that I’d rather have <em>some</em> money from a user than none. <title>Dialing Up the Internet Phonebook</title>

<description><p>You might’ve heard of the <a href="https://internetphonebook.net/">Internet Phone Book</a>, a collection of poetic, interesting, personal websites, and essays about websites, collected by Kristopher and Elliott. We chatted about how the phonebook was made, and about why they’re optimistic about the web that’s made by real people and artists.</p> <p>TLDR: Make a website, it’s good for your soul.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/internet-phonebook/1.webp" class="" alt="dropping off a phonebook into a local little library box" /> <figcaption> <span> Because <a href="https://kinopio.club">Kinopio</a> sponsored the first printing of the phone book, I was sent three extra copies. I wasn’t sure what to do with them though. When I asked what I should do on social media, there was no shortage of people asking me to mail them a copy. But the more I thought about it, the more I felt like I should share the book with people who aren’t already part of ‘the scene’. </span> <br /><br /> <span> Right now, I’m living in Charlotte, NC. Beautiful weather, but not a place you’ll find many indie web enthusiasts. So I decided to drop them off in <a href="https://littlefreelibrary.org/">little libraries</a> around town in the hopes that maybe someone new will have their eyes opened to the world of websites outside corporate control. </span> </figcaption> </figure> <table> <thead> <tr> <th class="td-avatar-col"></th> <th>Name</th> <th>Info</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td class="td-avatar-col"><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span></td> <td>Piri</td> <td>The proprietor of this website, and your host</td> </tr> <tr> <td class="td-avatar-col"><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span></td> <td><a href="https://www.naiveweekly.com/">Kristoffer Tjalve</a></td> <td>Author, artist, organizer of <a href="https://naiveyearly.com/">naive yearly</a></td> </tr> <tr> <td class="td-avatar-col"><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span></td> <td><a href="https://home.elliott.computer">Elliott Cost</a></td> <td>Author, artist, sees the <a href="https://html.energy/">html.energy</a> all around us</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <h2 id="about-the-poetic-web">About the Poetic Web</h2> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> You describe the book as a directory for exploring the vast “poetic web”, not the “indie web”, nor the “blogging web”. Why the term poetic web?</p> <p>Personally, I’m not sure that I’m part of the ‘poetic’ web. I don’t write poetry, but I do want the things I make to be beautiful in a way.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> <a href="https://laurelschwulst.com/">Laurel Schwulst</a> started to write that she was interested in the poetic potential for the internet. I believe this inspired <a href="https://chia.design/">Chia Amisola</a> to name the Poetic Web. Now there are more people using it. For me, it’s not that the web has to be poetry. I don’t need to identify a particular grain and substance that these sites all share. Rather I like the term because poetry is less in-your-face, less trying to be right. It’s a bit more like asking questions. A little bit more suggestive. And I find that a way of describing the internet or the web that we engage with.</p> <p>I also like that the reading of poetry requires a bit more work on the reader’s behalf. You need to kind of make up your own mind of how you interpret poems. If non-fiction leans more towards five-step guides, then poetry is more like the Oracle with arbitrary answers. I’d like that the web is something people feel agency to shape and participate in.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> So it’s more like poetry is like an art form, which websites can also be.</p> <p>The internet phone book really reminds me of the original idea of <a href="https://web.archive.org/web/20141122194515/https://dir.yahoo.com/">Yahoo</a> as a directory of websites. Hand-curated where some guy was like “oh there’s a new cooking website, better add that in”. Do you think there’s a place for a new Yahoo-style site with trusted links? Maybe as a portal-y destination for people to come back to? Like a wirecutter of websites, if you will.</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> That’s kind of our intention with this book. In many ways we couldn’t take a conventional approach, like what worked in the 90s, when the directory served primarily as a utility. The phonebook speaks to a different time and context. The web is so vast that we can’t and didn’t want to fit it all into a book. Some artists have tried to print the internet, which is interesting, but in many ways we see the phonebook as a community-building project that connects people through printed matter. Starting small, and within the context of where we’ve already been working, felt important.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> It reminds me of the <a href="https://famicase.com/25/index.html">Famicase exhibitions</a> where artists come together each year to print their own physical Nintendo™ cartridges displayed in a physical place, which brings a lot of artists together.</p> <p>I’m wondering if most of these websites are fully hand-coded. To be part of the poetic web, is there a requirement that you write the HTML yourself?</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> Not necessarily. I think it’s great when people are hand-coding their sites. There’s something beautiful about that, where you can see the human behind the site through the code or the design. But we want to be open to anyone, and we also included sites made through a web builder, which is fine as well.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> You’re hearing this from the organizer of <a href="https://html.energy/html-day/2025/">HTML Day</a>, which started as free-writing HTML on paper in parks, but I would say, to Elliott’s point, I never got an exclusive feeling, of one type being better than the others. With Elliott and Laurel’s <a href="https://html.energy">HTML energy</a> it’s just much more about putting your fingerprint on what you do, and I think you can do that with any tool. So it’s more like, can you feel that someone is actually behind this?</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Can commercial sites ever be a part of the poetic web? In the same way that you have Walmart, but you also have your neighborhood coffee shop, and they have a very different influence on the community.</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> I think it was interesting how the advertisers in the book form their own <a href="https://pketh.org/organic-software.html">organic software</a> directory. The ads are a directory of companies that are doing good for the web, respecting people’s privacy, and staying small. They have more sustainable ways of keeping their businesses alive that are less extractive. So yeah, I think there’s definitely a place for commercial sites in the directory, and I think we’ll explore that more. Like, maybe you could have a small inline ad within the directory for people who can’t necessarily afford a full-page ad.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Yeah, it’s not like Google where people, mostly bots, bid on adsense and if you win the bidding war your ad is displayed. In contrast to that, you know that to be in the internet phonebook you have to meet a criteria of non-shitty-ness – so there’s a sense of trust in the directory.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> And also we picked yellow which was originally like the commercial phone book. In many countries you would also have another phone book for residential numbers, in my birth country that was white.. But when you ask people “what color is a phone book?” They say yellow.</p> <p>Buying ads are… let’s say, optimizing towards some very large companies. I wish there were other ways of finding your customers. And I think as Elliott said, I like your term of organic software. There are a lot of these kinds of companies on the internet, and I would love to help them also establish nice customer relationships. I think those are, like, the mom-and-pop stores of the internet, right?</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Yeah, it’s one of those things where the companies that need the help don’t have the resources for it, and the big ones that don’t have all the resources for marketing. But you reached out to me, to ask if I’d be interested in sponsoring and I thought that proactive outreach was really cool and helpful.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/internet-phonebook/3.webp" class="no-shadow large" alt="1913 pixel art" /></p> <h2 id="producing-the-book">Producing the Book</h2> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Before printing the book was there a point where you were like if we don’t get <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">n</code> feedback or reassurance then we won’t do this? Because a print run is an expensive commitment.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> We emailed potential sponsors to help us front the costs so at least we wouldn’t come up with a loss. And then that was actually really positive. You and other people that we wrote to were like “yeah sure, let’s do this, it sounds fun”. And I also wrote to a few bookshops to check if they would be interested in carrying it and some very nice bookshops said yes. So then we felt like, okay, it’s not gonna be a pile of paper that no one’s gonna look at, I think we can justify printing 500 copies.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> How did you decide on 500 as a number to print? Did it just feel right, or was there some other consideration?</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> Because we wanted to do offset print, which gives a nicer look and is a nicer reading experience, the minimum order for that to make sense was 500. With printing you have these different levels, where beyond a few thousand it becomes more about the bookbinding than the printing cost because binding has different gluing techniques and the machines need different quantities. Anyways, I like doing physical stuff which has different constraints that are really fascinating.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> I’ve read a little bit about that from <a href="https://craigmod.com/">Craig Mod</a> writing about his own membership books that he prints, and yeah, it’s pretty intense.</p> <p>How successful has the phone book been? Especially compared to your past work?</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> I’d say very successful – it sold out in less than 24 hours after the <a href="https://www.are.na/editorial/paging-the-poetic-web">are.na article</a>. I think we were a bit surprised by that. We would have printed more if we’d known that people really wanted this. So yeah, it’s been surprising, but also lovely.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Sounds like the way is paved for future releases. Do you see the phonebook as a yearly periodical? You mentioned in the book itself that a lot of these URLs will be lost to time. Some domains will expire, they’ll be replaced by scam pages and whatnot, so in theory, there is effort required to keep everything up to date.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> Given the reception and how many people seem excited about it, I think we would love to keep it going and make it a thing for some time. The support has really been crazy, from the people reading, to the bookshops, to, of course, all the people featured in it. I personally find that the book hits all the things that excites us and it’s kind of easier to understand. Like if people ask me, “so what are you doing?”, and I say, “I do internet stuff”, and then they want to understand, now I can just give them this phone book and it kind of explains what it is, and we can talk through that. And it kind of exemplifies a lot of the virtues around what I’m doing, and that feels very exciting, so it would be really cool to keep it going.</p> <p>I remember <a href="https://art.teleportacia.org">Olia Lialina</a>, who also has an essay in the book, posted that the phonebook is kind of like the <a href="https://wholeearth.info/">Whole Earth catalog</a> of our time, which I think is a very sweet reference. It would be incredible to do anything remotely similar in terms of cultural importance. And I think there is something about living around or outside of the big tech company platforms that the phonebook could be a part of.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Despite the name, I noticed the book is half traditional phonebook-style listings, and also half essays about the web in general. Do you think you’ll keep that 50-50 balance in future editions?</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> Originally we planned to do this one book, and thought there would be 250 people and their sites in it. And then when we actually opened for submissions, there were like 700~. But then when we put them together in a column layout, we had maybe like 20 pages – which was not enough to look like and feel like a book. That led us to put in essays. But I’m very happy we ended up with that, because the book ended up in all these bookshops, and people have been picking it up who have no familiarity of the part of the internet we’re talking about, so the essays sort of work like a primer to that. So we definitely want to keep having essays in the future.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/internet-phonebook/2.webp" class="no-shadow large" alt="1913 pixel art" /></p> <h2 id="www-to-irl">WWW to IRL</h2> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> How do you see the present and future relationship between the digital and real world evolving?</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> Hmm… personally, I’ve been looking for what I’ve called off-ramps from the web to real-life spaces. I’ve been part of a lot of projects trying to explore this more, like HTML Energy with offline events, where people meet and write HTML together. Friends of ours, Benjamin Earl and Kirsten Spruit, who are part of <a href="https://extrapractice.space/">Extra Practice</a>, the studio I’m in, came up with the term <a href="https://hackersanddesigners.nl/coding-in-situ.html">coding in situ</a>. It’s about working on technology that is situated in a physical place and has a sense of locality. I’m not really sure what these explorations will look like in the future, but I do hope that physicality is given more consideration. Think <a href="https://thecreativeindependent.com/people/web-developer-tara-vancil-on-exploring-how-to-be-online-in-radical-ways/">websites</a> that don’t have to be “on” all the time and digital tools designed around physical, <a href="https://dynamicland.org/">local spaces</a>.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> What advice would you give someone like me, perhaps, who kind of doesn’t live in a cool city. Can they be part of the poetic web, too? It’s a little tough here because you wonder if anyone will show up.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> You can host your own HTML Day, we’ll help. We were also thinking that we would really like for the book to integrate with these local physical spaces. Like, what are the doors that you can enter in different cities around the world, where you can have conversations with people who are interested in this kind of the internet?</p> <p>So, like, I think Extra Practice in Rotterdam is such a place, because we know the people there. There are like 5 members and all of them are interested in this. You have Bird Call in Seoul, and of course, these are bigger cities, but it would be nice to integrate everyone with a sort of ‘place directory’ in the phone book as well.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> And you guys are doing a book tour, which sounds pretty rock and roll. But I don’t know what a book tour is, so what can visitors expect when they come by?</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> It’s kind of based on the space and the organizers there, how they want to run it. So they’ve all been a little different.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> The Athens one was really nice, there were dear friends, but there were also strangers who came, some lived in New York. They came to get the book as it was sold-out online. So strange how the world works.</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> I see it also as a way of meeting people, having conversations about the web, our hopes and dreams for it and such.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/internet-phonebook/4.webp" class="no-shadow large" alt="1913 pixel art" /></p> <h2 id="future-internet">Future Internet</h2> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Speaking of, are you optimistic or pessimistic overall about the internet’s future?</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> I’m very optimistic. I continuously meet more and more people doing very meaningful things on the web. When I started writing <a href="https://www.naiveweekly.com/">Naive Weekly</a>, which was, I guess was 6 or 7 years ago, I would just write all the same things that everyone else did, often critical stuff about big tech. And then at some point, I was like, okay, I need to stop just criticizing, I need to find out what I really like, and it started a long journey…</p> <p>Now I keep finding new cool <a href="https://www.are.na/kristoffer-tjalve/publish-for-the-web">digital publications</a>, or conferences and <a href="https://www.are.na/kristoffer-tjalve/poetic-web-calendar">events</a> related to this. So many people are doing such cool things outside the few big platforms. I find it really very exciting,</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> It also feels like maybe in the last two years, things have shifted a bit, especially with what happened to platforms like Twitter and the people looking for other spaces to be online. But in some ways these other online spaces are all the same, which is a little disheartening. But then I’ve seen a lot of people kind of go deeper and ground themselves in their websites, which has been super inspiring. They were just like, “I’m gonna spend some time just really building out my website, and adding all the features that I want, or that I’ve always dreamed about”. It seems like there’s more mental space for that now.</p> <p><span class="badge-name" title="Piri">P</span> Lately, there seems to be way more people who want to get off social media, but they also feel addicted to it. They’ve identified a problem in their lives, but to fix it they usually try things like setting a timer or something, which to me sounds a bit like “I have a problem with cocaine, so maybe I’ll limit it to 30 minutes a day”.</p> <p>But I think an actual solution could be … make a website. I’ve found that by just grounding myself more in this blog it kind of takes away my interest in social media. I still post on it, but I don’t really care about the feeds, because I have my own site and other people’s blogs to wander in.</p> <p><span class="badge-name e" title="Elliot">E</span> It’s definitely better to be addicted to your website than social media. And not everything has to be public too.</p> <p><span class="badge-name k" title="Kristoffer">K</span> Social media made it very easy for us to feel like we were staying connected with people, like they’d surface our friends when they have birthdays and stuff like that. But I think that we also forgot some of the important things about being a human, which is to also remember the people we care about and show up for them. Once you start to do your own website and you realize that, oh, people don’t just automatically come there, it’s kind of a good reminder that it takes a little bit of effort to go to other people’s sites and see what they’re doing – like checking in on them.</p> <p>Also websites have longevity. They’re a longer commitment than a social media post, and I think the pace is more healthy for humans. Like, tending to a website is more aligned with what it means to live a fulfilling life.</p> <blockquote> <p><del>At the time of this writing the Internet Phone Book is now <a href="https://livingweb.metalabel.com/internetphonebook?variantId=1/">back in stock</a>.</del></p> </blockquote> <blockquote> <p>The Internet Phonebook is currently sold out.</p> </blockquote> </description>

<title>How I Do Support and Community</title>

<description><p>I could see myself running a hotel. A little world where the architecture is otherworldly. And of course, the service is impeccable – because nothing matters more in any business than how customers are treated.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/support-community/dezeen-Agbaria-House-by-Ron-Fleisher-Architects-01.jpeg" class="" alt="Agbaria-House-by-Ron-Fleisher-Architects" /> <figcaption> <a href="http://www.ronfleisher.co.il/index.php/portfolios/agbaria-house">(source)</a> <span> I collected this photo as </span> <a href="https://pketh.org/decade-of-inspiration.html">inspiration</a> <span> ~14 years ago. Feels good to finally use it. </span> </figcaption> </figure> <p>But while it’s normal for one night in a hotel to cost ~$300, most people expect the price of a month of using pro-grade software – some of which cost millions of dollars to produce and maintain – to be closer to a cup of coffee. That’s because software is inherently a volume business. While I’m busy fixing something, the app is still being used by everyone else. And when I ship improvements, everyone benefits.</p> <p>Does it make me feel better when <a href="https://kinopio.club">Kinopio</a> is more reliable, lighter-weight, simpler, more powerful, and faster? Well, I’ve been happily doing this kind of tuning all month. And honestly, if I could afford to, I’d keep doing it forever.</p> <p>When it comes to volume, Kinopio is in this awkward puberty phase, where it doesn’t have enough paid users to be completely self-sustainable yet. But I also get bug reports, feature requests, student discount requests, and emails from really nice people telling me how much they love Kinopio, everyday.</p> <p>So as much as I enjoy improving code, I also know that my primary focus right now really should be on helping new people find Kinopio – and giving them a really inviting first-impression when they do.</p> <p>Switching my brain from coding-for-computers to writing-for-humans is a violent gesture. It takes a lot of force and focus to pull that rusty mental switch. So when writing mode finally does click on, I’d like to stay there for a while.</p> <p>But staying away from code gets tricky once daily support requests start rolling in.</p> <p>People have this perception of a founder or a CEO as someone who floats above the business, separated from the day-to-day so they can think high-level thoughts about high-level things. But especially if you’re building <a href="https://pketh.org/organic-software.html">organic software</a>, you’ll be sneaking peeks at the sky, while wading knee-deep through mud. The way I do it, it’s not a glamorous job.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/support-community/cross-stitch2.webp" class="no-shadow large" alt="1913 pixel art" /></p> <figure> <figcaption> <span> How influencers picture founder life. <br />(I </span> <a href="https://c.im/@pirijan/114893336118105336">hand-traced</a> <span> this from it’s </span> <a href="https://www.antiquepatternlibrary.org/html/warm/C-TT008-008.htm">1913 source</a> <span> using </span> <a href="https://c.im/@gingerbeardman@mastodon.gamedev.place/114778654824999491">Dottie</a>) </figcaption> </figure> <p>It’s all too easy to <a href="https://jakobgreenfeld.com/anti-optimization/">over-optimize</a> the support process. With the incantations provided by enterprise help-desk software, you could cast a barrier that only lets people contact you if they’ve searched the docs first. Or you could make them go through an annoying chat bot trained on those docs. And if someone is patient enough to clear the hurdles, you can effortlessly respond with a fully automated reply.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/support-community/s-l1600.webp" class="" alt="ff7 materia playing card" /> <figcaption> In Final Fantasy 7, the Barrier spell protects you from physical damage. In 2025, help-desk software protects you from your customers. </figcaption> </figure> <p>But treating support like a bean-counter really is missing the forest for the trees. One of the major benefits of buying products made by actual people is the expectation of being able easily to reach out to a real person if you need help.</p> <p>If you’re not beholden to short-sighted investors, and you’re willing to consider the unmeasurable, and act long-term, then support becomes an opportunity to grow a community which changes the relationship between creator and customer from 1:many to many:many.</p> <p> <video autoplay="" loop="" muted="" playsinline="" class=" no-shadow"> <source src="/images/2025/support-community/vid-before-after.mp4" /> </video> </p> <p>I don’t know how unique or interesting my approach is, but here are the tools and habits I use to stop support from becoming overwhelming, without compromising quality.</p> <h2 id="embrace-contact">Embrace Contact</h2> <p>The primary purpose of the help site isn’t to reduce support – it’s to have URLs that I can point people to for commonly asked questions. Some people prefer to use the site to look for answers on their own, while others prefer direct contact. Both are okay.</p> <p>I also have a <a href="https://kinopio.club/forum">support forum</a> and a <a href="https://kinopio.club/discord">community chat</a>. People will choose whatever you’re comfortable with, but there are good reasons to use one over the others.</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">Type</th> <th style="text-align: left">Pros</th> <th style="text-align: left">Cons</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Email</td> <td style="text-align: left">Everyone knows email, easy to start</td> <td style="text-align: left">Conversations are <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">1:1</code> and can’t be shared, hard to keep track of</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Chat (Discord)</td> <td style="text-align: left"><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">1:many</code> conversations encourage community and faster responses</td> <td style="text-align: left">Requires a Discord account, old posts are washed away by new ones</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Forum (Discourse)</td> <td style="text-align: left">Everything is public, Topics are easier to organize work around, Slower pace</td> <td style="text-align: left">Functional nature isn’t the best for casual community discussions</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p>Making contact easy is just the start of a long road. The shape of a welcoming community, like the shape of a bonsai tree, doesn’t just happen. Without regular care, bonsai fall over and die as their branches crowd each other out for light and nutrients.</p> <h2 id="constant-pruning">Constant Pruning</h2> <p>At least once a day, I check my inbox and read through all the latest updates on the forum and chat. When someone new joins the discord I write them a greeting. Something simple like,</p> <blockquote> <p><img src="/images/2025/support-community/avatar.png" width="24" height="24" class="no-shadow" style="vertical-align: middle; margin-right: 6px; border-radius: 10px; float: left" /> Hi @you, welcome to the Kinopio community chat</p> </blockquote> <p>I worry that they may think this is just a bot, which I could easily do. But I hand-type it anyways with slight variations to evoke the friendly feeling of being greeted when you walk into a shop or bar – and to show that I, and the other active people here, are listening and want to hear what you have to say.</p> <p>When you form a community you have a responsibility to actively keep it organized and healthy. When creators and moderators check out of communities, your biggest fans, feeling ignored or betrayed, may become your biggest haters. Passion cuts both ways.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/support-community/Gremlins-Movie-Promo-Book---Japanese-Edition.webp" class="" alt="gremlins movie poster" /></p> <figure> <figcaption> Abandoned places can go full gremlin overnight. </figcaption> </figure> <p>One of the most helpful things that I do to prevent discussions from getting overwhelming is proactively proposing that they be moved elsewhere when it makes sense. Here are some examples of how this plays out IRL:</p> <p> <video autoplay="" loop="" muted="" playsinline="" class=" no-shadow"> <source src="/images/2025/support-community/mediums.mp4" /> </video> </p> <ul> <li>Someone <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">emails</code> in a niche feature request that may not be used by anyone else. I would ask them to share their ideas and use-cases in <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">chat</code> to see if others are interested.</li> <li>Multiple people <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">email</code> me about the same bug, and others have reported it in <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">chat</code>. I would make a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">forum</code> topic for the issue, containing all the repro steps and other debug clues, then share the link with everyone so they can track it’s status.</li> <li>A non-critical bug is reported in <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">chat</code> that I don’t have the bandwidth to investigate right now (maybe because I’m writing a blog post). So I’ll ask them to post it in the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">forum</code> so I can get back to it once I have time.</li> <li>In <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">chat</code>, everyone’s sharing their hopes and hot-takes on a newly proposed feature. It’s getting spicy. I’d ask the OP to start a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">forum</code> thread and continue the discussion there so that we don’t lose anyone’s ideas later when I’m ready to work on it.</li> </ul> <p>Although changing communication types comes with friction, it’s also a filter. If the user doesn’t feel like making the effort, then it’s fair to assume that they didn’t feel that strongly about their request.</p> <p>If you had to choose between chat or a forum, you should choose a forum. But if you have a chat, you <em>need</em> a forum. The slower-pace and more async nature of a forum acts as a blow-off valve that lets the steam escape from heated conversations.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/support-community/sweet.webp" class="" alt="sweet" /></p> <h2 id="future-support">Future Support</h2> <p>As Kinopio grows, will I still be able to do support, and build community like this? The advantage of slower growth is that I have the time to <a href="https://99percentinvisible.org/article/least-resistance-desire-paths-can-lead-better-design">pave the desire paths</a> around the needs of real customers.</p> <p>I know that customer service isn’t the coolest topic. When costs are being cut it’s usually first on the chopping block. But when the world zigs, my passion zags – because why would you care about something made with the same thinking as everything else?</p> <p>If you have a problem with your 50 year old vintage Leica, Mercedes, or Tag Heuer, they’ll still service it. Even the most well-made things can break, but treating old customers as well as your new customers is what you do when you’re building a company to last beyond you.</p> <p>And of course, once it exists, you’re welcome at my hotel anytime.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/support-community/icon-street.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="gremlins movie poster" /> <figcaption> <a href="https://mercerhotel.com/">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> </description>

<title>Saying Bye to Glitch</title>

<description><p>Here lies Glitch, a place on the web you could go to write up a website or a node.js server that would be hosted and updated as you type. 🥀 RIP 2015 – 2025.</p> <p> <video autoplay="" loop="" muted="" playsinline="" class="large"> <source src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/darkmode.mp4" /> </video> </p> <figure> <figcaption> The Glitch editor in action. Built on top of ACE, and later Codemirror. </figcaption> </figure> <p>I co-created Glitch at Fog Creek Software. I laid out the original product vision for web development with real code that was as easy as editing a Google Doc, designed and built the editor, started the community site, and of course drew the logo and many of the illustrations.</p> <p>Now seems like the time to look back on just what a unique experience my 6~ years of working at Fog Creek Software and Glitch were, the many mistakes I and others made, and, of course, what I learned and took forward with me after</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/logo-bw2.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="black and white logo" /> <figcaption> While iterating on the logo, I knew I finally had something when the silhouette was unique and recognizable. </figcaption> </figure> <h2 id="working-at-fog-creek-software">Working at Fog Creek Software</h2> <p>Fog Creek Software is best known (by those over 40~) for being founded by <a href="https://www.joelonsoftware.com/">Joel Spolsky</a> to be the best <a href="https://www.joelonsoftware.com/2008/12/29/the-new-fog-creek-office/">place</a> for software developers to work, and for being the company that created Trello and StackOverflow.</p> <p>In 2014, I was recruited by Fog Creek because, despite being a huge fan of Joel’s blog and their bug tracker, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FogBugz">Fogbugz</a>, was frustrating to use at work. So, for fun and spite, I did some concept art for how the app might work if it was designed for programmers first and managers second, and posted it to dribbble (another thing only remembered by the 40+). A senior manager saw it and a couple weeks later I was staying at the Ritz in Manhattan to meet the team.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/dribbble1.webp" class="" alt="fogbugz for mac concept window" /> <img src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/dribbble2.webp" class="" alt="fogbugz for mac concept quick entry panel" /> <figcaption> Definitely not what FogBugz looked like. I did these like 20~ years ago, so they're a bit cringe to me. </figcaption> </figure> <p>I was hired as a <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">member of technical staff</code>. We all were I think. I worked on Fogbugz a little bit with some of the smartest programmers I’ve ever met; and because we shared the office with Trello, some of the the coolest programmers I’ve ever met too.</p> <p>But I was primarily hired to help come up with the next big new successful product for the company, which eventually became Glitch.</p> <blockquote> <p> <img src="https://pketh.org/images/2019/Screen\_Shot\_2016-01-28\_at\_3-33a4a830-51d3-466e-b67b-2ac85993ab6f.04.38\_PM.png" width="" class="" /> <br /> In 2019, I wrote about how Glitch started and <a href="https://pketh.org/the-first-four-years-of-glitch.html">how it evolved over the first four years</a>. It was designed to be beautiful and functional, in its own unique way. </p> </blockquote> <h2 id="promising-early-days">Promising Early Days</h2> <p>I always thought of Glitch as web development without the <br /><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$ git how-do-I-stage-and-commit-updates-again?</code>, and the <br /><code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">$ heroku push-how-come-this-only-works-on-my-own-computer-wtf</code> tango 💃. Of course, these are both powerful and necessary tools for experienced programmers and large projects, but what if these were optional instead of required?</p> <p>The real innovation of Glitch was in its belief in a new “middle-market” of developers that wanted the creative power and copy-paste-ability that you only get with real coding – but with the ease-of-use of the normal consumer software they were used to.</p> <blockquote> <p>Why does creating a new web app have to be harder than creating a new google doc? To the user, they’re both just boxes you type into.</p> </blockquote> <p>When Glitch launched it was really well received, with write-ups in The Verge, TechCrunch, and the other big publications of the day.</p> <p>People used Glitch in schools, to build games and apps to share with their friends, and even to learn new skills in jail. According to the Fog Creek veterans who were working at Trello and SO, the electricity in the air was just like when those products launched.</p> <p>Some experienced programmers critiqued the editor for being too simple for serious work. This was really encouraging because at the time we wanted to follow in the footsteps of Ruby on Rails: initially used by hobbyists for fun → dismissed as a toy by serious coders → right up until it becomes the status quo enterprise choice.</p> <p>We had the toy-like, easy-to-use, and approachable first part down. But to walk the rest, we discussed and wrote specs so that one day you could do things like:</p> <ul> <li>Safely work on collaborative projects in your own branch.</li> <li>Backup and control deploys by syncing projects with Github repos.</li> <li>Edit how you want by syncing projects to your local text editor or filesystem.</li> </ul> <p>Alas, these were not to be. Nothing was ever directly said, but after leadership cemented it’s focus on learning and hobbyists, there was never the time or the resources to seriously iterate on the hard parts of that vision.</p> <p> <video autoplay="" loop="" muted="" playsinline="" class="large"> <source src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/find-replace.mp4" /> </video> </p> <figure> <figcaption> Find and Replace was one of the last features that I did. Looking at this now, the density of search results could’ve been tightened up, but v1 releases were all I had time for. For better and for worse, I’ve gotten really used to designing and building UI really quickly and iteratively. </figcaption> </figure> <h2 id="easy-and-friendly-are-the-trees-not-the-forest">Easy and Friendly Are the Trees, Not the Forest</h2> <p>Coincidentally, this was right around the time Glitch received $30m in VC funding. My understanding is that the pitch was basically “Youtube but for web apps”. The new vision was that web apps were going to be a new kind of entertainment medium that would be virally shared. And just like Youtube, there are way more watchers than creators, so discovery and community features would be the new core of the experience.</p> <p>Despite my protests, the size of the team was doubled every year. The new employees were fans of the new vision and more excited about Glitch as a learning and beginner platform. There were now two frontend teams: the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Community</code> team which was a team of 10-20. And the <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">Editor</code> team – which was just me.</p> <p>The editor had a lot of technical debt that made it intimidating to work on, but it was frustrating that new development projects were almost always easier greenfield tasks like redesigning the 404 page, or adding the ability to save apps to lists – there didn’t seem to be much interest from leadership in improving the developer experience. (After I left there was a full rewrite of the editor, but I found it … not good).</p> <p>Middle-market product positioning is hard. You need power users creating inspiring projects and recommending it, so the tooling can’t be half assed. You need to be able to engage new users with a fun experience and create social or collaborative loops that keep them coming back. This balance takes a clear vision, a lot of time – and a lot of luck – to nail.</p> <p>I do believe that Glitch could have benefited from VC funding <em>after</em> we found that balance – but taking that much money before even having paid customers still feels like a mistake.</p> <p> <video autoplay="" loop="" muted="" playsinline="" class=""> <source src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/loading.mp4" /> </video> </p> <figure> <figcaption> Look familiar? The project loading screen was the predecessor of <a href="https://kinopio.club">Kinopio’s</a> paint select. </figcaption> </figure> <h2 id="learning-from-failure">Learning from Failure</h2> <p>If you’re really deep in the Fog Creek lore, you might also know that the company had a long history of releasing products that were ahead of their time, but not positioned quite right, or stewarded in the wrong direction:</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">Released</th> <th style="text-align: left">Product</th> <th style="text-align: left">Description</th> <th style="text-align: left">Released Before</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2000</td> <td style="text-align: left">Fogbugz</td> <td style="text-align: left">Bug Tracker</td> <td style="text-align: left">Jira, 2002</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2001</td> <td style="text-align: left">CityDesk</td> <td style="text-align: left">Website CMS</td> <td style="text-align: left">Wordpress, 2003</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2005</td> <td style="text-align: left">Copilot</td> <td style="text-align: left">Screen-sharing and KVM</td> <td style="text-align: left">Zoom, 2013</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">2010</td> <td style="text-align: left">Kiln</td> <td style="text-align: left">Git hosting</td> <td style="text-align: left">Github, 2008</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p>I put the release year first to emphasize how pioneering most of these were. It’s hard to know what to build when you’re early.</p> <p>I still believe there’s a market for easy and fun web development and hosting, but a product like this needs power-users and enthusiasts willing to pay for it. To build any kind of prosumer software, you do have to be an optimist and believe that enough of the world still cares about quality and craft.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/bye-glitch/delete-team.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="delete team UI" /> <figcaption> I’ve always loved the little illustrations that <a href="https://x.com/GraphixArtiste">Walter</a> did, which were sprinkled throughout the app. </figcaption> </figure> <p>Ultimately I’ve learned a lot more from my failures, of which there are many, than my successes. I learned that I value clear and unambiguous communication above flowery narratives that change on a whim. And I learned that what I find admirable in a leader is someone who can simply say what they’ll do, and do what they say. And if it turns out they can’t, that’s okay because they’ll tell you why.</p> <p>It’s been 6 years since I worked on Glitch and I don’t really have any strong feelings about it anymore. I just feel really grateful to everyone who created something cool with it over the years.</p> <blockquote> <p>Special thanks to Aneesha for helping edit this</p> </blockquote> </description>

<title>The Lo-Fi Art and Human Tools Era</title>

<description><p>At the dawn of the industry, we needed big companies to deliver quality software. You’d buy version 1.0 because the way it worked was revolutionary, and it let you do amazing new things. You’d buy version 2.0 because it added helpful new features. Apple, Adobe, Microsoft, etc. became household names because software was hard to make, and even harder to box up and get on store shelves.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/human-tools/mac-ps.jpeg" class="large" alt="classic mac with photoshop" /></p> <p>But by the time version 10~ or so releases, the product includes basically every feature possible. It’s all things to all people.</p> <p>So how do you make version 11 compelling? The answer turned out to be <em>Lol who cares now</em>. Once you have monopoly market share, the product (and the people that made it) are largely irrelevant. What now keeps the almighty profit line going up and to the right 📈 is sales and marketing – and predictably the relationship between user and tool metamorphoses from symbiotic to parasitic.</p> <p>The old monopolies aren’t going anywhere. But a new era of tools designed for a different fate are sprouting up out of old, analogue ideas like creativity, purposefulness, human-made craftsmanship.</p> <blockquote class="row"> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/pizza.gif" class="no-shadow pizza" width="100" alt="pizza gif" /> <p> Domino’s – the Microsoft of pizza – also isn’t going anywhere. But for those who know, and those who care, there’s always a way better neighborhood spot. </p> </blockquote> <h2 id="chasing-good-friction">Chasing Good Friction</h2> <p>I spend a lot of time thinking about tools for thinking and creativity, and how they’ve changed. If you’re not used to drawing or writing – and even if you are – the scariest thing in the world is a blank page.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/blank-pages.webp" class="" alt="paper notebook open to blank page" /> <figcaption> “Eeep” </figcaption> </figure> <p>The first sentence, or the first brush stroke, is a high anxiety situation. Full of potential, full of unknown pain.</p> <p>So to help with the blank slate problem, software tools added Templates. <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">File → New</code> turned into <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">File → New → Do You Want to Make a Portfolio? Or a Résumé?</code>. This more recently turned into <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">File → New → Ask AI to Do It</code>. These features help some people, but also create new problems.</p> <p>These tools can generate almost effortless outputs. They unburden us from the abrasion of creativity and their generic-by-design results keep us cocooned from the risks of standing out. No one wants to make ‘ugly’ art, or a ‘bad’ website. But struggling and making mistakes is an important part of developing skills and – increasingly important – finding your own voice.</p> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/omw.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="‘omw to get a lil treat’ badge" /> <figcaption> <a href="https://tombinghamillustration.com/shop/treatcap">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> <p>We don’t talk enough about the many-years-long rite of passage that every creator goes through.</p> <p>Everyone can draw or write, but only the smallest sliver will decide that they want to earnestly make things to share with other people.</p> <p>At whatever age it grips you though, the urge to create pulls you into a world of frustration, where your skills don’t yet match your taste and everything you try feels not good enough. Unfortunately, the first step to being good at something is to suck at it.</p> <p>But the expedition is especially dispiriting when your tools constantly wink at you to generate ‘perfect’ results instantly.</p> <p>Why did I spend two+ painful weeks writing a blog post?</p> <h2 id="for-the-journey">For the Journey</h2> <p>I don’t write very often, so I’m not sure if my pace is glacial or merely tortoise-like. Then again Tolkien took <a href="https://middle-earth.xenite.org/how-long-did-it-take-j-r-r-tolkien-to-write-the-lord-of-the-rings/">12+ years</a> to write The Lord of the Rings.</p> <p>Speaking for myself, even after I plan things out, the shape of an essay (sometimes even my whole argument) changes as I write.</p> <p>I type with two <a href="https://ia.net/writer">IA Writer</a> text editor windows open. One for the post, and one where I throw rejected or tangential paragraphs and ideas that don’t quite fit. So far, each file contains ~2000 words… implying that I throw away ~half of what I type.</p> <p><img src="/images/2025/human-tools/david-lynch-teaches-typing-game.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="david lynch teaches typing game" /></p> <figure> <figcaption> <a href="https://rhinostew.itch.io/david-lynch-teaches-typing">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> <p>From a short-term-results point of view, this process may seem inefficient, painful, and somewhat savage. I wouldn’t really disagree – but, like working out, or eating well, the sweat and tears are worth it in the long-term because I solidify my ideas with every new post I write.</p> <p>Even after all that work, it’s never as good as I want to be – especially considering the effort. I’m still always a little bit embarrassed to share my work, because it’s never ‘perfect’.</p> <p>But it’s better than perfect, it’s <strong>imperfect</strong>.</p> <p>Struggling is an important part of developing skills and finding your own voice, and having an audience that’s watched your work evolve over years is a slept on privilege.</p> <h2 id="the-human-tools-era">The Human-Tools Era</h2> <p>When you’re excited about a new hammer, everything looks like a nail. In the case of LLMs, just a year or two ago investors couldn’t catapult money fast enough at anyone incanting the magic letters <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">A</code> and <code class="language-plaintext highlighter-rouge">I</code>. Now that the dust is settling, I suspect we’ll look back at AI as something that was good(<a href="https://www.crikey.com.au/2024/09/03/ai-worse-summarising-information-humans-government-trial/">ish</a>) at summarizing text and image recognition – but was used to make everything in the world harder to understand, less fun, and worse.</p> <p>More so now than ever before, making something yourself that you fully understand is deeply empowering. As the world gets noisier, glossier, and more fake, the more valuable the human-touch becomes in both the real world and on the real web.</p> <p>LLMs are the new normal. “Easy to use” became “let me tell you what to write and how to draw”, “here’s what you’ll watch next”, and “here’s what you believe”.</p> <p>But there is always be a market for understandable, engaging, and fun-to-use tools.</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">What Most People Use</th> <th style="text-align: left">What Enthusiasts Use</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Phone camera</td> <td style="text-align: left">Dedicated camera</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Whatever pen is nearby</td> <td style="text-align: left">Fountain pen</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">The keyboard that came with your computer</td> <td style="text-align: left">Mechanical Keyboard</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Coors Light™</td> <td style="text-align: left">Craft brew</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Smart watch</td> <td style="text-align: left">Non-smart watch</td> </tr> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Fast fashion</td> <td style="text-align: left">hand-made knitwear</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <figure> <img src="/images/2025/human-tools/field-quilt.webp" class="no-shadow" alt="field quilt" /> <figcaption> <a href="https://www.are.na/block/9117165">(source)</a> </figcaption> </figure> <p>In your heart, you probably can’t be an enthusiast for everything. I like cool cameras, I don’t care about wine (TBH I’ll happily drink Coors Light™), and obviously I care about good software. Let me add a row:</p> <table> <thead> <tr> <th style="text-align: left">What Most People Use</th> <th style="text-align: left">What Enthusiasts Use</th> </tr> </thead> <tbody> <tr> <td style="text-align: left">Software that generates quick results for you</td> <td style="text-align: left">Software that gives you the time, space, and encouragement to do your best work</td> </tr> </tbody> </table> <p>When it comes to thinking through problems, working through projects, or expressing yourself, special tools aren’t a necessity – but they can make the journey a lot more fun.</p> <p>Doing things a little differently makes the results more imperfect and personal. Being authentically you is what makes the journey worthwhile.</p> <blockquote> <p>Special thanks to Aneesha and <a href="https://bentsai.org/">Ben</a> for helping edit this.</p> </blockquote> </description>
